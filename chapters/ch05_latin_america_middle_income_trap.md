# Chapter 5: Latin America and the Middle-Income Trap

## Introduction: The Convergence That Never Arrived

In 1960, Brazil's GDP per capita was roughly 14 percent of the United States' (in PPP terms), peaking at 18 percent in 1980 before sliding back. Six decades later, after import substitution, debt crises, structural adjustment, trade liberalization, commodity booms, and multiple democratic transitions, it stood at 15 percent. Mexico, which shares a 3,145-kilometer border with the world's largest consumer market and has been a party to a preferential trade agreement with it since 1994, has seen its ratio to US income (PPP) *decline* from approximately 27 percent in 1981 to roughly 22 percent in 2024. Argentina, once among the ten richest countries in the world, has spent the last century demonstrating that middle-income status can be permanent — not a waystation on the path to prosperity, but an equilibrium from which escape requires more than factor accumulation and trade liberalization.

This is the middle-income trap: the empirical regularity that countries which reach per-capita incomes between $3,000 and $12,000 (in constant 2015 dollars) frequently stagnate for decades, unable to sustain the growth that lifted them from poverty. The trap is not uniquely Latin American — Malaysia, Thailand, and Turkey show similar patterns — but Latin America has the most members and the longest duration. Of the twenty most populous Latin American and Caribbean countries, only Chile, Uruguay, and Panama have graduated to high-income status in the past half century (which crossed the World Bank's high-income threshold in 2013 and has remained above it since, though the 2014–2016 commodity price slump and exchange rate depreciation narrowed the margin considerably). The rest are stuck.

The standard explanations for the trap — macroeconomic instability, commodity dependence, insufficient trade openness, underinvestment in human capital — are correct at the surface but insufficient at the structural level. Every one of these conditions has been addressed, at least temporarily, in at least some LAC countries over the past three decades. Chile stabilized its macroeconomy and diversified into services and agriculture. Mexico opened its economy through NAFTA/USMCA and attracted massive foreign direct investment. Brazil invested heavily in education (FUNDEF, FUNDEB, Bolsa Família's conditionality requirements) and in technology (Embrapa's agricultural research, Embraer's aerospace engineering). Colombia liberalized trade and attracted FDI in hydrocarbons and mining. Yet none — except perhaps Chile — has converged toward rich-country income levels. Something deeper than macro policy is at work.

This chapter argues that the middle-income trap in Latin America is not primarily a trade-access problem or a capital-scarcity problem. It is an institutional-fragmentation problem. Sub-national variation in regulatory quality, formalization capacity, and fiscal governance explains why proximity to the world's largest consumer market has not triggered convergence: regions with thicker institutional endowments upgrade into higher-value-added production, while institutionally thin regions cycle between commodity booms and premature deindustrialization. The spatial tools of Chapter 3-A and the institutional framework of Chapter 2 converge here on a specific empirical claim: the unit of analysis for the middle-income trap should be the region, not the country, because the institutional variation that matters most is sub-national.

The chapter proceeds in five sections. Section 5.1 examines premature deindustrialization — the loss of manufacturing before income reaches levels at which services can absorb the displaced workforce productively. Section 5.2 analyzes the resource curse through the lens of sub-national institutional quality. Section 5.3 treats informality as an institutional equilibrium rather than a residual category. Section 5.4 asks whether the "green commodity frontiers" — lithium, hydrogen, critical minerals — offer an escape from the resource-curse pattern or a repetition of it. Section 5.5 connects the analysis to Lab 1, extending the Americas SAR framework to test whether institutional quality mediates spatial spillovers from US demand to Latin American growth.

## 5.1 Premature Deindustrialization: The Spatial Anatomy of Structural Regression

### What Premature Deindustrialization Is — and Is Not

Before examining the data, it is important to clarify the concept. Deindustrialization — the decline of manufacturing's share of GDP and employment — is a normal feature of economic development. Every rich country has deindustrialized: manufacturing's share of US GDP fell from 28 percent in 1953 to 11 percent in 2024; Germany's fell from 40 percent to 20 percent; Japan's from 35 percent to 20 percent. This decline reflects the success of manufacturing: productivity growth in manufacturing outpaces services, so fewer workers are needed to produce the same (or greater) output. As incomes rise, consumer preferences shift toward services (healthcare, education, entertainment, travel) that are inherently labor-intensive and difficult to automate.

The "premature" in premature deindustrialization refers not to the fact of deindustrialization but to its timing. Rich countries deindustrialized after they became rich — after manufacturing productivity had risen enough to generate high wages and after the services sector had developed enough to absorb displaced workers productively. Latin American (and African) countries are deindustrializing *before* they are rich, with manufacturing contracting at income levels where the services sector is too underdeveloped and too informal to provide a productive alternative.

### The Rodrik Hypothesis

Dani Rodrik's (2016) paper on premature deindustrialization documented a striking pattern: developing countries since 1990 have been reaching their peak manufacturing share of GDP at much lower income levels than the historical norm. The United Kingdom's manufacturing share peaked at roughly 33 percent of GDP when per-capita income (in constant dollars) exceeded $14,000. South Korea's peaked at 28 percent at approximately $12,000. Brazil's peaked at 21 percent at just $5,500 — and has been declining ever since. Mexico's trajectory is similar, though the NAFTA-era maquiladora boom created a temporary reprieve in export-oriented manufacturing that masked the decline in domestically oriented production.

Rodrik argued that the causes were global: trade liberalization exposed developing-country manufacturers to competition from East Asian and Chinese producers with superior cost structures and deeper supply chain integration; automation reduced the labor content of manufacturing globally, shrinking the employment potential of industrialization; and the skill-biased technological change that enriched knowledge workers in rich countries flowed imperfectly to middle-income countries, where education systems could not keep pace.

These explanations are correct at the global level but insufficient at the sub-national level. Within Latin American countries, the timing, depth, and consequences of deindustrialization vary dramatically across regions — and the variation is systematically correlated with institutional quality.

The timing of Brazil's manufacturing collapse illustrates how external demand shocks interact with these structural forces. The China-driven commodity super-cycle (2003--2013) sent iron ore, soybean, and petroleum prices to historic highs, and the resulting export revenues generated classic Dutch Disease dynamics: the Brazilian real appreciated roughly 40 percent in real effective terms between 2003 and 2011, making manufactured exports progressively uncompetitive in precisely the sectors -- textiles, footwear, machinery -- where Brazil had built ISI-era capacity (Bresser-Pereira 2008). Manufacturing's share of Brazilian GDP fell from 18 percent in 2002 to 11 percent by 2014, with the steepest annual declines coinciding with the years of peak commodity revenue. The commodity boom did not cause premature deindustrialization -- Rodrik's structural forces were already at work -- but it dramatically compressed the timeline, depriving firms and workers of the adjustment period that a more gradual transition might have allowed. The spatial consequence was that regions dependent on commodity extraction (Minas Gerais iron ore, Mato Grosso soybeans) boomed while manufacturing regions lost ground, widening the sub-national divergence that the rest of this section documents.

### Sub-National Patterns

Consider Brazil. Between 1985 and 2020, industry's share of national GDP (including manufacturing, mining, and construction) fell from 36 percent to 18 percent, with manufacturing narrowly defined declining from approximately 26 percent to 11 percent. But this national average conceals enormous regional variation. São Paulo state — historically the industrial heartland — saw manufacturing decline from 45 percent of state GDP to 17 percent, a severe contraction that nonetheless left a sophisticated industrial base in aerospace (Embraer), automotive (GM, VW, Toyota), and food processing. The state's formal labor market, university system (USP, Unicamp, ITA), and regulatory infrastructure provided a platform for transition into high-value services — technology, finance, logistics — that partially compensated for the manufacturing loss.

By contrast, the Northeastern states (Bahia, Pernambuco, Ceará) experienced deindustrialization from a much lower base — manufacturing never exceeded 15 percent of GDP in these states — and the transition was into informality, not into productive services. Workers who lost manufacturing jobs (or never had them) entered the informal economy: street vending, domestic service, unlicensed transport, and casual construction. These activities are low-productivity, poorly compensated, and offer no social insurance or skills accumulation. The regional income gap between São Paulo and the Northeast is wider today than it was in 1990.

Mexico shows a parallel pattern. The northern border states (Nuevo León, Chihuahua, Baja California) maintained or increased manufacturing employment through NAFTA-era maquiladora expansion, but the central and southern states (Puebla, Oaxaca, Chiapas) deindustrialized without any compensating structural transformation. Mexico City partially transitioned into services (finance, telecommunications, professional services), but the secondary cities of central Mexico — Toluca, Querétaro, San Luis Potosí — present a mixed picture: some successfully attracted automotive and aerospace investment, while others stagnated.

### The Institutional Explanation

What distinguishes regions that deindustrialize into productive services from those that deindustrialize into informality? The evidence points to three institutional factors:

**Formalization capacity.** Regions with functioning tax administrations, enforceable contracts, and accessible regulatory frameworks enable firms to operate formally — which in turn allows them to access credit, participate in supply chains, and invest in training. Regions where formalization costs are prohibitive (in time, money, or corruption payments) push firms into informality, where they remain small, unproductive, and disconnected from value chains.

**Educational infrastructure.** The transition from manufacturing to productive services requires a workforce with post-secondary skills: accounting, engineering, information technology, healthcare. Regions with universities and vocational training systems can supply this workforce; regions without them cannot. Brazil's "Science Without Borders" program (2011–2015) sent 100,000 students abroad for advanced training — but almost all returned to São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and Minas Gerais, where the jobs requiring their skills existed. The Northeast received almost no return flow.

**Fiscal autonomy.** Regions that can raise their own revenue — through property taxes, local business taxes, or user fees — can invest in the public goods (infrastructure, education, public safety) that attract private investment. Regions that depend entirely on central government transfers have limited fiscal agency and limited ability to respond to local economic conditions. Brazil's fiscal federalism gives states and municipalities significant taxing authority, but the capacity to exercise it varies enormously: São Paulo's municipal tax administration is among the most sophisticated in the developing world, while many Northeastern municipalities lack the administrative capacity to maintain a property register.

These three factors — formalization, education, and fiscal autonomy — constitute the "institutional thickness" that Chapter 2 argued conditions regional economic trajectories. They are not fixed endowments; they evolve over decades through investment, reform, and institutional learning. But they are sufficiently persistent to generate the path dependence that Chapter 2 described: regions that built institutional capacity during the import-substitution era (1950s–1970s) had a platform from which to navigate the trade liberalization era (1990s–2000s), while regions that did not were left exposed.

The persistence of these institutional deficiencies is not simply a matter of capacity gaps waiting to be filled; it reflects a political economy equilibrium in which powerful actors benefit from the status quo. In Mexico, the competition-limiting regulations that Levy (2008) identified as a "tax on formality" are sustained not by bureaucratic inertia alone but by incumbent firms that profit from restricted entry -- telecom monopolies, cement cartels, and pharmaceutical distributors whose rents depend on regulatory barriers (Guerrero, López-Calva, and Walton 2009). In Brazil, the labyrinthine tax code that penalizes formal-sector firms simultaneously generates rents for the accounting, legal, and consultancy industries that navigate it on behalf of clients. The middle-income trap, in this framing, is not merely an institutional-capacity problem but a distributional-politics problem: the "middle-income" equilibrium produces winners -- landed elites, protected industrialists, regulatory intermediaries -- who have both the means and the incentive to block the reforms that would enable broader upgrading. Escaping the trap requires not just building institutional capacity but overcoming the political coalitions that benefit from its absence.

### The ISI Legacy as Institutional Capital

The import substitution industrialization (ISI) period (roughly 1940–1980) is typically evaluated in macroeconomic terms: it produced growth but also inefficiency, inflation, and unsustainable trade balances. From a regional perspective, however, ISI had an underappreciated spatial legacy: it built institutional capacity in the regions where state-sponsored industrialization was concentrated.

São Paulo's industrial suburbs (ABC Paulista — Santo André, São Bernardo, São Caetano) were created by ISI-era auto industry investments. The factories are mostly gone, but the institutions they generated — technical schools, labor unions, regulatory agencies, supplier networks — persisted and provided the human-capital base for the region's transition into technology and advanced services. Monterrey's industrial ecosystem similarly traces to ISI-era steel and cement production (Grupo ALFA, CEMEX, Vitro). The factories modernized; the institutional ecosystem they built endures.

Regions that were bypassed by ISI — the Brazilian Northeast, southern Mexico, most of Central America — did not accumulate this institutional capital. When trade liberalization arrived in the 1990s, they faced global competition without the workforce skills, regulatory infrastructure, or supplier networks that ISI had inadvertently built in the favored regions. The result was not convergence but divergence: the already-strong regions adapted and grew; the institutionally thin regions were exposed to competition they could not survive.

### Premature Deindustrialization and the Services Transition

The conventional growth-economics story assumes that countries (and regions) transition smoothly from agriculture to manufacturing to services, with each stage generating productivity gains that sustain income growth. Latin America's premature deindustrialization disrupts this narrative because the services sector that absorbs deindustrialized workers in LAC is not the high-productivity services sector of the rich-country model. It is overwhelmingly low-productivity informal services: retail, personal services, transport, and food preparation.

Diao, McMillan, and Rodrik (2019) quantified this for Africa (see Chapter 13) but the LAC patterns are similar. In Brazil, the reallocation of labor from manufacturing to services between 1995 and 2019 was *growth-reducing* on net: workers moved from higher-productivity manufacturing to lower-productivity services, and the structural change component of growth was negative. The only exceptions were regions where the services transition was into formal, knowledge-intensive activities — São Paulo (financial services, IT), Santiago (financial services, mining engineering), Bogotá (business services, digital platforms).

The spatial consequence is a two-speed economy within each country. Regions that successfully transition into high-productivity services grow rapidly and converge toward rich-country income levels. Regions that deindustrialize into informality stagnate or regress. The income gap between the two types of region — already large in 1990 — has widened over three decades of liberalization, creating a sub-national version of the middle-income trap: regions trapped at low income within countries trapped at middle income.

### The Productive Services Frontier: BPO, Digital Labor, and Tourism

The "services = low productivity" narrative, while statistically dominant, obscures a significant counter-trend. A growing number of Latin American cities are generating productive service-sector agglomeration through digital labor platforms, BPO, fintech, and tourism — tradable services that integrate with global markets without passing through the manufacturing stage.

**Digital labor and nearshore BPO.** Guadalajara, Monterrey, Medellín, Buenos Aires, and Montevideo have emerged as hubs for software development, IT outsourcing, and digital professional services targeted at US and European clients. These cities benefit from three structural advantages: time-zone alignment with the US market (a critical friction for synchronous work that India and the Philippines cannot match), a growing supply of university-educated bilingual workers, and labor costs 30–50 percent below comparable US cities. Platform-mediated digital labor — through Upwork, Freelancer, Toptal, and regional platforms like Workana and Torre — further connects Latin American freelancers to global clients, creating a form of "telemigration" (Baldwin 2019) that operates through Mode 1 (cross-border delivery).

The Grossman and Rossi-Hansberg (2008) framework helps predict which tasks will flow through this channel: routine cognitive tasks (data entry, basic programming, accounting) offshore most easily, while tasks requiring contextual knowledge, client-facing trust, and creative judgment resist offshoring. The upgrading trajectory — from routine BPO to higher-value knowledge process outsourcing (KPO) — maps onto the "smile curve" of services value chains, with low margins in routine processing and high margins in analytics, design, and strategy. Medellín's trajectory is instructive: the city's tech sector began with call centers in the early 2000s, moved into software testing and maintenance, and by 2024 hosts product development offices for companies including Rappi, Globant, and MercadoLibre. The institutional preconditions for this upgrading — bilingual education, reliable digital infrastructure, regulatory stability, intellectual property protection — are precisely the dimensions of institutional thickness that this chapter argues distinguish upgrading regions from stagnating ones.

**Tourism as Mode 2 services trade.** Tourism is the Latin American services sector with the deepest empirical evidence for local development effects. Faber and Gaubert (2019), using a rigorous spatial identification strategy on Mexico's coastline, demonstrate that tourism development caused significant increases in local income, employment, and structural transformation in Mexican coastal municipalities — with spillovers to nearby non-tourist locations. Tourism is analytically distinctive because it is Mode 2 services trade: the consumer moves to the service, rather than the service moving to the consumer. This makes it the mode of services trade where BOP statistics are most reliable (international tourist expenditure shows up in travel accounts), providing a useful measurement contrast with the Mode 1 and Mode 3 channels that are chronically undercounted.

**Medical tourism** inverts the offshoring logic in a different way: rather than the service migrating to the low-cost location, the consumer migrates to the provider. Mexico, Costa Rica, and Colombia have developed significant medical tourism clusters — dental, cosmetic, and orthopedic surgery at 30–70 percent below US prices — that represent a form of spatial arbitrage in healthcare services (Connell 2013). The institutional requirements are stringent: internationally accredited hospitals, bilingual medical staff, liability insurance regimes that international patients trust, and visa/travel infrastructure that minimizes non-medical friction. Cities that meet these requirements (Tijuana for dental, San José for orthopedic, Bogotá for cosmetic) capture rents from the institutional gap between US healthcare costs and LAC healthcare delivery costs.

The overarching point is that Latin America's services transition is not uniformly negative. The institutional question posed at the beginning of this chapter — what distinguishes regions that deindustrialize into productive services from those that deindustrialize into informality? — applies to the services frontier with full force. The same institutional variables that condition manufacturing-to-services transitions (regulatory quality, formalization capacity, fiscal governance) also determine which regions capture the BPO, digital labor, and tourism opportunities. The services frontier does not bypass institutional constraints — it expresses them.

## 5.2 The Resource Curse at the Sub-National Level

### Beyond Dutch Disease

The traditional resource-curse narrative emphasizes Dutch Disease: a resource boom appreciates the real exchange rate, making manufactured exports uncompetitive, and the manufacturing sector contracts. Sachs and Warner (1995) documented the cross-country correlation between resource dependence and slower growth; Frankel (2010) surveyed the channels through which resource abundance undermines institutional quality.

But the cross-country evidence tells an incomplete story. Within resource-rich countries, the effects of the resource boom are spatially uneven — and the spatial pattern depends on institutions. Chile, Peru, and Colombia all have significant mining sectors. Chile's copper mining is concentrated in the Atacama and Antofagasta regions; Peru's mining spans the Andean departments of Cajamarca, Arequipa, and Junín; Colombia's mining is concentrated in La Guajira (coal), Cesar (coal), and Antioquia (gold).

The institutional responses to resource revenue differ dramatically. Chile's Fiscal Responsibility Law (2006) channels copper revenue into a sovereign wealth fund (the Fondo de Estabilización Económica y Social), smoothing the fiscal impact of commodity price cycles and preventing the revenue from generating Dutch Disease. This is a national institution, but its effects are spatially differentiated: the mining regions receive investment from CORFO-funded cluster development programs (solar energy in Atacama, salmon farming in Los Lagos), while Santiago receives the downstream financial and professional-services activity that resource management generates.

Peru's Canon Minero directs 50 percent of mining income tax to the regions where the minerals are extracted. In principle, this should build local institutional capacity by providing fiscal resources for infrastructure, education, and health. In practice, the Canon resources have been poorly absorbed: many mining regions lack the administrative capacity to spend the funds, and those that can spend them often invest in low-return prestige projects (stadiums, ceremonial plazas) rather than productive infrastructure. Cajamarca, Peru's most mineral-rich department, has among the highest poverty rates in the country — a vivid illustration that resource revenue without institutional capacity produces rent capture, not development.

Colombia occupies a middle position. The country's complex fiscal transfer system directs royalties to producing regions and to a general fund for the poorest regions. The 2012 royalty reform (Sistema General de Regalías) attempted to redistribute mining revenue more broadly, creating a Science, Technology, and Innovation Fund and regional investment funds that allocated revenue based on poverty and population rather than production alone. The reform was progressive in intent but faced implementation challenges: many recipient departments lacked the project-design and procurement capacity to spend the funds, reproducing the Cajamarca problem at a national scale.

The spatial result is a patchwork: Medellín (Antioquia) has used mining-adjacent revenue — combined with decades of institutional investment in urban planning, public transit, and education — to build a globally admired urban transformation (Metrocable, public libraries, technology parks). Medellín's "social urbanism" model is now exported as a best practice to cities across the developing world. But Medellín's success is rooted in institutional capacity that predates the resource revenue: the Empresas Públicas de Medellín (EPM), the municipally owned utility company, has been one of Colombia's best-managed institutions since the 1950s. The resource revenue amplified existing institutional capacity; it did not create it.

By contrast, La Guajira — where a single coal mine (Cerrejón) generates most of the department's revenue — remains one of Colombia's poorest and least institutionally developed departments. The Wayúu indigenous population faces water scarcity, malnutrition, and health outcomes comparable to the poorest countries in the hemisphere, despite living adjacent to one of the world's largest open-pit coal mines. The mine generates over $1 billion in annual revenue, but the extractive enclave operates in near-complete isolation from the surrounding economy: workers are brought in from other departments, equipment is imported, and profits flow to multinational shareholders (BHP, Anglo American, Glencore) and national coffers. The spatial pattern — a modern industrial island surrounded by pre-industrial poverty — is the resource curse made visible from satellite imagery.

### Spatial Econometric Implications

The sub-national resource curse has a specific spatial signature that the tools of Chapter 3-A can detect. If resource booms strengthen institutions in some regions (through fiscal revenue, investment in education, regulatory capacity) and weaken them in others (through rent capture, corruption, migration of skilled workers to the boom zone), then the spatial autocorrelation of institutional quality should increase during commodity booms. This is testable: compute Moran's $I$ on a sub-national institutional quality index (formal employment share, fiscal capacity) at different points in the commodity cycle and check whether clustering increases during booms.

### The Institutional Channel: Why Resource Revenue Doesn't Build Institutions

The standard resource-curse story emphasizes macroeconomic channels: Dutch Disease, commodity price volatility, and the "government spending effect" (resource revenue finances public consumption rather than investment). But at the sub-national level, the most damaging channel is institutional: resource rents weaken the fiscal bargain between the state and citizens.

In non-resource economies, the state must tax productive activity to finance public goods. This creates an implicit bargain: citizens pay taxes in exchange for services (infrastructure, education, security). The bargain creates accountability — citizens who pay taxes demand that the state use the revenue effectively — and builds administrative capacity (the tax collection apparatus doubles as a information system about economic activity).

In resource economies, the state can finance public goods from resource rents without taxing citizens. This severs the fiscal bargain: citizens have no leverage over state spending because they are not its source. The result, documented by Moore (2004) and Ross (2012), is lower accountability, weaker bureaucratic capacity, and higher corruption. The effect is strongest at the sub-national level, where resource revenue creates "rentier regions" — sub-national governments with large budgets but no incentive to build the administrative capacity to manage them.

Cajamarca in Peru is the canonical case. The Canon Minero transfers have given the department a budget that, on a per-capita basis, exceeds many middle-income municipalities in Lima. But the department lacks the engineers to design infrastructure projects, the procurement specialists to manage competitive bidding, the financial controllers to audit spending, and the project managers to ensure construction quality. The result is a combination of unspent balances (millions of soles sitting in bank accounts because there are no approved projects to spend them on) and wasteful spending (projects that are approved but poorly executed). The paradox — poverty amid plenty — is institutional, not fiscal.

Lab 1's institution-interaction specifications are relevant here. If the interaction between institutional quality and spatial spillovers is positive (as the Lab 1 results suggest), then the resource curse deepens the spatial divergence: resource-rich regions with good institutions benefit from both resource revenue and amplified spillovers, while resource-rich regions with weak institutions suffer from resource-curse dynamics and attenuated spillovers. The result is a widening gap between the best and worst performers within resource-dependent countries — precisely the pattern observed in Chile (prosperous Santiago vs. lagging Atacama towns outside the mining sector) and Peru (Lima's services boom vs. Cajamarca's poverty persistence).

## 5.3 Informality as Institutional Equilibrium

### The De Soto-Levy Debate

Hernando de Soto's *The Mystery of Capital* (2000) and *The Other Path* (1989) established the canonical framing of informality in Latin America: the poor are informal because formal institutions are inaccessible. Registration costs are prohibitive, licensing procedures are opaque, and property rights are insecure. The solution, De Soto argued, was institutional simplification: reduce the cost of formalization, and firms and workers will voluntarily enter the formal economy.

Santiago Levy's *Good Intentions, Bad Outcomes* (2008) challenged this framing with Mexican evidence. Levy argued that informality in Mexico is partly an equilibrium response to distortionary social policy: the Mexican government provides free healthcare (Seguro Popular, now INSABI), cash transfers (Prospera/Oportunidades), and subsidized housing to informal-sector workers, while formal-sector workers pay social security contributions and receive contributory benefits. The result is a tax on formality: a worker who formalizes gains social security but loses non-contributory benefits and incurs payroll tax deductions, producing a net disincentive to formalize.

Both perspectives contain truth, and the resolution is spatial. De Soto's formalization-barrier story is more applicable in regions where state capacity is genuinely weak — rural Oaxaca, where registering a business requires travel to the state capital, multiple interactions with corrupt bureaucracies, and payment of informal "facilitation fees" that can exceed the formal registration cost. In these regions, informality is not a choice but an imposition: the state is simply absent.

Levy's distortionary-incentive story is more applicable in regions where state capacity exists but is poorly targeted — Mexico City, where both formal and informal benefits are readily accessible and the margin of choice is real. Mexico City's informal sector includes software developers who freelance to avoid payroll taxes, taxi drivers who work without medallions because licensing fees are prohibitive, and food vendors who operate without health permits because the inspection regime is capricious. These are not the desperately poor — many earn more than formal-sector workers — but they have rationally calculated that the costs of formalization exceed the benefits.

A third perspective, advanced by La Porta and Shleifer (2014), argues that informality is simply a reflection of low productivity: informal firms are small, use old technology, and employ unskilled workers because they cannot afford to be anything else. Formalization barriers and distortionary incentives matter at the margin, but the fundamental constraint is that these firms operate in sectors (retail, personal services, construction) where the returns to scale and technology are too small to justify the fixed costs of formal operation. In this view, the policy priority is not to formalize existing informal firms (which may be too unproductive to survive in the formal economy) but to create conditions for new, productive formal firms to grow.

### The Spatial Distribution of Informality

Informality in Latin America follows a distinct spatial pattern: it is highest in rural areas, secondary cities, and peripheral urban neighborhoods, and lowest in central business districts, formal industrial zones, and regions with strong institutional presence. In Mexico, informal employment ranges from roughly 35 percent in Nuevo León (Monterrey's state) to over 80 percent in Chiapas and Guerrero. In Brazil, the range is similar: São Paulo's formal employment share exceeds 70 percent, while Maranhão's is below 40 percent.

This spatial gradient is not merely a reflection of income differences (though richer regions are more formal). Controlling for income, informality rates remain higher in regions with weaker institutional environments — suggesting that the relationship between informality and institutions is not reducible to income alone. The World Bank's sub-national Doing Business data provides direct evidence: in Mexico, the time and cost of registering a business vary by a factor of five across states, from roughly 10 days and $1,000 in Nuevo León to 50 days and $5,000 in Oaxaca. These are not income differences — they are bureaucratic differences that reflect the quality of local regulatory infrastructure.

The gradient reflects institutional accessibility: the cost, in time and money, of interacting with formal institutions. A firm in São Paulo can register online, pay taxes through an integrated digital system, and enforce contracts through a functioning judiciary. A firm in Maranhão faces multi-week registration procedures, paper-based tax administration, and a judicial system with multi-year backlogs. The *effective* cost of formality — not just the statutory cost — varies spatially in ways that the national average obscures.

### Implications for Structural Upgrading

Informality is not merely an inconvenience or a statistical nuisance. It is a structural barrier to upgrading because informal firms cannot participate in the formal supply chains that connect domestic producers to international markets. A maquiladora in Monterrey cannot source components from an informal workshop in Oaxaca, because the workshop cannot provide the invoices, certifications, and origin documentation that USMCA compliance requires. An agribusiness exporter in São Paulo cannot source from an informal farm in Bahia, because the farm cannot meet phytosanitary standards or provide the traceability documentation that European and Japanese buyers demand.

The result is a dual economy within each country — a formal sector integrated into global value chains and a large informal sector disconnected from them. The spatial expression of this duality is the institutional gradient: well-documented formal regions at the center of global trade networks, poorly documented informal regions at the periphery. Trade liberalization, in this framework, benefits the formal regions (which can exploit new market access) and leaves the informal regions behind (which lack the institutional capacity to participate). This is the micro-mechanism behind the conditional-spillover result in Lab 1: trade linkages transmit growth only where the institutional infrastructure for formal economic activity exists.

### Cross-Border Institutional Gradients

Some of the most revealing spatial variation in informality occurs along international borders. The US-Mexico border regions offer a natural experiment: on the US side of the border (El Paso, Laredo, San Diego), formal employment is essentially universal — even low-wage workers participate in social security, workers' compensation, and tax systems. On the Mexican side (Ciudad Juárez, Nuevo Laredo, Tijuana), formal employment in the maquiladora sector coexists with massive informality in the local service economy that surrounds the factories.

The cross-border gradient is not merely a reflection of income differences. It reflects the institutional environment: the marginal cost of formality is lower on the US side (streamlined registration, digital tax filing, established labor law enforcement) than on the Mexican side (complex registration, in-person bureaucratic requirements, uneven enforcement). Workers and firms respond to these incentives rationally, producing a spatial discontinuity in informality that is visible in satellite-derived night-lights data: the Mexican border cities show a characteristic pattern of bright industrial zones (maquiladoras operating formally, with electricity contracts and documented employment) surrounded by dimmer residential and commercial areas (the informal economy that serves the formal one).

The Colombian-Ecuadorian border shows a similar but less extreme pattern. Tulcán (Ecuador) and Ipiales (Colombia) are twin cities separated by the Rumichaca bridge. Both are commercial hubs for cross-border trade in agricultural products, textiles, and consumer goods. But Ecuador's historically more centralized regulatory system (reformed under the "dolarización" regime that adopted the US dollar in 2000) and Colombia's more decentralized system produce different informality rates and different integration into national value chains.

These border-region comparisons are natural laboratories for testing the institutional theory of informality. If informality is driven by institutional barriers (as De Soto argues), it should jump discretely at the border — reflecting the different regulatory environments. If it is driven by income levels (as simple human-capital models suggest), it should decline gradually with distance from the border on both sides. The evidence supports the institutional interpretation: informality exhibits a sharp discontinuity at the border, consistent with regulatory environment rather than income as the primary determinant.

### The Measurement Challenge

Informality creates a fundamental measurement problem for the spatial analyst. If a substantial share of economic activity is informal — unregistered, untaxed, and unmeasured — then official GDP data understate the true level of economic output in high-informality regions. The understatement is not uniform: it is larger in regions with more informality, which are also the poorest regions. This means that official income data exaggerate regional inequality within LAC countries — the "poor" regions are not as poor as the data suggest, because much of their economic activity is invisible to statistical agencies.

The night-lights methodology that Lab 6 develops for Africa is relevant here. Night-lights radiance provides an alternative activity measure that captures both formal and informal economic activity — any activity that generates light (residential, commercial, industrial, informal market) registers in the satellite data. Henderson, Storeygard, and Weil (2012) showed that night-lights-augmented GDP estimates significantly revise growth rates in countries with large informal sectors. Applying this methodology to LAC would allow a test of whether the sub-national middle-income trap looks different when informal activity is included — a possibility that the official data cannot evaluate.

## 5.4 Green Commodity Frontiers: Lithium, Hydrogen, and the Question of Institutional Learning

### A Historical Parallel: The Guano Age

The green commodity frontier is not the first time Latin American resources have been revalued by global technological change. In the mid-nineteenth century, Peruvian guano — bird droppings accumulated over millennia on the Pacific coast islands — became the most valuable fertilizer in the world as European agriculture intensified. Guano exports generated enormous revenue for the Peruvian state between 1840 and 1880, transforming Lima into one of the wealthiest cities in the Americas. But the revenue was consumed rather than invested: government spending on patronage, military adventures, and infrastructure projects that benefited Lima rather than the guano-producing coast. When synthetic fertilizers replaced guano in the 1880s, Peru was left with neither the resource nor the institutional capacity to sustain growth.

The parallel to contemporary lithium is uncomfortably close. Lithium, like guano, is a natural resource whose value derives from a specific technological regime (batteries, as fertilizer was to agriculture). If solid-state batteries or sodium-ion chemistries mature, lithium demand could plateau or decline — and the extractive regions would face the same adjustment that Peru's coast faced when guano prices collapsed. The institutional question is whether the lithium-producing countries and regions can convert extraction revenue into productive diversification before the technological regime shifts. Chile's sovereign wealth fund approach is designed for exactly this scenario; Bolivia's and Argentina's are not.

### The New Commodity Geography

Latin America's resource endowment is being revalued by the energy transition. The "lithium triangle" — spanning the salt flats of northern Chile (Salar de Atacama), Bolivia (Salar de Uyuni), and Argentina (the Puna plateau) — contains an estimated 56 percent of the world's known lithium resources. Chile's Atacama Desert has among the highest solar irradiance on earth, making it a prime location for green hydrogen production. Brazil's northeast has comparable solar potential plus substantial wind resources. Colombia and Peru have significant deposits of copper, nickel, and other minerals essential for electric vehicles, battery storage, and grid infrastructure.

These resources are generating a new wave of commodity-driven investment. Chile's National Lithium Strategy (2023) aims to create a state-managed lithium sector modeled on Norway's petroleum fund approach: maximum value extraction, environmental sustainability, and sovereign ownership of the resource. The strategy requires that future lithium concessions include a mandatory state equity stake and commitments to downstream processing within Chile — an attempt to capture more of the value chain domestically rather than exporting raw lithium carbonate for processing in China and South Korea.

Argentina's approach is the opposite: provincial governments (Salta, Jujuy, Catamarca) control mineral rights and compete to attract foreign investment with favorable tax terms and minimal regulatory requirements. The result has been rapid investment (over $10 billion in announced lithium projects by 2025) but minimal local value capture. Most projects are controlled by Canadian, Chinese, or Australian firms that will process the lithium overseas.

Bolivia's approach is more radical and less successful. The Morales government declared lithium a "strategic resource" in 2008 and assigned extraction exclusively to the state enterprise YLB (Yacimientos de Litio Bolivianos). Political constraints have delayed commercialization for over fifteen years: as of 2025, Bolivia has produced less than 1 percent of global lithium output despite holding an estimated 21 million tonnes of reserves — the world's largest.

Beyond lithium, Argentina's Vaca Muerta shale formation — the second-largest shale gas reserve outside North America — is attracting billions in foreign investment for LNG export. Brazil's wind and solar corridor in the Northeast (Rio Grande do Norte, Ceará, Bahia) is growing at 20–30 percent annually, attracting European and Chinese developers who see Brazil's northeastern coast as one of the world's best onshore wind resources.

### The Institutional Question

The central question for this chapter is whether these green commodity frontiers will produce different outcomes than the extractive commodity frontiers that preceded them. The optimistic case argues that renewable resources are fundamentally different from depletable resources: solar and wind do not run out, lithium demand will grow for decades (unlike commodity cycles that boom and bust), and the climate-crisis urgency creates political commitment to long-term planning that previous commodity booms lacked.

The pessimistic case argues that the institutional dynamics are the same. Resource rents — whether from copper, oil, or lithium — attract the same rent-seeking behavior. The lithium triangle countries face the same governance challenges as the oil states: how to distribute revenue equitably, how to prevent corruption in licensing and extraction, how to invest resource wealth in productive diversification rather than consumption. Bolivia's attempt to nationalize lithium extraction (through the state company YLB) has produced minimal output and massive delays. Chile's more market-oriented approach (granting concessions to SQM and Albemarle) has produced large output but controversies over water use, indigenous land rights, and corporate taxation. Argentina's decentralized approach (provinces control mineral rights) has attracted foreign investment but created a race-to-the-bottom in regulatory standards across lithium-producing provinces.

The spatial analysis adds a layer. Green commodity investments are spatially concentrated — lithium in the Puna, solar in the Atacama, wind in Brazil's Northeast — and their local effects depend on whether the host regions have the institutional capacity to capture backward linkages (local supply chains), forward linkages (processing and manufacturing), and fiscal linkages (tax revenue invested in local public goods). If the investment produces only an "enclave" — a mine or solar farm staffed by external workers, with revenue flowing to national coffers or foreign shareholders — then the host region sees minimal benefit and bears the environmental and social costs.

CORFO's experience with cluster development in Chile suggests that enclave dynamics can be broken, but only with sustained institutional investment: vocational training programs linked to the mining sector, supplier development programs that qualify local firms for procurement contracts, and fiscal mechanisms that reinvest resource revenue in regional infrastructure. The Atacama Solar Platform — a public-private initiative to develop a domestic solar-component supply chain — is an early test case.

### Climate Migration and the Spatial Economics of the Central American Dry Corridor

Green commodity frontiers represent one spatial force reshaping Latin America's economic geography. A second, operating simultaneously and in the opposite direction — generating displacement rather than investment — is climate-induced migration from the Central American Dry Corridor. The two forces interact: regions gaining green investment may absorb climate migrants, while regions losing population to climate emigration experience compounding institutional erosion.

The Central American Dry Corridor is a semi-arid zone stretching from Chiapas (southern Mexico) through Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador, and into Nicaragua — a belt of roughly 6 million people who depend primarily on rain-fed subsistence agriculture. The corridor has always been climatically marginal for food production, but rising temperatures, shifting precipitation patterns, and increased drought frequency since the 2000s have pushed it beyond the adaptation capacity of smallholder farming systems. The 2018–2019 drought caused crop failures affecting 2.2 million people across Honduras, Guatemala, and El Salvador (ECLAC/FAO 2020). The drought of 2023 produced comparable displacement. Between droughts, episodic hurricanes (Eta and Iota in 2020 caused $3.5 billion in damage in Honduras and Guatemala) create acute displacement events that accumulate into chronic migration flows.

The economics of climate migration follow the logic of spatial equilibrium under deteriorating opportunity sets. Schlenker and Roberts (2009) document nonlinear damage to corn and soybean yields above 29–32°C; Hsiang, Meng, and Cane (2011) connect El Niño-driven rainfall variability to civil conflict onset; Dell, Jones, and Olken (2012) estimate that a 1°C increase in annual temperature reduces per-capita income by roughly 1.4 percent in poor countries, primarily through agricultural productivity effects. In the Dry Corridor context, these productivity shocks combine with the poverty and institutional weakness documented throughout this chapter to produce emigration as the dominant household-level adaptation strategy.

The spatial destination patterns of Dry Corridor migrants have economic-geographic consequences at multiple scales. At the intra-regional scale, Guatemala City, San Salvador, and San Pedro Sula receive migrants who join the urban informal economy — reinforcing the deindustrialization-into-informality pattern of Section 5.1 at the receiving cities. At the cross-border scale, the northward flow to Mexico and the United States creates a labor supply expansion in US agricultural regions (California, Florida, the Carolinas), construction, and food processing — a Mode 4 services trade channel whose volume, in workers rather than dollars, rivals the Mode 1 digital services flows discussed in Section 5.1. At the origin-region scale, remittances have become the primary income source for many Dry Corridor villages — substituting for formal employment, providing a consumption floor, but also potentially reducing the political pressure for institutional reform by making exit (emigration) more attractive than voice (local political engagement).

For Central American and Caribbean economies, remittances function as a commodity-like inflow that creates macroeconomic distortions analogous to Dutch Disease -- but without the natural resource. Honduras receives remittances equivalent to roughly 25 percent of GDP; El Salvador approximately 24 percent; Guatemala 18 percent (World Bank 2024). These dollar-denominated inflows appreciate the real exchange rate, making domestic manufacturing and commercial agriculture less competitive on international markets -- the same mechanism through which oil or mineral windfalls crowd out tradeable sectors. Acosta, Lartey, and Mandelman (2009) document significant real appreciation and manufacturing contraction in remittance-dependent economies across the Caribbean and Central America. The spatial effect is a "consumption floor" that sustains household welfare in receiving communities but hollows out the tradeable sectors that might otherwise generate formal employment and productivity growth. Unlike commodity booms, which at least build some extractive infrastructure, remittance-driven appreciation produces no productive capital stock in the receiving economy, reinforcing the informality trap documented in Section 5.3 and reducing the political urgency of institutional reform by substituting private transfers for public provision.

The interaction with green commodity investment is consequential but underexplored. Northern Chile and Argentina's Puna — the lithium triangle — are not significant sources of climate migration, because they are already too dry for rain-fed agriculture and their populations are small. The solar and wind frontiers in Brazil's Northeast present a more complex picture: the same semi-arid Sertão region that is being developed for wind and solar is also experiencing episodic drought and chronic rural poverty that generates northward and southward migration. If green energy development creates local employment (as CORFO's cluster model tries to achieve), it could reduce out-migration by providing formal-economy alternatives to subsistence agriculture. If it produces enclave extraction (offshore developers, external labor), it compounds displacement.

The Central American climate migration pattern represents a harder policy challenge than the green commodity frontier, because its spatial economics span international borders and mix humanitarian, trade, labor market, and development policy dimensions that no single institutional framework addresses. It is also a leading indicator of climate-driven regional economic dislocation that will become more common across the developing world as temperature and precipitation trends continue — a theme that Chapter 15 synthesizes across all regions.

### The Product Space and Green Diversification

The product space framework (Hidalgo et al. 2007; see Chapter 2) offers a diagnostic tool for evaluating green commodity strategies. The framework predicts that countries can diversify most easily into products that are "nearby" in the product space — products that require similar capabilities to those the country already possesses. For lithium-producing countries, the relevant question is: what capabilities does lithium extraction build, and what products are adjacent in the capability space?

Raw lithium extraction (evaporation from brine or hard-rock mining) builds capabilities in mining engineering, chemical processing, water management, and heavy logistics. These capabilities are adjacent to: lithium carbonate refining (the first processing step), cathode material production (the second step), and eventually battery cell manufacturing (the most value-added step). The product space predicts a potential upgrading path from extraction to processing to manufacturing — but only if the institutional environment supports each step.

Chile has moved furthest along this path, with CORFO-supported investments in lithium carbonate processing and early-stage cathode material research. But the gap between mining and battery manufacturing is enormous in terms of capabilities: battery production requires precision manufacturing, clean-room environments, quality control systems, and integration with electronics and automotive supply chains that do not exist in the Atacama Desert. The product-space distance from "lithium mine" to "battery factory" is comparable to the distance from "copper mine" to "aircraft factory" — not impossible, but requiring decades of institutional investment.

Argentina and Bolivia are further behind. Argentina's decentralized model has attracted extraction investment but no processing investment — foreign firms extract lithium carbonate and export it for processing in China and South Korea. Bolivia's nationalized model has produced a state-owned pilot plant that has not reached commercial production. In neither case is the green commodity creating the local institutional capacity that would enable upgrading.

The spatial implication is that the green commodity frontier, absent institutional upgrading, will reproduce the resource-curse geography: extraction in peripheral regions, processing and manufacturing in existing industrial centers (East Asia, North America, Europe), and a terms-of-trade relationship that transfers value from commodity producers to manufacturers. The lithium triangle may be the new copper belt — a source of raw materials for someone else's industrial revolution.

## 5.5 Lab 1 Extended: Institutional Quality and Spatial Spillovers in the Hemisphere

### From North America to the Hemisphere

Chapter 4 interpreted Lab 1's results primarily through the lens of USMCA and the US-Mexico-Canada corridor. But the Lab 1 sample includes the entire Western Hemisphere — 34 economies spanning from Canada to Chile. The conditional-spillover finding has implications that extend beyond North America.

Consider the trade-weighted $W$ matrix for the full hemisphere. The dominant off-diagonal entries involve the United States: nearly every country in the Americas trades more with the US than with any neighbor. This means that $Wy_i$ — the trade-weighted average growth of $i$'s partners — is heavily influenced by US growth for most countries. The near-zero $\rho$ implies that US growth cycles do not systematically predict growth in trading partners after conditioning on macro variables. This seems counterintuitive — surely US demand matters for its trading partners? — but the conditioning is important. The macro controls (GDP per capita, manufacturing share) absorb much of the variation that is correlated with US demand, and the residual spatial signal is weak.

The interaction term reveals what the unconditional specification misses. The interaction between border-governance quality and the spatial lag operates across the hemisphere: Caribbean economies with strong customs administrations (Trinidad and Tobago, Dominican Republic) show different growth responses to US demand shocks than those with weak customs (Haiti, Guyana). South American commodity exporters with transparent fiscal regimes (Chile, Uruguay) transmit regional growth shocks differently than those with opaque fiscal systems (Venezuela, Bolivia).

The $\beta$ coefficient on the quality × log-GDP-per-capita interaction ($\approx -3.7$) deserves careful interpretation. The negative sign means that *among countries with better border governance*, the convergence effect (poorer countries growing faster) is stronger. In plain language: institutions accelerate convergence. A poor country with good customs and logistics quality catches up faster than a poor country with bad logistics quality. This is consistent with the entire theoretical apparatus of Chapters 1–2: the NEG's agglomeration benefits and the institutional framework's growth mechanisms both predict that institutional quality enables the exploitation of market access — and market access, for poor countries, means convergence toward the technological frontier.

The $\beta$ on quality × manufacturing share ($\approx 4.0$) is also informative. The positive sign means that *among countries with better governance*, a higher manufacturing share is more strongly associated with growth. Manufacturing is productive — but only when the institutional environment supports it. A manufacturing sector embedded in a formal, well-regulated economy contributes more to growth than one embedded in informality and regulatory chaos. This provides a micro-foundation for the premature deindustrialization story of Section 5.1: when institutions are weak, manufacturing's contribution to growth is small, and countries "deindustrialize" — lose manufacturing share — at lower income levels because manufacturing is not generating enough productivity growth to sustain itself.

This chapter's contribution is to interpret these patterns through the institutional lens of Sections 5.1–5.4. The near-zero unconditional $\rho$ reflects the coexistence of institutional regimes that respond very differently to the same trade linkages. The interaction specifications show that institutional quality *mediates* the spatial transmission of growth — it is not merely a level effect (better institutions → higher growth) but a *slope* effect (better institutions → stronger spatial multiplier).

### Sub-National Extensions

The Lab 1 framework is estimated at the country level because sub-national GDP data are not available for all 34 hemispheric economies. But the theory applies — and arguably applies more forcefully — at the sub-national level. The institutional gradient within Brazil (São Paulo vs. Maranhão) is larger than the institutional gradient between Brazil and Chile at the national level. If institutional quality conditions spatial spillovers, then the within-country variation in spillover strength should exceed the between-country variation — a prediction that could be tested with sub-national data from the five CEPALSTAT countries.

A natural extension of Lab 1 would construct a sub-national panel for Brazil and Mexico (the two largest LAC economies, with the best sub-national data), build a trade-weighted or distance-weighted $W$ at the state level, and estimate the same SAR with institution-interaction specifications. If the conditional-spillover result holds at the sub-national level, it would provide much stronger evidence for the institutional mechanism — because within-country analyses control for national-level institutions (monetary policy, trade agreements, legal systems) that confound the cross-country comparison.

## Data in Depth: Measuring Premature Deindustrialization at the Sub-National Level

**The question.** At what income level does manufacturing's share of GDP peak in Latin American sub-national regions, and how does this "turning point" vary with institutional quality?

**Data.** ECLAC's CEPALSTAT provides sub-national GDP by sector for Brazil (27 states), Mexico (32 states), Colombia (33 departments), Chile (16 regions), and Peru (25 departments). ILO ILOSTAT provides informal employment shares at the national level, with sub-national breakdowns available from national labor force surveys for Brazil (PNAD Contínua), Mexico (ENOE), and Colombia (GEIH).

**Method.** Following Rodrik (2016), estimate the relationship between manufacturing share and log income using a quadratic specification:

$$
\text{mfg_share}_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \ln y_{it} + \beta_2 (\ln y_{it})^2 + \gamma X_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}
$$

where $X_{it}$ includes institutional quality proxies (formal employment share, fiscal autonomy index) and time effects. The turning point — the income level at which manufacturing peaks — is $y^* = \exp(-\beta_1 / 2\beta_2)$. The key test is whether the turning point shifts upward when institutional quality is high — that is, whether institutionally strong regions sustain manufacturing to higher income levels before deindustrializing.

**Expected findings.** Preliminary results suggest that the turning point for institutionally strong LAC regions (São Paulo, Nuevo León, Antioquia) is roughly $8,000–10,000 (constant 2015 dollars), while for institutionally weak regions (Maranhão, Chiapas, La Guajira) it is $3,000–5,000. The 2:1 ratio is consistent with the hypothesis that institutional quality delays premature deindustrialization by enabling firms to upgrade into higher-value manufacturing rather than exiting the sector entirely.

**Sensitivity checks.** The key robustness exercise is to replace the ILO informality measure with alternative institutional proxies: (a) the World Bank's sub-national Doing Business scores (available for Mexico, Colombia, Peru); (b) own-source fiscal revenue as a share of total sub-national revenue (from OECD/ECLAC); (c) the Economic Complexity Index computed at the sub-national level (from the Growth Lab's sub-national complexity data). If the turning-point shift is robust to alternative institutional measures, the result is more credible.

**Takeaway.** Premature deindustrialization is not a national-level inevitability. It is a sub-national phenomenon whose timing and severity depend on institutional quality. Policy responses should be spatially differentiated — defending manufacturing where institutions support upgrading, and facilitating transition into productive services where deindustrialization is already advanced. The Data in Depth exercise directly connects the theoretical argument of this chapter to the spatial methods of Chapter 3-A: the quadratic specification is testable, the institutional interaction is measurable, and the sub-national turning point is a quantity that policymakers can use to calibrate intervention timing.

## Spatial Data Challenge: Informal Trade and the Limits of Official Statistics

ECLAC estimates that 40 to 60 percent of cross-border trade in the Andean sub-region — Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia — flows through informal channels: unregistered small-scale traders, smugglers, and informal re-export networks that bypass customs infrastructure entirely. Similar estimates apply to Central American borders and the Brazil-Paraguay corridor (Ciudad del Este is one of the world's largest informal re-export centers for electronics and consumer goods).

This systematic undercounting creates a fundamental problem for any spatial analysis of LAC economic integration that relies on official bilateral trade data. Gravity-model estimates using recorded Comtrade flows will understate the true economic integration of border regions, generating border-effect estimates that appear large but partly reflect data gaps rather than genuine trade costs. Sub-national analysis is worse: while Comtrade records flow at the country level, informal trade is inherently local — small traders operating specific border crossings — meaning that any sub-national analysis based on officially recorded flows misrepresents the spatial pattern of economic linkages in high-informality regions.

**What can be done.** Several approaches partially address the gap:

1. *Mirror statistics.* For a given pair of countries, the exporter's reported exports should equal the importer's reported imports (adjusted for insurance and freight). Where they diverge systematically, the gap proxies for informal trade flows. ECLAC's trade statistics working group maintains bilateral discrepancy tables that provide an approximate upper bound on informal trade volumes.

2. *Night-lights as informal activity proxies.* If informal markets generate light (from stalls, residences, informal electricity connections), night-lights radiance may partially capture their economic activity in ways that official statistics do not. Border towns that appear larger in night-lights data than in official GDP estimates may have large informal trade sectors that the GDP data misses.

3. *Household survey consumption data.* Some informal goods show up in household consumption surveys (ENAHO in Peru, ENIGH in Mexico) as consumer expenditure on goods whose supply chain is formally unrecorded. Cross-walking household consumption to production statistics can identify sectors where the discrepancy implies large informal supply.

**The research implication.** Any chapter-level analysis of trade integration in LAC — including the Lab 1 SAR framework applied to hemispheric data — should be interpreted with awareness that the effective economic integration of the most informal regions is understated. The conditional-spillover finding (institutional quality mediates trade spillovers) may partly reflect the measurement effect: formally integrated regions with good customs administration are precisely the regions whose trade flows are fully recorded, while informally integrated regions with poor customs are precisely the regions where trade is undercounted. Addressing this would require integrating satellite-derived activity measures with official trade data — a data infrastructure project currently beyond the capacity of most LAC statistical agencies.

## Spatial Data Challenge: Diagnosing Premature Deindustrialization in a Heterogeneous Services Sector

The Rodrik (2016) premature deindustrialization framework measures structural change through the manufacturing share of GDP or employment: regions that lose manufacturing share at low income levels are diagnosed as "prematurely deindustrializing." But this diagnosis is far more informative about what regions are leaving (manufacturing) than about where they are going.

The services sector that absorbs displaced manufacturing workers in Latin America spans an enormous productivity range. At the top: financial services in São Paulo, software development in Bogotá, agribusiness logistics in Buenos Aires, and medical tourism in Monterrey — tradable services with productivities comparable to manufacturing. At the bottom: street vending, domestic cleaning, unlicensed transport, and informal construction — activities with productivities well below manufacturing and no connection to formal value chains. Standard national accounts aggregate all of these under "services" using ISIC or CNAE classification systems that were designed for structural-account purposes, not for measuring structural transformation quality.

**The problem.** Using the standard ISIC classification, a region that loses 10 percentage points of manufacturing and gains 10 percentage points of finance and software is diagnosed identically to a region that loses 10 points of manufacturing and gains 10 points of informal retail. Both show the same "deindustrialization" in national accounts — but one is upgrading and one is regressing. The analytical challenge is to distinguish productive structural transformation from distressed informalization at the sub-national level, where official data are least granular.

**Approaches.** Three approaches provide partial solutions:

1. *Formal employment share as a services-quality proxy.* If most formal jobs are in higher-productivity services, and most informal jobs are in lower-productivity services, then the share of formal employment (from labor force surveys) proxies for the productivity composition of the services sector. ENOE (Mexico) and PNAD Contínua (Brazil) provide sub-national formal employment rates that can be tracked over time.

2. *Economic Complexity Index at the sub-national level.* The Growth Lab's sub-national complexity data, derived from occupation and industry mix in labor force surveys, provides a summary measure of a region's productive capabilities. A rising ECI alongside declining manufacturing share indicates productive diversification; a falling ECI indicates that the services transition is into low-capability activities.

3. *Earnings distribution analysis.* If a region's services sector is absorbing workers at high wages (comparable to the manufacturing wages they left), the structural change is productive; if workers are absorbing into services at lower wages, the change is distressing. Panel data linking individual wages to their industrial transitions — available from Brazil's RAIS linked employer-employee database — provides the most direct measure, but only for the formal sector.

**The research gap.** No standardized, sub-nationally comparable measure of "productive services share" exists for Latin America as a whole. This is a significant data infrastructure gap: the difference between a region upgrading into productive tradable services and a region absorbing workers into subsistence informality is the single most policy-relevant distinction in the structural transformation literature, and it cannot be measured directly with available sub-national data for most LAC countries.

## Institutional Spotlight: CORFO and the Architecture of Adaptive Industrial Policy

Chile's Production Development Corporation (Corporación de Fomento de la Producción, CORFO) was founded in 1939 in the aftermath of a devastating earthquake and the Great Depression, with a mandate to rebuild national infrastructure and promote industrialization. For its first four decades, CORFO was a classic import-substitution-industrialization (ISI) vehicle: it built steel mills, electricity grids, and state-owned enterprises across Chile's territory.

The transformation began in the 1980s under the Pinochet government's privatization program, which sold most of CORFO's industrial assets. Rather than disappearing, CORFO reinvented itself as a "metastructural" institution: instead of owning productive assets, it would build the institutional infrastructure within which private firms could innovate, cluster, and compete. The shift was gradual and contested, but by the 2000s, CORFO had become one of Latin America's most effective cluster-development agencies.

Three programs illustrate the model:

**The salmon cluster (Los Lagos region, 1990s–2000s).** CORFO co-financed research on salmon disease management, organized industry-university partnerships around aquaculture genetics, and funded infrastructure (cold-chain logistics, port upgrades) that reduced the cost of exporting premium salmon to Asian and European markets. Chile became the world's second-largest salmon exporter. The spatial effect was concentrated: Los Lagos and Aysén regions went from subsistence fishing to industrialized aquaculture in two decades.

**The solar platform (Atacama region, 2015–present).** As copper mining faces long-run depletion and climate transition pressures, CORFO has invested in building a domestic solar-energy ecosystem in Atacama — not just solar farms (which global developers can build without local capacity), but the upstream supply chain: solar panel component manufacturing, energy storage systems, and green hydrogen pilot plants. The goal is to avoid the enclave pattern by building local absorptive capacity alongside the resource investment.

**Startup Chile (national, 2010–present).** A global accelerator program that brings early-stage entrepreneurs to Chile, provides seed funding and mentorship, and connects them to the Chilean innovation ecosystem. The program has attracted over 2,000 startups from 85 countries. Its spatial effect is concentrated in Santiago, but CORFO has launched satellite programs in Valparaíso and Concepción to diffuse entrepreneurial activity beyond the capital.

CORFO's evolution from ISI vehicle to cluster-development agency illustrates that institutions can learn and adapt — but the learning is slow, path-dependent, and requires political protection from short-term electoral cycles. Chile's success is not easily replicable in countries where the institutional prerequisites (a professional civil service, fiscal discipline, political insulation of development agencies from patronage) are absent. CORFO is the exception that illuminates the rule: most Latin American development agencies remain captured by political clientelism, which is precisely why institutional thickness varies so dramatically across the continent.

## Conclusion: Escaping the Spatial Middle-Income Trap

Taken together, Chapters 4 and 5 deliver a unified message about the Americas: the continental economy is spatially interconnected through trade, investment, and migration, but the benefits of that interconnection are gated by institutional quality. Regions that have invested in formalization capacity, regulatory infrastructure, and human capital can exploit their trade linkages to grow. Regions that have not made those investments are connected to the same trade network but cannot absorb or transmit the growth impulses it carries.

The policy implication is that trade liberalization and industrial policy are necessary but insufficient for regional convergence. Without institutional upgrading at the sub-national level — the formalization of labor markets, the professionalization of customs administrations, the building of educational infrastructure — the spatial multiplier remains weak, and the gains from integration accumulate in the already-strong regions. This is the middle-income trap expressed as a spatial equilibrium: countries are "trapped" not because they lack access to global markets, but because the institutional infrastructure needed to exploit that access is unevenly distributed across their territory.

The contrast with East Asia illuminates what Latin America lacks. South Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore — the successful escapees from middle-income status — combined trade liberalization with aggressive institutional investment: universal education, meritocratic civil services, state-directed industrial policy, and regulatory capacity sufficient to manage complex supply chains. Their spatial economies are not free of inequality (see Chapters 6–7), but the institutional floor is high enough that even peripheral regions participate in the formal economy and benefit from trade integration.

Latin America's institutional floor is much lower. Brazil's most institutionally developed state (São Paulo) is comparable to a middle-tier East Asian economy; its least developed states (Maranhão, Piauí) are comparable to Sub-Saharan Africa. The within-country institutional range in LAC is among the widest in the world — wider than within China, wider than within India, and vastly wider than within the East Asian tigers. This institutional heterogeneity is the structural condition that produces the conditional-spillover result in Lab 1 and the premature deindustrialization pattern documented in this chapter.

The comparison suggests that escaping the middle-income trap requires not only the right macroeconomic policies (exchange rate management, fiscal discipline, trade openness) but also a minimum level of institutional thickness at the sub-national level — below which trade integration produces winners and losers rather than convergence. The challenge for Latin American policymakers is that building sub-national institutional capacity is slow, expensive, and politically unrewarding: its benefits are diffuse, long-term, and difficult to claim credit for. Building a factory is a photo opportunity; building a customs administration is not. But the evidence of this chapter suggests that the customs administration matters more.

The comparison also illuminates a temporal dimension. South Korea's institutional transformation took roughly thirty years (1960–1990), during which the country moved from a subsistence economy to an industrial powerhouse. Chile's transformation has taken roughly forty years (1985–2025) and is still incomplete. The institutional prerequisites for escaping the middle-income trap are achievable — the East Asian examples prove that — but they require sustained investment over a period that exceeds most electoral cycles and most policy-planning horizons. The spatial dimension adds urgency: within-country inequality tends to increase during the transition, as institutional upgrading occurs first in the already-advantaged regions. The political backlash against regional inequality — visible in Brazil's Nordeste-Sudeste tensions, Mexico's north-south divide, and Colombia's center-periphery conflicts — can itself undermine the institutional reforms needed to address it. This is the political economy trap within the middle-income trap: the inequality that institutional reform creates in the short run generates political opposition to the reforms that would reduce inequality in the long run.
