## Introduction: The Services Paradox

In 2023, India exported approximately $200 billion in information technology and business process outsourcing services — more than the combined merchandise exports of Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, and Pakistan. These exports were produced overwhelmingly in five metropolitan areas: Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune, Chennai, and the National Capital Region around Delhi. Together, these five clusters account for roughly 80 percent of India's IT-BPO export revenue, serving clients in North America, Europe, and increasingly the Middle East and Southeast Asia.

The spatial concentration is striking on its own terms, but the deeper puzzle is what it implies for India's development trajectory. The canonical development story — the one described by the flying-geese framework in Chapter 6 — involves manufacturing: rural workers migrate to cities, take factory jobs, acquire industrial skills, and the economy moves up the value chain from textiles to electronics to automobiles. India has, in a sense, skipped this step. Its globally competitive export sector is not manufacturing (where India lags China, Vietnam, and even Bangladesh) but services — specifically, the knowledge-intensive business services that the trade literature classifies under GATS Mode 1 (cross-border supply) and Mode 4 (presence of natural persons). The question for spatial economics is whether a services-led growth path can deliver the same broad-based development that manufacturing-led paths delivered in East Asia, or whether it creates a fundamentally different spatial pattern: a few globally connected urban nodes surrounded by a vast hinterland that the services revolution bypasses.

This question has theoretical roots in Chapter 1's foundational puzzle about services and distance. If services trade is distance-sensitive — as the gravity model evidence in Chapter 3-B demonstrates — then why do India's IT services exports flow so readily to clients 12,000 kilometers away in New York and London, while failing to transform the economy of Lucknow, 500 kilometers from Delhi? The answer lies in the nature of the distance that matters. Geographic distance is nearly irrelevant for digitally delivered services (an engineer in Bangalore can debug code for a client in San Francisco with the same latency as a colleague across the office). But institutional distance — the regulatory frameworks, quality signals, contractual enforcement mechanisms, and trust infrastructure that enable cross-border services transactions — is enormous. India's IT clusters have bridged the institutional distance to Western clients through decades of relationship-building, accreditation, and process standardization. They have not bridged the institutional distance to their own hinterland, where the educational infrastructure, physical connectivity, and regulatory environment required to participate in the knowledge economy remain underdeveloped.

The chapter proceeds in six sections. Section 8.1 examines the spatial economics of India's IT services clusters, tracing how Bangalore, Hyderabad, and their peers emerged as globally integrated nodes through a combination of institutional scaffolding (STPI, SEZ policy), human capital endowments (IITs, regional engineering colleges), and first-mover agglomeration dynamics. Section 8.2 applies the Grossman and Rossi-Hansberg (2008) task-trading framework to explain India's position on the services value chain and its evolving trajectory from BPO to KPO to GCC (Global Capability Centers). Section 8.3 analyzes brain circulation — the two-way flow of human capital between India and the West — as a spatial economic phenomenon with consequences for both origin and destination economies. Section 8.4 examines telemedicine as a test case for whether digital services delivery can reduce the geographic concentration that characterizes India's services economy. Section 8.5 turns to Bangladesh's garment-sector GVC integration as a manufacturing-track comparator, and examines the wider South Asian institutional landscape including SAARC's failures and Sri Lanka's debt crisis. Section 8.6 connects the analysis to Lab 3's empirical framework for mapping IT-BPO export concentration across Indian states.

## 8.1 The Clusters: Bangalore, Hyderabad, and the Institutional Origins of India's IT Geography

### Bangalore: First Mover and Agglomeration Anchor

Bangalore's emergence as India's — and one of the world's — leading IT services hubs was not inevitable. In the 1980s, Bangalore was a mid-sized city of about three million people, known primarily for its aerospace and defense establishments (Hindustan Aeronautics Limited, the Indian Space Research Organisation), its pleasant climate, and its cantonment-era civic infrastructure. It was not the obvious location for an IT services cluster. Bombay (now Mumbai) had the financial infrastructure. Delhi had the political connections. Madras (now Chennai) had the port. What Bangalore had was a concentration of engineering talent — the Indian Institute of Science (IISc), several regional engineering colleges, and the defense laboratories that employed technically skilled workers — and a state government in Karnataka that was willing to create the institutional framework for a software services industry to grow.

The institutional scaffolding came through the Software Technology Parks of India (STPI) scheme, launched nationally in 1991 but implemented with particular effectiveness in Bangalore. STPI provided three critical institutional services that individual firms could not have supplied for themselves. First, **telecommunications infrastructure**: in an era when Indian telephone networks were unreliable and international data links prohibitively expensive, STPI provided dedicated satellite uplinks and leased lines that gave software firms in Bangalore the same connectivity to Western clients that their competitors in Silicon Valley enjoyed. Second, **customs and regulatory simplification**: STPI firms could import hardware duty-free and were exempt from excise taxes on software services, reducing the bureaucratic burden that strangled manufacturing enterprises. Third, **a single-window clearance mechanism**: in a country where starting a business required navigating dozens of government agencies, STPI provided a single point of contact for permits, approvals, and compliance — an institutional shortcut through India's famously complex regulatory environment.

The agglomeration dynamics that followed are textbook NEG. Texas Instruments opened its Bangalore development center in 1985 — one of the first multinational technology operations in India. Infosys, founded in Pune in 1981, moved its headquarters to Bangalore in 1983, drawn by the engineering talent pool. Wipro, originally a vegetable oil company, transformed itself into an IT services firm in Bangalore during the late 1980s. Each new entrant deepened the local labor market (workers could move between firms without relocating), attracted more specialized suppliers (training institutes, placement agencies, legal firms with technology contract expertise), and strengthened the knowledge spillovers (engineers who left large firms to start companies drew on the skills and relationships they had acquired). By the mid-1990s, when the Y2K remediation boom created sudden global demand for large-scale software maintenance, Bangalore had the scale, the talent, and the institutional infrastructure to absorb it.

The Y2K episode deserves attention because it illustrates how a contingent event can lock in spatial advantage. The Y2K problem required millions of lines of legacy COBOL and FORTRAN code to be inspected and modified before January 1, 2000. The work was routine, labor-intensive, and time-sensitive — ideal for offshoring to a location with large numbers of trained programmers and lower wage costs. Bangalore's STPI firms were ideally positioned: they had the connectivity, the English-speaking workforce, and — crucially — the contractual relationships with Western firms that had been built during the previous decade of smaller outsourcing engagements. The Y2K work was not technologically sophisticated, but it demonstrated at scale that Indian firms could deliver large software projects reliably, on time, and at a fraction of Western costs. The trust established during Y2K became the foundation for the much higher-value outsourcing relationships that followed.

### Hyderabad: The Challenger Model

Hyderabad's emergence as India's second IT hub illustrates a different institutional path. While Bangalore grew organically around existing engineering institutions and defense establishments, Hyderabad's IT sector was the product of deliberate state policy — specifically, the industrial strategy of Chandrababu Naidu, Chief Minister of Andhra Pradesh from 1995 to 2004.

Naidu's strategy centered on HITEC City (Hyderabad Information Technology and Engineering Consultancy City), a public-private partnership between the state government and a private developer that created a purpose-built technology campus with international-standard infrastructure — reliable power, broadband connectivity, landscaped office parks — in a country where these amenities were far from guaranteed. The state government complemented HITEC City with aggressive investment promotion: personal meetings with the CEOs of Microsoft, Oracle, and IBM; streamlined approval processes that compressed months of bureaucracy into weeks; and tax incentives calibrated to attract anchor tenants whose presence would draw other firms.

The strategy worked. Microsoft opened its first Indian development center in Hyderabad in 1998. Google, Amazon, Facebook, and Apple followed. By 2023, Hyderabad hosted the largest concentration of Global Capability Centers (GCCs) — the corporate R&D and services centers that multinationals operate as captive facilities, distinct from the third-party outsourcing model that Infosys and Wipro pioneered. The distinction is analytically important: GCCs represent a different position on the services value chain than third-party BPO. A GCC is an internal unit of a multinational firm, performing functions that the firm considers strategic enough to control directly — product development, data science, cybersecurity, financial analytics — rather than outsourcing to a vendor. The GCC model implies deeper knowledge transfer and higher value-added per worker than the third-party model, and its spatial concentration in Hyderabad reflects the city's deliberate positioning as a premium destination for corporate R&D rather than a low-cost outsourcing hub.

### Pune, Chennai, and the Tier-2 Question

Pune and Chennai represent the next tier — cities that participate in the IT services economy but with distinct specialization patterns. Pune, two hours from Mumbai by expressway, has developed strength in engineering services and automotive software, leveraging its proximity to India's automotive manufacturing cluster (Tata Motors, Bajaj Auto, multiple multinational R&D centers) and its large population of engineering graduates from the University of Pune ecosystem. Chennai's IT sector, centered on the Mahindra World City SEZ and the Tidel Park complex, grew initially as a lower-cost alternative to Bangalore, absorbing routine BPO work that Bangalore firms shed as they moved up the value chain.

The tier-2 question — whether India's IT services economy can spread beyond the five major clusters to second-tier cities like Jaipur, Kochi, Bhubaneswar, Coimbatore, and Chandigarh — is the central spatial policy question for India's services-led development model. The government has promoted this decentralization through the Smart Cities Mission, expanded STPI centers, and incentives for IT firms that locate in non-metropolitan areas. The results have been modest. India's IT-BPO exports remain overwhelmingly concentrated in the big five, and the concentration has, if anything, intensified over time as the industry moves from routine BPO (where cost arbitrage favors lower-cost locations) to high-value GCC work (where agglomeration economies in specialized talent dominate).

The spatial economics are straightforward. Routine BPO — call centers, data entry, transaction processing — requires large numbers of workers with basic English and computer skills. These workers are available in many Indian cities, and the work can be distributed across locations without significant coordination costs. Knowledge process outsourcing and GCC work — financial modeling, drug discovery analytics, AI/ML development, cybersecurity operations — requires specialist talent that is concentrated in the cities with elite engineering and business schools, established tech ecosystems, and the lifestyle amenities (international schools, healthcare, entertainment) that attract globally mobile professionals. The smile curve applies to cities as well as firms: the high-value ends of the services value chain are the most spatially concentrated.

The COVID-19 pandemic introduced a natural experiment that briefly challenged this concentration thesis. In March 2020, roughly 4.5 million Indian IT workers shifted to remote work almost overnight — the largest work-from-home experiment in the industry's history. Many workers relocated to Tier-2 and Tier-3 cities (Kochi, Indore, Jaipur, Coimbatore), and firms reported that productivity held up for most routine delivery tasks. By 2024, the major IT firms had settled on hybrid models requiring two to three days per week in-office. The spatial implications remain ambiguous. WFH has distributed some economic activity to smaller cities, visible in co-working space expansion and residential real estate appreciation in IT-worker destination cities. But the command functions — client relationship management, solution architecture, presales — remain stubbornly concentrated in Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Pune. Whether remote work represents a durable centrifugal force or a temporary shock whose spatial effects will attenuate as firms reassert in-person coordination norms is the key open question for India's IT geography.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: Measuring IT-BPO Exports at the State Level**
>
> India's services export data is compiled by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) at the national level through balance of payments accounting. State-level disaggregation is not directly available from RBI sources — a significant gap for spatial analysis. Researchers must combine multiple imperfect proxies: STPI zone-level export data (which captures only firms registered under the STPI scheme, missing SEZ firms and non-registered exporters), NASSCOM industry estimates (which are self-reported by member firms and may have upward bias), and state-level GDP by sector from the Central Statistical Organisation (which captures the "information and communication" sector but does not separate exports from domestic services).
>
> The result is a measurement landscape where different data sources produce different geographies. STPI data overstates Bangalore's share (because Karnataka's STPI centers are older and more comprehensive than those in other states). NASSCOM data may overstate the total (because it includes revenue from domestic clients that is not technically an export). CSO data understates the IT sector's share in newer IT hubs (because the sector classification lags behind actual firm activities). Lab 3 asks students to confront these discrepancies directly: constructing concentration indices from multiple sources and interpreting the differences as signals about the data-generating process rather than as noise to be averaged away.

## 8.2 Task Trading and the Services Value Chain

### The Grossman–Rossi-Hansberg Framework

The theoretical foundation for understanding India's services offshoring pattern comes from Grossman and Rossi-Hansberg (2008), who reconceptualized international trade as trade in tasks rather than trade in goods. In their framework, production of any good or service requires the completion of a set of tasks, each of which can potentially be performed in a different location. The decision of where to perform each task depends on the interaction of three factors: the communication costs of coordinating across locations, the routineness of the task (how easily it can be codified and transmitted), and the factor price difference between locations.

The framework generates a clear prediction about the spatial distribution of offshored tasks. Tasks that are highly routine, require little real-time coordination, and can be codified in written instructions — data entry, transaction processing, rule-based accounting — will be offshored to the lowest-cost location regardless of distance. Tasks that require tacit knowledge, real-time interaction, and complex judgment — strategic consulting, creative design, legal argumentation — will remain in high-cost locations because the communication costs of offshoring them exceed the labor cost savings. Between these extremes lies a large middle zone of tasks where the offshoring decision is sensitive to incremental changes in communication costs, automation technology, and the skill composition of the offshore workforce.

India's IT-BPO industry has progressively moved up this task hierarchy. The first wave (late 1980s to late 1990s) was dominated by routine software maintenance and data processing — the COBOL remediation and back-office work that established the offshoring model. The second wave (2000s) moved into more complex business processes: financial services back-office (trade settlement, loan processing, regulatory compliance), healthcare administration (medical billing, claims processing, clinical data management), and customer relationship management. The third wave (2010s to present) has reached knowledge process outsourcing and Global Capability Centers: pharmaceutical R&D analytics, advanced financial modeling, artificial intelligence and machine learning development, and product engineering.

### The Smile Curve: Value Added Across the Services Chain

The "smile curve" — coined by Stan Shih, founder of Acer, to describe the distribution of value added in manufacturing supply chains — applies with equal force to services. In the services value chain, the left side of the smile (high value-added upstream activities) includes strategic consulting, system architecture, product design, and intellectual property creation. The bottom of the curve (low value-added middle activities) includes routine processing, data entry, basic customer service, and standardized testing. The right side (high value-added downstream activities) includes client relationship management, market-specific customization, and implementation oversight.

India's five major IT clusters occupy different positions on this curve, and their positions are shifting over time. Bangalore has moved furthest up both sides: it hosts the largest concentration of R&D-intensive GCCs (Google's largest office outside the US, Amazon's second-largest development center, Goldman Sachs' largest engineering office) and has developed a venture-capital-funded startup ecosystem that produces original products rather than providing services to foreign clients. Hyderabad's GCC concentration places it firmly on the left side (upstream R&D). Pune's engineering services specialization occupies the middle-to-upper range. Chennai and the NCR still have significant exposure to the bottom of the curve through large-scale BPO operations, though both are shifting upward.

The smile curve has a spatial corollary that connects to Chapter 1's agglomeration framework. Activities at the bottom of the curve — routine BPO — exhibit weak agglomeration economies: they need workers, desks, and connectivity, all of which are available in many locations. Activities at the extremes of the curve — upstream R&D and downstream client management — exhibit strong agglomeration economies: they need specialized talent, knowledge spillovers, client proximity (physical or institutional), and the creative density that urban theorists from Jane Jacobs to Richard Florida have emphasized. The implication is that India's IT services industry will become more spatially concentrated, not less, as it moves up the value chain — precisely the opposite of the spatial diffusion that policymakers hope for.

Yet the centripetal forces sustaining Bangalore's dominance now face increasingly powerful centrifugal counterweights: world-class traffic congestion (average commute times exceeding ninety minutes), severe water scarcity (the 2024 crisis forced IT parks to truck in water at substantial cost), deteriorating air quality, and real estate prices that rival some European cities. These congestion costs operate exactly as Helpman's (1998) housing-cost mechanism predicts — they erode real wages and quality of life, pushing workers and firms toward lower-cost alternatives. Hyderabad's rise as a serious rival to Bangalore in the 2010s was driven in part by Hyderabad's lower congestion costs and the Telangana state government's aggressive infrastructure investment, a "second city" dynamic consistent with NEG predictions that congestion eventually limits the dominant cluster's growth. Whether these centrifugal forces are strong enough to produce genuine spatial rebalancing or merely redistribute activity among the top five clusters — without reaching the Tier-2 cities that policymakers target — remains an empirical question that Lab 3's concentration indices can track over time.

### From BPO to GCC: The Institutional Transition

The shift from third-party BPO to captive Global Capability Centers represents an institutional as much as an economic transition. Under the BPO model, Indian firms like Infosys, TCS, and Wipro mediated the relationship between Western clients and Indian workers. The Indian firms managed recruitment, training, quality control, and project delivery — institutional functions that individual Western clients could not efficiently perform at a distance. The BPO firm was, in effect, an institutional intermediary that reduced the transaction costs of cross-border services trade.

The GCC model eliminates this intermediary. When Microsoft operates a development center in Hyderabad with 10,000 employees, those employees work directly for Microsoft, use Microsoft's internal tools and processes, participate in Microsoft's global product development workflows, and are evaluated by Microsoft's performance management systems. The GCC imports the multinational's institutional environment directly — a kind of institutional enclave within the Indian economy. The knowledge transfer is potentially deeper (employees learn the firm's frontier practices, not a simplified version filtered through an outsourcing contract), but the spillover to the local economy is potentially narrower (the firm's proprietary knowledge stays within its boundaries, and the GCC's links to the local supplier ecosystem are weaker than those of a standalone Indian IT firm that sources locally).

This institutional transition has spatial implications. BPO operations could be distributed across multiple cities because the work was standardized and the Indian intermediary firms managed the coordination. GCC operations tend to concentrate in the cities where the multinational has already established a presence, where the senior talent it needs is available, and where the institutional environment (infrastructure, regulatory predictability, lifestyle quality) meets corporate standards. The GCC model thus reinforces the spatial concentration of India's IT sector rather than dispersing it.

## 8.3 Brain Circulation: Two-Way Flows and Spatial Consequences

### The Outflow: IITs, Immigration, and the Diaspora Premium

India is the world's largest source of international students, with approximately 770,000 studying abroad as of 2023 — a number that has been growing at roughly 10 percent per year. The spatial pattern of this outflow is highly non-random: students from the Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs), the Indian Institutes of Management (IIMs), and a handful of elite private institutions account for a disproportionate share of those who secure positions in the US, UK, and increasingly Canada, Australia, and Germany. The IIT-to-Silicon Valley pipeline is the most studied version of this pattern, but it has analogues across engineering, medicine, and the natural sciences.

The "brain drain" framing — which dominated the development economics literature through the 1990s — treats this outflow as a pure loss for India: the human capital that India's public education system produced is captured by foreign economies, and the benefits flow to the destination rather than the origin. The brain drain story has a spatial dimension: the loss is concentrated in the cities and states that invested in the educational institutions from which emigrants depart (Karnataka for IISc, Tamil Nadu for IIT Madras, Maharashtra for IIT Bombay), not in the rural areas from which many of the students originally came.

The "brain circulation" revision — developed by AnnaLee Saxenian (2006) and refined by subsequent researchers — challenges the one-way loss narrative. Saxenian documented how Indian (and Chinese and Taiwanese) engineers in Silicon Valley maintained dense professional and personal networks with their home countries, creating channels through which technology, management practices, and investment capital flowed back. The Indian diaspora in Silicon Valley funded and mentored Indian startups (Flipkart, InMobi, Ola were all founded by engineers who had studied or worked in the US). Indian-born executives who rose to leadership positions in American firms (Satya Nadella at Microsoft, Sundar Pichai at Google, Arvind Krishna at IBM) expanded their companies' Indian operations — creating the GCC investments that now employ hundreds of thousands of workers in Bangalore and Hyderabad.

The spatial economics of brain circulation are bidirectional. The outflow depletes India's talent base in the short run but creates the diaspora network effects that channel foreign investment, technology transfer, and entrepreneurial knowledge back to India in the medium run. The return flow is spatially concentrated in the same cities from which the emigrants departed — Bangalore, Hyderabad, Mumbai, Delhi — because those cities have the institutional infrastructure (venture capital, legal services, talent pools) to absorb returning entrepreneurs and the diaspora investments they bring. The hinterland is bypassed in both directions: it neither produces the emigrants nor receives the return flows.

### The Inflow: India as Education Destination

India is simultaneously becoming an international education destination, though at a scale far smaller than its outbound flows. The IITs and IIMs attract students from Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. The Indian government's "Study in India" program, launched in 2018, aims to increase international student enrollment to 200,000 by 2025. Medical education is a significant draw: India produces approximately 100,000 medical graduates per year, and its private medical colleges attract students from across South Asia and Africa who cannot secure admission to medical programs in their home countries.

The Mode 2 services trade implications (consumption abroad, in GATS terminology) parallel the medical tourism analysis in Chapter 7, but with a longer time horizon and deeper institutional requirements. An international student who spends four years at an IIT is not merely purchasing an educational service — they are being socialized into India's technology ecosystem, building professional networks that will shape their career geography for decades. The student who returns to Nigeria or Bangladesh after studying computer science at IIT Bombay carries with them not just technical skills but institutional knowledge of how India's IT sector operates — knowledge that may facilitate future trade, investment, and collaboration between India and the student's home country. Education is a services export that creates its own future demand.

## 8.4 Telemedicine: Can Digital Delivery Overcome Spatial Concentration?

### The Promise: Apollo and eSanjeevani

India's healthcare system exhibits spatial inequalities that dwarf even the IT-sector concentration described above. Approximately 70 percent of India's qualified doctors practice in urban areas, which contain about 35 percent of the population. The ratio of doctors to population in Kerala (the best-served state) is roughly six times that in Bihar (the worst-served). Rural primary health centers are frequently understaffed, underequipped, and geographically remote from the specialist care available in metropolitan hospitals.

Telemedicine — the delivery of healthcare services through digital telecommunications — represents a test case for whether Mode 1 services trade can reduce these spatial inequalities. If medical consultation can be delivered digitally, then the geographic concentration of doctors in five metropolitan areas need not prevent patients in rural Uttar Pradesh or tribal Odisha from accessing specialist care.

Apollo Telemedicine Networking Foundation (ATNF), established by the Apollo Hospitals Group in 2000, was among the world's earliest large-scale telemedicine networks. By 2023, ATNF operated over 700 telemedicine centers across rural India and in nine countries (including Iraq, Sri Lanka, and several African nations), connecting village health workers to Apollo's specialist physicians in Chennai, Hyderabad, and Delhi via satellite and broadband links. The model addresses the spatial mismatch directly: the specialist stays in the metropolitan hospital; the patient stays in the village; the consultation traverses the distance digitally.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated telemedicine adoption dramatically. India's eSanjeevani platform — a government-developed telemedicine system — conducted over 100 million consultations between its launch in 2020 and 2024, making it one of the world's largest telemedicine deployments. The platform enables both patient-to-doctor teleconsultations (eSanjeevani OPD) and hub-and-spoke referrals between rural health centers and urban hospitals (eSanjeevani AB-HWC).

### The Limits: Why Digital Delivery Does Not Eliminate Geography

The telemedicine evidence complicates the simple "technology kills distance" narrative. While teleconsultation can effectively deliver certain services — dermatology diagnosis from photographs, mental health counseling, chronic disease management through remote monitoring, post-surgical follow-up — it cannot replicate the full range of healthcare services. Surgery, physical examination, emergency care, and complex diagnostics require physical co-presence. The services that can be delivered digitally are, by and large, the lower-value components of the healthcare value chain, while the high-value specialist services remain geographically concentrated.

Moreover, the effective delivery of telemedicine depends on local institutional infrastructure that is itself unequally distributed. A teleconsultation requires a stable internet connection (unavailable in roughly 30 percent of Indian villages), a trained health worker at the patient end who can operate diagnostic equipment and communicate clinical information accurately, a local pharmacy that can dispense prescribed medications, and a referral system that can arrange physical follow-up when needed. These institutional prerequisites create a new kind of distance — not geographic distance but institutional readiness — that determines whether a village can actually benefit from telemedicine services.

The spatial economics of telemedicine thus reproduce, rather than eliminate, the core-periphery pattern. Villages with better infrastructure, more educated health workers, and stronger connections to the formal healthcare system are better positioned to benefit from telemedicine — and these are, by and large, the villages that are already closer (institutionally if not geographically) to urban healthcare networks. Telemedicine extends the reach of urban healthcare systems, but it extends furthest toward communities that are already partially connected.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: India's GDP Revision and Services Measurement**
>
> In 2015, the Central Statistics Office (CSO) revised India's GDP methodology, switching the base year from 2004-05 to 2011-12 and adopting the UN System of National Accounts 2008 framework. The revision produced two contentious changes. First, GDP growth in several recent years was revised substantially upward, generating debate about whether the new methodology was more accurate or politically motivated. Second, the treatment of the "information and communication" sector changed in ways that affect the measurement of IT-BPO output at the state level.
>
> Under the old methodology, the IT-BPO sector was measured primarily through enterprise surveys of large registered firms. Under the new methodology, the CSO incorporated data from the Ministry of Corporate Affairs' MCA-21 database, which captures a broader universe of firms but uses a different sectoral classification. The result is a discontinuity in the time series: pre-2011-12 and post-2011-12 estimates of state-level IT-sector output are not directly comparable. Researchers working with Indian GDP data must decide whether to use the old series (comparable over time but narrower in coverage), the new series (broader coverage but with a structural break), or to construct a spliced series that bridges the methodological change — each choice involving assumptions that affect the spatial pattern of measured IT-BPO activity.

## 8.5 Bangladesh's Manufacturing Track: A Comparator

### Garments, GVCs, and the Spatial Economics of Ready-Made Garments

Bangladesh provides the sharpest available contrast with India's services-led growth path. While India's exports are dominated by digitally delivered services produced in five metropolitan clusters, Bangladesh's exports are dominated by ready-made garments (RMG) — which account for over 80 percent of total merchandise exports — produced in and around Dhaka and Chittagong. The RMG sector employs approximately 4 million workers, predominantly women from rural areas, in a labor-intensive manufacturing process that is the canonical flying-geese pattern: garment production migrated from Korea and Taiwan (as their wages rose) to Bangladesh (where wages were among the lowest in Asia) following the pathway described in Chapter 6.

The spatial economics of Bangladesh's garment sector differ from India's IT sector in ways that illuminate the institutional requirements of each growth path. Garment production requires minimal fixed capital per worker (a sewing machine, a cutting table, basic factory space), creating low barriers to entry that allow production to disperse across a large number of small and medium enterprises. India's IT services require substantial fixed capital per firm (office infrastructure, telecommunications, software licenses) and, more importantly, human capital investments (university education, specialized training, English-language proficiency) that create high barriers to entry and favor spatial concentration.

The welfare implications of the two models diverge. Bangladesh's garment sector, despite its well-documented labor rights problems (the 2013 Rana Plaza disaster killed 1,134 workers and exposed systemic safety failures), has been a powerful vehicle for poverty reduction and female economic empowerment. The women who leave rural villages to work in Dhaka's garment factories earn wages that, while low by international standards, substantially exceed what they could earn in agriculture — and the factory employment provides a degree of economic independence that rural social structures do not. India's IT sector generates much higher value-added per worker but employs far fewer people relative to the population (approximately 5.4 million in IT-BPO, compared to 4 million in Bangladesh's garments from a population roughly ten times larger) and the employment is accessible only to those with university-level education and English proficiency.

### SAARC and the Absence of Regional Integration

South Asia is the world's least integrated major region in terms of intra-regional trade. Intra-SAARC trade accounts for approximately 5 percent of total trade — compared to roughly 25 percent for intra-ASEAN trade, 60 percent for intra-EU trade, and 40 percent for intra-NAFTA trade. The South Asian Free Trade Area (SAFTA), signed in 2004, has had negligible impact on trade flows because its sensitive lists (products excluded from tariff reduction) cover a substantial share of bilateral trade, and because the India-Pakistan political relationship makes meaningful economic integration periodically impossible.

The spatial economic consequences of failed regional integration are particularly severe for services trade. A regionally integrated South Asian services market — where Indian IT professionals could work in Dhaka, Bangladeshi nurses could serve in Indian hospitals, Sri Lankan accountants could practice across SAARC countries — would create opportunities for specialization and scale that the fragmented national markets cannot sustain. Instead, each country's services sector develops in isolation: India exports IT services to the United States and Europe rather than to Pakistan and Bangladesh; Sri Lanka's IT sector (which has genuine capabilities in software engineering and animation) exports to the same distant markets rather than serving the South Asian region.

The absence of a regional services framework also limits the development of cross-border value chains that could benefit smaller South Asian economies. If a Bangalore-based IT firm could seamlessly subcontract routine data processing to a Dhaka-based firm (leveraging Bangladesh's lower labor costs for the routine components while retaining the higher-value work in Bangalore), the result would be a South Asian services value chain analogous to the East Asian manufacturing value chains described in Chapter 6. The institutional barriers — visa restrictions, regulatory incompatibility, lack of mutual recognition of professional qualifications, and the persistent India-Pakistan security tensions that contaminate all SAARC initiatives — prevent this integration from emerging.

### Sri Lanka's Debt Crisis: Services Without Institutional Foundations

Sri Lanka's 2022 sovereign debt crisis — which led to the country's first-ever default, the collapse of the Rajapaksa government, and an IMF bailout — illustrates the risks of a development strategy that relies on services exports (tourism, remittances) without building the institutional foundations for sustained growth. Sri Lanka had developed a genuine IT services sector (concentrated in Colombo), a significant tourism industry, and large remittance inflows from Sri Lankan workers in the Middle East. But the services earnings masked structural vulnerabilities: an overvalued exchange rate sustained by foreign borrowing rather than export competitiveness, a tax base too narrow to fund public services without deficit spending, and an infrastructure investment program (Hambantota port, Mattala airport) financed by Chinese loans that generated debt without generating the returns needed to service it.

The spatial dimension of Sri Lanka's crisis connects to the BRI analysis in Chapter 7. Hambantota port — built with Chinese financing on the southern coast, far from Colombo's commercial center — was a spatial bet that a new trade corridor would develop to justify the investment. When it did not, the port became a fiscal liability that contributed to the sovereign debt spiral. The lesson for services-led development is that services exports can fund imports and sustain consumption, but they cannot substitute for the institutional foundations (fiscal discipline, exchange rate management, productive investment) that sustain growth.

## Institutional Spotlight: Software Technology Parks of India — Institutional Scaffolding for a Services Export Sector

The Software Technology Parks of India (STPI) scheme, launched by the Department of Electronics in 1991, is the institutional origin story of India's IT services revolution. STPI was not a science park in the East Asian mold (like Hsinchu, described in Chapter 6) — it did not fund R&D, build fabrication facilities, or pick technological winners. It was an institutional intermediary that reduced the transaction costs of exporting software services from India, at a time when those costs were prohibitively high for individual firms.

**The problem STPI solved.** In 1991, India's telecommunications infrastructure was among the worst in the world: international telephone calls were routed through government-controlled exchanges with multi-hour wait times, data transfer speeds were negligible by international standards, and the cost of a dedicated international data link exceeded what most software firms could afford individually. Customs procedures for importing computers and peripherals were labyrinthine, with duties and delays that could add months and substantial cost to equipment acquisition. The regulatory environment for foreign exchange — which software exporters needed to receive payments from international clients — was controlled by the Reserve Bank of India through a complex approval process.

**The institutional solution.** STPI provided three services that transformed the economics of software exporting. First, it established shared satellite earth stations and high-speed data links at STPI centers across the country — initially in Pune, Bangalore, Hyderabad, Bhubaneswar, and Thiruvananthapuram — giving individual firms access to international connectivity at shared cost. Second, it provided a single-window clearance for customs, foreign exchange, and regulatory approvals, compressing months of bureaucratic processing into days. Third, it offered duty-free import of hardware for software development under a simple bonding arrangement — firms could import computers without paying customs duties as long as the computers were used for software development or export.

**The spatial effect.** STPI's location decisions shaped the geography of India's IT sector. The first five centers were placed in cities that already had engineering talent but lacked the connectivity infrastructure to export software services. Bangalore's STPI center, which opened in 1991, was the catalyst that transformed the city's existing engineering base into an export-oriented IT cluster. The STPI centers that opened later — in Chennai, Noida, Kolkata, and dozens of smaller cities — followed the same model but with diminishing returns: the agglomeration advantages had already concentrated in the early-mover cities, and later STPI centers could not replicate the ecosystem effects that made Bangalore's center so successful.

**The policy lesson.** STPI demonstrates that institutional interventions can create globally competitive services export sectors in developing countries — but only when the institutional design addresses the binding constraints (connectivity, regulatory burden, customs costs) and is implemented at a moment when latent capabilities (engineering talent, English-language proficiency, proximity to Western time zones) can be activated. The lesson for other developing countries seeking to replicate India's IT success is not to build science parks or offer tax holidays — it is to identify the specific institutional barriers that prevent existing human capital from connecting to global services markets, and to design targeted institutions that remove those barriers.

## Conclusion: Services-Led Development and Its Spatial Limits

This chapter has examined India's IT services revolution as a spatial economic phenomenon — one that creates globally competitive urban clusters while leaving most of the country untouched. The services-led growth path is real and consequential: $200 billion in IT-BPO exports, 5.4 million direct employees, and a diaspora network that channels technology and investment back to India's metropolitan nodes. But it is also spatially exclusive in ways that the manufacturing-led paths described in earlier chapters were not.

The manufacturing-led models of East Asia (Chapter 6), North America (Chapter 4), and even Bangladesh's garment sector (this chapter) created employment that was accessible to workers with limited formal education. Factory jobs required physical dexterity and a willingness to follow routinized procedures — skills that were widely distributed across the population. The services-led model requires university education, English proficiency, and the specific technical skills that global clients demand — qualifications that exclude the vast majority of India's workforce. The spatial corollary is that manufacturing-led growth, while also concentrated in industrial zones, created ripple effects — supplier networks, transportation demand, housing construction, retail services — that spread employment across the urban hierarchy. Services-led growth concentrates employment in a narrow stratum of the labor market located in a narrow set of cities, with limited backward linkages to the surrounding economy.

A potentially more transformative force operates largely outside the export IT sector. The "India Stack" — the layered digital infrastructure comprising Aadhaar (biometric identity covering 1.4 billion people), UPI (unified payments interface processing over ten billion transactions per month by 2024), and Jan Dhan (universal bank accounts) — enables digital services delivery to rural and semi-urban populations that export-oriented IT bypasses entirely. A farmer in Bihar can now access crop insurance, digital payments, and government transfers through the Stack without any interaction with Bangalore's IT cluster. This is a spatial equalization force operating through public institutional infrastructure rather than through market-mediated agglomeration — the opposite of the concentration dynamic that the export IT sector produces. Whether the India Stack's domestic reach ultimately does more for India's spatial equity than the $200 billion export sector does for its aggregate growth is a question the chapter's framework poses but cannot yet resolve.

The telemedicine case (Section 8.4) offers a partial counter-narrative: digital delivery of services can, in principle, extend the benefits of urban concentration to rural populations without requiring those populations to physically migrate. But the evidence shows that effective digital delivery depends on institutional infrastructure — connectivity, trained intermediaries, regulatory frameworks — that is itself unequally distributed. Technology does not automatically overcome spatial inequality; it operates through institutions that may either reinforce or reduce existing patterns.

The brain circulation analysis (Section 8.3) reveals a spatial dynamic that is genuinely novel: the emigration of skilled Indians creates a diaspora network that channels investment and knowledge back to India, but the return flows concentrate in the same metropolitan clusters from which the emigrants departed. The diaspora enriches Bangalore and Hyderabad; it does not develop Bihar or Chhattisgarh.

Chapter 9 shifts the analysis to Europe, where the EU's Single Market represents the most ambitious experiment in regional economic integration ever attempted. The European case inverts the spatial question posed here: instead of asking whether a few globally connected clusters can lift a national economy, it asks whether political integration across sovereign states can create the conditions for convergence across an entire continent. The tools of spatial analysis — convergence regression, gravity models, institutional distance — travel directly from the South Asian context to the European one, but the institutional landscape is entirely different.
