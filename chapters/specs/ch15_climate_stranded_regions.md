## Core Thesis
Climate change operates as a cross-regional spatial force that reshapes the economic map through capital reallocation and labor mobility. This synthesis capstone chapter provides formal modeling of climate-induced capital and labor shifts, drawing on the regional analyses from preceding chapters to construct a unified framework for understanding how climate stress creates stranded regions, redirects investment flows, and forces managed retreat — with adaptive capacity determined by the institutional variables documented throughout the book.

## Key Arguments (3-5)
1. Climate change is not a uniform shock but a spatially differentiated force that creates winners and losers across and within regions. Formal modeling of climate-induced capital shifts (stranded assets in fossil-fuel regions, redirected investment toward climate-resilient locations) and labor shifts (climate migration, managed retreat) requires integrating the regional evidence from preceding chapters into a cross-regional comparative framework.
2. Adaptive capacity is institutionally determined: regions with stronger governance, fiscal capacity, and regulatory frameworks can absorb climate shocks through infrastructure investment, insurance markets, and managed transition — while institutionally fragile regions face compounding climate-conflict-displacement spirals (as documented in the Sahel analysis of Ch. 13).
3. The spatial economics of climate adaptation generate new agglomeration and dispersion dynamics: climate-resilient cities attract capital and talent, while climate-exposed regions face accelerating outflows — a process that interacts with every regional story told in this book.

## Comparative Sidebar — Pacific Islands
SIDS as extreme cases of managed retreat and remittance-dependency. Small Island Developing States (SIDS) in the Pacific — Tuvalu, Kiribati, the Marshall Islands, Fiji — represent the extreme end of climate-induced spatial restructuring, where entire national territories face existential inundation risk. These cases illuminate the economics of managed retreat at sovereign scale: the role of remittance flows as de facto climate insurance, negotiated migration frameworks (Australia's Pacific Engagement Visa, New Zealand's Pacific Access Category), sovereign wealth funds built from fishing license revenues, and the legal and economic implications of statelessness when territory disappears. The Pacific SIDS experience provides a limiting case that clarifies the mechanisms operating at lesser intensity in every other region covered in the book.

## Stranded Regions
Cross-regional comparisons of adaptive capacity. This section compiles a systematic comparison of "stranded regions" across all parts of the book — regions facing structural decline due to climate exposure, resource depletion, or technological obsolescence. Cases include: Appalachian coal counties (Ch. 4), petro-states facing peak demand (Ch. 11 MENA), Sahel displacement corridors (Ch. 13), low-lying coastal megacities (Lagos, Jakarta, Ho Chi Minh City), and Pacific SIDS. The comparison is structured around a common diagnostic framework: climate exposure × institutional adaptive capacity × fiscal buffer × human capital mobility, yielding a typology of stranded-region trajectories from managed transition to compounding decline.

## Global Comparative Spread
A 2-page visual spread compiling all regional diagnostics from the book into a single comparative framework. The spread synthesizes key spatial-institutional indicators across all regions covered: institutional quality, climate exposure, trade openness, agglomeration patterns, and adaptive capacity — allowing readers to compare regional trajectories side by side and identify cross-cutting patterns that individual regional chapters cannot reveal in isolation.

- The "stranded regions" framing risks normative implications about which regions deserve investment — the chapter must be analytically rigorous without being fatalistic.
