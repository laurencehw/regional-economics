## Core Thesis
Regional divergence is fundamentally institutional and evolutionary: regions grow when institutions reduce transaction costs and when local capabilities can recombine into adjacent, higher-value activities. Path dependence is strong, but not immutable.

## Key Arguments (3-5)
1. Institutional quality conditions whether agglomeration economies convert into sustained productivity growth.
2. Historical shocks and settlement patterns generate durable development trajectories through institutional persistence.
3. Related variety and product-space proximity explain feasible upgrading paths better than sector-targeting in unrelated activities.
4. Subnational "varieties of capitalism" shape labor coordination, innovation systems, and firm financing constraints.
5. Informal institutions can either compensate for weak state capacity or entrench extractive equilibria.
