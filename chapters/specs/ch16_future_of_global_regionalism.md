## Core Thesis
The next great spatial restructuring of the global economy will be driven by services trade — specifically by the interaction of three forces: (1) digital platforms that enable "telemigration" of white-collar service work across borders, (2) regulatory fragmentation ("splinternets") that erects new invisible borders in the digital economy, and (3) the post-pandemic spatial equilibrium shock from remote work that is redistributing service-sector employment within and between countries. The chapter synthesizes the services trade threads from all regional sections and deploys the gravity model for services trade as the unifying empirical framework.

## Key Arguments (3-5)
1. **The telemigration hypothesis: will white-collar services follow manufacturing offshore?** Baldwin's (2019) "globotics upheaval" predicts that digital platforms will enable developing-country freelancers to perform service-sector work for developed-country clients at scale — replicating the spatial dynamics of 1990s manufacturing offshoring. The Grossman & Rossi-Hansberg (2008) "trading tasks" framework provides the theoretical scaffolding: which tasks offshore depends on communication costs, routineness, and factor price differences. Evidence from remote work adoption (Althoff et al. 2022) and spatial GE models (Delventhal, Kwon & Parkhomenko 2022) suggests the reshuffling is real but spatially selective — routine cognitive tasks are most vulnerable, while tasks requiring tacit knowledge and face-to-face interaction resist offshoring, consistent with the Storper & Venables (2004) thesis from Chapter 1.
2. **The splinternet: regulatory borders in the service economy.** Data localization laws, digital service taxes, content licensing territories, and professional licensing requirements are creating three competing digital-regulatory regimes — the US (market-led, minimal data regulation), the EU (rights-based, GDPR as the "Brussels Effect"), and China (sovereignty-first, data firewall). These regimes function as invisible trade barriers that are often more restrictive than tariffs for goods (Ferracane 2017). The geography of cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud data center regions) represents the physical footprint of this regulatory fragmentation — and Lab 7's mapping exercise makes this visible.
3. **The gravity model reveals that distance matters *more* for services than for goods — counterintuitively.** Kimura & Lee (2006) and Head, Mayer & Ries (2009) demonstrate that distance elasticities for services trade are often larger than for goods trade, and that language, colonial ties, and regulatory similarity matter much more. This challenges the "death of distance" narrative and implies that the spatial economics of services are more, not less, geographically embedded than goods trade. The OECD STRI quantifies these regulatory barriers; Lab 7 deploys the gravity framework to estimate their tariff-equivalent trade costs.
4. **The spatial equilibrium of "service cities" is being reshaped.** Diamond (2016) models how high-skill service workers sort into expensive cities, driving up local service prices and creating spatial welfare inequality. The post-pandemic remote work shock partially disrupts this equilibrium — Moretti's (2010) local multiplier attenuates if high-skill workers deconcentrate. But the evidence so far (Althoff et al. 2022) suggests selective redistribution rather than wholesale decentralization: secondary cities with quality-of-life amenities and institutional capacity gain, while truly peripheral regions do not.
5. **Education and health as traded services represent enormous but underanalyzed spatial flows.** International students are one of the largest service exports for the US, UK, and Australia (US education exports exceed $40 billion annually). University towns function as service export clusters with deep local multiplier effects — Mode 2 trade with longer duration than tourism. Telemedicine represents a digital-delivery counterpart: the pandemic generated a natural experiment in cross-border health services whose spatial consequences are now measurable (Zeltzer et al. 2023). Both cases illustrate the book's central theme: institutional capacity (visa policy, accreditation, medical licensing) shapes the geography of services trade more than technology alone.

## Telemedicine
Expanding on Zeltzer et al. (2023) — does Mode 1 digital delivery reduce or reinforce geographic concentration of specialized care? The pandemic-era expansion of cross-border telehealth generated a natural experiment in whether digital service delivery flattens the geography of specialist medical care or reinforces concentration at existing centers of excellence. Zeltzer et al. (2023) find that telemedicine adoption increased access for rural and underserved patients but primarily connected them to providers in existing urban specialist hubs — reinforcing rather than dispersing geographic concentration. This section expands the analysis cross-regionally: comparing telemedicine adoption patterns in the US (VA system, Medicare waivers), the EU (cross-border healthcare directive and e-Health Digital Service Infrastructure), MENA (Gulf specialist centers serving the wider region, building on Ch. 11's services hub analysis), and Sub-Saharan Africa (where mobile health platforms operate in institutional environments with weak regulatory frameworks for cross-border practice). The core question for services trade geography is whether Mode 1 digital delivery is a substitute for Mode 2 (patient travel) and Mode 4 (physician migration) or a complement that strengthens existing hub-and-spoke patterns.

## Global Comparative Spread
Master matrix comparing services frameworks across USMCA, EU Single Market, AfCFTA, CPTPP, RCEP. This 2-page visual spread provides a systematic side-by-side comparison of how the major regional trade agreements covered in the book govern services trade. Columns: listing approach (positive vs. negative list), Mode 1-4 coverage (which modes are liberalized and which are carved out), e-commerce and data provisions (data localization, source code disclosure, digital services taxes), mutual recognition agreements (MRAs for professional qualifications), and dispute settlement mechanisms (investor-state, state-state, or sectoral). The matrix makes visible the regulatory fragmentation that the "splinternet" argument describes — showing readers where agreements converge (e.g., negative listing in CPTPP and USMCA), where they diverge (e.g., EU's horizontal approach vs. AfCFTA's progressive liberalization), and where gaps remain (e.g., Mode 4 restrictions across all agreements). This comparative spread serves as both a reference tool and an analytical instrument for the chapter's argument about regulatory fragmentation as the binding constraint on services trade.

## Open Questions/Risks
- BOP-based services trade data quality is uneven: Mode 1 (cross-border) and Mode 2 (travel) are reasonably well-captured, but Mode 3 (commercial presence/FDI) is severely undercounted. The chapter must be transparent about measurement limitations.
- The telemigration hypothesis is forward-looking — current data show accelerating trends but cannot confirm a manufacturing-offshoring-scale restructuring. The chapter should distinguish established empirical patterns from informed extrapolation.
- The gravity model for services requires careful handling of zeros (many bilateral services trade flows are zero or unreported); PPML estimation or Heckman selection models may be needed.
- Cloud infrastructure data is commercially compiled and may not be consistently available across all providers.
- Remote work data is primarily available for the US; cross-country comparability is limited.
