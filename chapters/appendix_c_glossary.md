# Appendix C: Glossary of Key Terms

---

## Spatial Economics and Economic Geography

**Agglomeration economies.** The benefits that firms and workers derive from locating near each other. Includes labor market pooling, input sharing, and knowledge spillovers (Marshallian externalities). Ch. 1.

**Backward linkages.** Demand-side connections: a firm's demand for intermediate inputs creates markets for upstream suppliers, encouraging them to locate nearby. Part of the NEG circular causation mechanism. Ch. 1.

**Comparative advantage.** The principle, originating with Ricardo, that countries benefit from trade by specializing in goods they produce at lower relative (opportunity) cost. In regional economics, comparative advantage interacts with agglomeration forces: regions may specialize not because of innate cost differences but because increasing returns lock in early patterns of specialization. The concept underpins the Heckscher-Ohlin framework and contrasts with the NEG emphasis on increasing returns as a driver of trade patterns. Ch. 1, Ch. 3-B.

**Core-periphery model.** Krugman's (1991) model demonstrating that the interaction of increasing returns, transport costs, and labor mobility can produce spatial concentration of manufacturing in a "core" region, with the "periphery" supplying agricultural goods. Ch. 1.

**Dispersion forces.** Economic forces that spread activity across space: immobile factors (land, natural resources), congestion costs (housing prices, commuting times), and competition effects. Offset agglomeration forces. Ch. 1.

**First nature geography.** Physical characteristics of locations (climate, coastlines, natural resources, navigable rivers) that are exogenous to economic activity. Contrasted with second nature geography. Ch. 1.

**Firm heterogeneity (Melitz model).** The Melitz (2003) framework in which firms within an industry differ in productivity. Trade liberalization induces selection: only the most productive firms can bear the fixed costs of exporting, while the least productive firms exit. The resulting reallocation of market share toward high-productivity firms raises aggregate industry productivity -- a gain from trade absent in representative-firm models. Ch. 1, Ch. 3-B.

**Forward linkages.** Supply-side connections: a firm's production of intermediate inputs makes downstream firms more productive, encouraging them to locate nearby. Ch. 1.

**Home market effect.** The prediction that, in the presence of increasing returns and trade costs, countries with larger domestic demand for a good will be net exporters of that good. Opposite of the comparative-advantage prediction. Ch. 1, Ch. 3-B.

**Iceberg transport costs.** A modeling convention where a fraction of the good "melts" during transport — if $$\tau > 1$$ units must be shipped for 1 unit to arrive, the transport cost is $$(\tau - 1)/\tau$$. Simplifies general equilibrium analysis by avoiding a separate transport sector. Ch. 1, Ch. 3-B.

**Increasing returns to scale.** A production technology in which doubling all inputs more than doubles output. Internal increasing returns (at the firm level) generate imperfect competition and are central to the NEG and new trade theory. External increasing returns (at the industry or city level) arise from agglomeration externalities and can coexist with perfect competition among individual firms. Ch. 1.

**Industrial cluster.** A geographic concentration of interconnected firms, specialized suppliers, service providers, and associated institutions in a particular industry. Porter (1990) popularized the concept as a framework for regional competitive advantage. Clusters generate Marshallian externalities -- labor pooling, input sharing, and knowledge spillovers -- and can be self-reinforcing through path dependency. Ch. 1, Ch. 2.

**Knowledge spillovers.** The uncompensated transfer of knowledge between firms or workers through informal interaction, labor mobility, and observation. The third Marshallian externality. Ch. 1.

**Labor market pooling.** The benefit to firms and workers from locating in areas with many employers and employees in the same industry, reducing matching frictions and insurance costs. The first Marshallian externality. Ch. 1.

**MAUP (Modifiable Areal Unit Problem).** The sensitivity of spatial statistical results to the geographic boundaries used to define spatial units. Results can change depending on whether data are aggregated to states, provinces, counties, or grid cells. Ch. 3-A.

**Marshallian externalities.** The three mechanisms by which geographic concentration of economic activity generates productivity advantages: labor market pooling, input sharing (backward and forward linkages among specialized suppliers), and knowledge spillovers. Named after Alfred Marshall, who identified them in *Principles of Economics* (1890). These externalities are the micro-foundations of agglomeration economies and remain the organizing framework for empirical work on clusters and cities. Ch. 1, Ch. 2.

**Moran's I.** A measure of spatial autocorrelation: the correlation between a variable's values at neighboring locations. Positive I indicates clustering (similar values near each other); negative I indicates dispersion. Ch. 3-A, Lab 6.

**New Economic Geography (NEG).** The theoretical framework, pioneered by Krugman (1991), that uses general equilibrium models with increasing returns, transport costs, and factor mobility to explain the spatial distribution of economic activity. Ch. 1.

**Second nature geography.** Spatial patterns that arise endogenously from economic interactions (agglomeration, market access, infrastructure networks) rather than from physical geography. Ch. 1.

**Spatial autocorrelation.** The tendency for values of a variable at nearby locations to be correlated — positive (similar values cluster) or negative (dissimilar values cluster). Ch. 3-A.

**Spatial autoregressive model (SAR).** A regression model where the dependent variable in each unit depends on a weighted average of the dependent variable in neighboring units: $$y = \rho W y + X\beta + \varepsilon$$. Ch. 3-A, Lab 1, Appendix A.

**Spatial Durbin Model (SDM).** A regression model that includes both a spatial lag of the dependent variable and spatial lags of the regressors: $$y = \rho W y + X\beta + WX\theta + \varepsilon$$. Ch. 3-A, Appendix A.

**Spatial error model (SEM).** A regression model where spatial dependence enters through the error term: $$y = X\beta + u$$, $$u = \lambda W u + \varepsilon$$. Captures spatially correlated unobservables. Ch. 3-A, Appendix A.

**Spatial sorting.** The tendency for workers and firms of different productivity levels to sort across locations, with the most productive agents selecting into the largest and most expensive cities. Spatial sorting amplifies observed urban productivity premia beyond the causal effect of agglomeration: cities appear more productive partly because they attract more productive workers. Combes, Duranton, and Gobillon (2008) developed methods to separate sorting from true agglomeration effects. Ch. 1, Ch. 3-A.

**Spatial weight matrix ($$\mathbf{W}$$).** A matrix encoding the spatial relationships between units. Common constructions: binary contiguity, inverse distance, $$k$$-nearest neighbors. Usually row-standardized. Ch. 3-A, Appendix A.

## Trade and Services Economics

**CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism).** EU policy applying a carbon price to imports of carbon-intensive goods (steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers) to prevent carbon leakage. Ch. 15.

**Elasticity of substitution.** A parameter measuring how easily consumers or producers substitute between different varieties, inputs, or trading partners in response to relative price changes. In the CES gravity model, the elasticity of substitution $$\sigma$$ governs how sensitive trade flows are to trade costs: higher $$\sigma$$ means varieties are more substitutable and bilateral trade costs have a larger effect on trade patterns. Typical estimates for goods trade range from 3 to 10. Ch. 3-B, Appendix A.

**Factor price equalization.** The Heckscher-Ohlin prediction that free trade in goods equalizes factor prices (wages, rents) across countries, even without factor mobility. Ch. 16 discusses whether telemigration could produce a services analogue.

**GATS (General Agreement on Trade in Services).** The WTO agreement governing international services trade, defining four modes of supply. Ch. 2.

**Gravity model.** The workhorse empirical model of bilateral trade: trade between two countries is proportional to their economic sizes and inversely related to the trade costs between them. Ch. 3-B, Lab 7, Appendix A.

**Intra-industry trade.** Two-way trade in products within the same industry classification -- for example, Germany exporting BMWs to France while importing Renaults. Grubel and Lloyd (1975) documented its prevalence. Intra-industry trade is driven by product differentiation and increasing returns rather than comparative advantage, and its share tends to be highest among countries with similar income levels and factor endowments. Ch. 1, Ch. 3-B.

**Mode 1 (Cross-border supply).** Services trade where the service crosses the border but neither provider nor consumer moves. Examples: IT outsourcing, financial data processing, telemedicine. Ch. 2.

**Mode 2 (Consumption abroad).** Services trade where the consumer moves to the provider's country. Examples: tourism, medical tourism, international education. Ch. 2.

**Mode 3 (Commercial presence).** Services trade through foreign direct investment — the provider establishes a subsidiary in the consumer's country. Examples: foreign bank branches, hotel chains, law firm offices. Ch. 2.

**Mode 4 (Presence of natural persons).** Services trade where individual service providers temporarily move to the consumer's country. Examples: consulting engagements, construction workers, nurses. Ch. 2.

**Multilateral resistance.** The Anderson and van Wincoop (2003) insight that bilateral trade depends not only on bilateral trade costs but on each country's trade costs with all other partners. A country that is remote from all partners faces high multilateral resistance, which makes any given bilateral trade cost less deterrent. Ch. 3-B, Appendix A.

**Negative list.** A listing approach in trade agreements where all sectors are liberalized unless specifically excluded. More liberalizing than positive list. Used by USMCA, CPTPP. Ch. 2, Ch. 16.

**Positive list.** A listing approach where only sectors explicitly named are liberalized. More restrictive than negative list. Used by GATS, RCEP, AfCFTA. Ch. 2, Ch. 16.

**PPML (Poisson Pseudo-Maximum Likelihood).** The preferred estimator for gravity models, consistent under heteroskedasticity and able to handle zero trade flows. Santos Silva and Tenreyro (2006). Ch. 3-B, Lab 7, Appendix A.

**MRA (Mutual Recognition Agreement).** An agreement between jurisdictions to recognize each other's regulatory standards, professional qualifications, or conformity assessments. Reduces Mode 1 and Mode 4 barriers. Ch. 2, Ch. 9.

**Rules of origin.** Regulations in preferential trade agreements that define the conditions under which a product qualifies for tariff preferences. Typically require a minimum share of domestic or regional value-added, a change in tariff classification, or specific processing requirements. Restrictive rules of origin can undermine the liberalizing intent of trade agreements by raising compliance costs and limiting the use of imported intermediates, particularly in sectors with fragmented global value chains. Ch. 4, Ch. 15.

**Servicification.** The increasing share of services value-added embodied in manufacturing exports. Measured using TiVA data. Ch. 3-B, Ch. 6.

**STRI (Services Trade Restrictiveness Index).** OECD index measuring regulatory barriers to services trade across sectors and countries, on a 0–1 scale. Ch. 2, Lab 7.

**Task trading.** The Grossman and Rossi-Hansberg (2008) framework where production is decomposed into discrete tasks that can be performed in different locations, with offshoring determined by task-level communication and trade costs. Ch. 3-B, Ch. 8.

**Trade creation / trade diversion.** Viner's (1950) distinction between the two effects of preferential trade agreements. Trade creation occurs when a tariff reduction shifts production from a high-cost domestic source to a lower-cost partner, increasing welfare. Trade diversion occurs when preferences shift imports from a low-cost non-member to a higher-cost member, reducing welfare. The net welfare effect of any agreement depends on the balance between these two forces. Ch. 4, Ch. 9, Ch. 15.

**TiVA (Trade in Value Added).** OECD database decomposing gross trade flows into domestic and foreign value-added components. Reveals the services content embedded in goods exports. Ch. 3-B, Lab 2.

## Institutional Economics

**Institutional thickness.** The density and quality of organizations, norms, and inter-organizational relationships in a region. Amin and Thrift (1995). High institutional thickness supports economic coordination, knowledge transfer, and collective action. Ch. 2.

**Inclusive institutions.** Political and economic institutions that distribute power broadly, provide secure property rights, enforce the rule of law, and permit creative destruction. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) argued that inclusive institutions are essential for sustained economic growth because they incentivize investment, innovation, and broad-based participation in economic life. The concept provides a counterpoint to geography-based explanations of development. Ch. 2, Ch. 13.

**Middle-income trap.** The empirical regularity that many countries experience rapid growth at low income levels but stall at middle-income levels, failing to converge with high-income economies. Explanations emphasize the exhaustion of easy productivity gains from structural transformation, the difficulty of transitioning from imitation to innovation, institutional weaknesses, and the erosion of low-wage competitive advantages before high-skill advantages are established. Ch. 6, Ch. 8, Ch. 13.

**Path dependency.** The tendency for historical events and decisions to constrain future institutional development, even when the original rationale no longer applies. Ch. 2.

**Related variety.** The hypothesis (Frenken, Van Oort, and Verburg 2007) that regional economic growth is driven by the presence of industries that are different enough to learn from each other but similar enough to communicate effectively. Ch. 2.

**Rentier state.** A state that derives a substantial share of its revenue from external rents (resource royalties, transit fees, foreign aid) rather than from domestic taxation, weakening the institutional feedback loop between taxation and representation. Beblawi and Luciani (1987). Ch. 11.

**Resource curse.** The paradox that countries rich in natural resources tend to have slower economic growth, weaker institutions, and less economic diversification than resource-poor countries. Ch. 11.

**Smart Specialization (S3).** The EU's framework for regional innovation policy, requiring regions to identify their competitive advantages through an Entrepreneurial Discovery Process (EDP) and concentrate public investment on domains where they can achieve excellence. Ch. 9.

**Special Economic Zone (SEZ).** A geographically delimited area within a country that operates under different regulatory, tax, and trade rules than the national territory. SEZs aim to attract investment and promote exports by offering streamlined customs procedures, tax incentives, and sometimes more flexible labor regulations. China's coastal SEZs (Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shantou, Xiamen) established in 1980 are the most cited success case; outcomes elsewhere have been mixed, with many zones failing to generate spillovers to the broader economy. Ch. 6, Ch. 7, Ch. 11.

**Varieties of Capitalism (VoC).** The Hall and Soskice (2001) framework distinguishing Liberal Market Economies (LMEs) from Coordinated Market Economies (CMEs) based on their institutional arrangements for corporate governance, labor relations, education, and inter-firm coordination. Ch. 2, Ch. 9.

**Consumption city.** An urban area whose economic base is primarily services, retail, and informal employment rather than manufacturing or tradeable production. Used in Chapter 13 to describe the dominant form of Sub-Saharan African urbanization, where density generates congestion and informality without the agglomeration gains predicted by models that assume manufacturing-led growth. Ch. 13.

**Developmental state.** A state that systematically intervenes in the economy to promote industrialization and structural transformation, using tools such as directed credit, trade protection for infant industries, public investment in infrastructure and education, and coordination of private-sector activity. The concept, developed by Johnson (1982) with reference to Japan's MITI, has been applied to South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and more recently to China and Ethiopia. Ch. 6, Ch. 7, Ch. 13.

**Extractive institutions.** Political and economic institutions that concentrate power and wealth in the hands of a narrow elite, failing to provide secure property rights, enforce contracts, or create a level playing field for broad-based participation. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) argued that extractive institutions are the fundamental cause of cross-country income differences, as they discourage investment, innovation, and creative destruction. Contrasted with inclusive institutions. Ch. 2, Ch. 13.

**Dutch disease.** The phenomenon whereby a natural resource boom (oil, gas, minerals) raises the real exchange rate, making non-resource tradeable sectors (manufacturing, agriculture) less competitive. Named after the Netherlands' experience following North Sea gas discoveries. A key mechanism in the resource curse. Ch. 5, Ch. 11.

**Premature deindustrialization.** The decline of manufacturing's share of GDP and employment at income levels well below those at which advanced economies deindustrialized. Rodrik (2016) documented this pattern across developing countries, suggesting that the traditional manufacturing-led growth path may be closing for late industrializers. Ch. 5, Ch. 13.

**Telemigration.** Baldwin's (2019) term for the remote provision of services across borders by workers who remain in their home country — a form of Mode 1 services trade that replicates some features of labor migration without physical movement. The spatial implications are potentially transformative: if services tasks can be performed remotely at scale, the geographic distribution of services employment could shift dramatically toward lower-cost locations. Ch. 16.

## Econometric Methods

**Event study.** An econometric design that estimates dynamic treatment effects by interacting treatment indicators with a set of leads and lags relative to the treatment date. The resulting coefficient plot shows how outcomes evolve before and after treatment, with the pre-treatment coefficients serving as a test of parallel trends. Widely used in conjunction with difference-in-differences to assess anticipation effects and the time path of treatment impacts. Ch. 3-A, Lab 5.

**Fixed effects.** A panel data estimation strategy that controls for all time-invariant characteristics of each unit (country, region, firm) by including unit-specific intercepts, effectively using only within-unit variation over time to identify coefficients. In the gravity model context, exporter and importer fixed effects absorb the multilateral resistance terms, enabling consistent estimation of bilateral trade cost parameters without solving the structural model. Ch. 3-A, Ch. 3-B, Appendix A.

**Half-life of convergence.** The number of years required for a region to close half of its initial income gap with the steady-state level. Computed as $$t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / b$$, where $$b$$ is the implied convergence speed from a $$\beta$$-convergence regression. Ch. 3-A, Lab 2, Appendix A.

**Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI).** A concentration measure computed as the sum of squared market (or employment) shares: $$\text{HHI} = \sum_i s_i^2$$. Ranges from $$1/n$$ (perfect equality) to 1 (complete concentration). Used in Lab 3 to measure IT services concentration across Indian states. Ch. 3-A, Lab 3.

**IRLS (Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares).** The numerical algorithm used to estimate PPML gravity models. At each iteration, observations are reweighted by the ratio of observed to predicted values, and OLS is re-estimated on the reweighted data until convergence. Lab 7, Appendix A.

**Instrumental variable (IV).** A variable used to address endogeneity in regression analysis. A valid instrument must be correlated with the endogenous regressor (relevance) and affect the outcome only through that regressor (exclusion restriction). In spatial economics, common instruments include historical variables (colonial-era railroad networks, 19th-century population), Bartik shift-share constructs, and geographic features. Two-stage least squares (2SLS) is the standard IV estimator. Ch. 3-A.

**Kernel function.** A weighting function used in local regression and RDD estimation to give more weight to observations near a target point. Common kernels include triangular (linear decay) and uniform (equal weight within bandwidth). Lab 4, Appendix A.

**Location Quotient (LQ).** The ratio of a region's share of employment (or output) in an industry to the national share. LQ > 1 indicates regional specialization; LQ < 1 indicates under-representation. Used in Lab 3 to measure IT services specialization across Indian states. Ch. 3-A, Lab 3.

**Night-lights data.** Satellite-measured radiance data (typically from the VIIRS instrument) used as a proxy for economic activity, especially in contexts where official statistics are unreliable or unavailable. Henderson, Storeygard, and Weil (2012) validated the approach. Ch. 13, Lab 6.

**Bartik instrument.** A shift-share instrument that predicts local employment growth by interacting the local industry composition (shares) with national industry growth rates (shifts). Used to address endogeneity in spatial economic analysis. Ch. 3-A, Lab 1.

**$$\beta$$-convergence.** The empirical finding that initially poorer regions grow faster than initially richer regions, conditional on other determinants of growth. Ch. 3-A, Appendix A.

**Clustering (standard errors).** The practice of computing standard errors that allow for arbitrary correlation of residuals within defined groups (clusters), such as countries, regions, or time periods. Clustering is essential when observations within a group share common shocks or are otherwise not independent -- for example, regions within the same country facing correlated policy environments. Failure to cluster when appropriate leads to understated standard errors and inflated t-statistics. Cameron and Miller (2015) provide a practical guide. Ch. 3-A, Ch. 3-B.

**Difference-in-differences (DiD).** A quasi-experimental method that estimates causal effects by comparing the change in outcomes over time between a treated group and a control group. Ch. 3-A.

**Permutation inference.** A non-parametric approach to hypothesis testing where the reference distribution is constructed by randomly permuting the data. Used for Moran's I inference in Lab 6.

**Placebo test.** A falsification exercise that applies the empirical method to a setting where no treatment effect should exist, in order to assess the credibility of the research design. In the SCM context (Lab 5), in-space placebos apply the synthetic control procedure to each untreated donor unit; if many placebos show gaps as large as the treated unit, the estimated effect is not credible. In RDD, placebo tests check for discontinuities at non-treatment thresholds. Ch. 3-A, Lab 4, Lab 5.

**Regression discontinuity design (RDD).** A quasi-experimental method that estimates causal effects by comparing units just above and just below a threshold that determines treatment assignment. Spatial RDD uses geographic boundaries. Ch. 9, Lab 4.

**$$\sigma$$-convergence.** A declining cross-sectional dispersion of income levels over time. Necessary condition: $$\beta$$-convergence. Ch. 3-A, Appendix A.

**Synthetic control method (SCM).** A method for estimating causal effects of events or policies by constructing a weighted combination of untreated units (the "synthetic control") that matches the treated unit's pre-treatment trajectory. Abadie and Gardeazabal (2003). Ch. 12, Lab 5.
