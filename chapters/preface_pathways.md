# Pathways Through This Book

---

This book is designed to be read in multiple ways. The full sequence — from the micro-foundations of spatial economics through the synthesis chapters on climate and the future of regionalism — constitutes a two-semester graduate course. But few instructors will assign the entire text, and few students will read it cover to cover. The five pathways below are curated tracks through the book, each designed for a one-semester course with a coherent intellectual arc.

Each pathway specifies a set of core chapters (required reading), elective chapters (choose some or all), and applied labs. The dependency diagram below shows which chapters build on which, so instructors can verify that any custom sequence respects the analytical prerequisites.

**Reading rule:** Before reading any chapter in a lower row, read the chapter(s) it connects to in upper rows. Chapter 1 is prerequisite for everything. Chapters 2, 3-A, and 3-B can be read in any order. Regional chapters (4–14) require the relevant foundation chapter(s) from Part I. The synthesis chapters (15–16) benefit from all prior material but can be read after any subset of regional chapters.

## Notes for Instructors

**Selecting a pathway.** The pathways are designed to be self-contained: each provides enough theoretical foundation (from Part I) to support the regional chapters it includes. Students who have taken a prior course in international trade can skip Ch. 3-B; students with spatial econometrics background can skim Ch. 3-A. The elective chapters allow instructors to add depth in areas that match their expertise or their students' interests.

**Combining pathways.** A two-semester sequence might combine Pathway 1 (fall) with Pathway 2 (spring), or Pathway 3 (fall) with Pathway 4 (spring). In a two-semester sequence, Part I need only be taught once.

**Lab flexibility.** Each lab has a Minimum Viable Version (2–3 hours, guided replication) and an Extended Version (6–10 hours, independent analysis). Courses with a quantitative focus should assign 4–5 labs; courses with a reading-and-discussion focus might assign 1–2 labs as optional extensions.

**Assessment ideas.** Each chapter ends with 6 discussion questions that can serve as essay prompts. The labs produce replicable outputs (model summaries, datasets, visualizations) that can be submitted as empirical assignments. A capstone option: students choose a region not covered in their pathway and produce a "Regional Diagnostic" modeled on the book's template.
