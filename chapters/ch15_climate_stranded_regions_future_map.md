# Chapter 15: Climate, Stranded Regions, and the Future Map

## Introduction: The Great Spatial Reallocation

In July 2023, Phoenix, Arizona, recorded 31 consecutive days with temperatures exceeding 110 degrees Fahrenheit. Emergency rooms treated hundreds of patients for heat-related illness; the Maricopa County medical examiner eventually attributed over 600 deaths to heat exposure across the calendar year (Maricopa County Department of Public Health 2024). Phoenix is a city of five million that built its postwar economic model on cheap air-conditioned sprawl — an implicit bet that technology could permanently override the Sonoran Desert's climate. For half a century, that bet paid off: Phoenix attracted retirees, semiconductor fabrication plants, and data centers with abundant land, low taxes, and sunshine. Now the city confronts the limits of the model. Outdoor construction workers — the people who build the sprawl — cannot safely work during afternoon hours for weeks at a stretch. Electricity demand for cooling strains a grid that depends partly on hydroelectric power from a shrinking Lake Mead. The spatial economics of habitability, which economists once treated as a fixed background condition, have become the binding constraint on a metropolitan economy.

Phoenix is not exceptional. It is exemplary. Every regional chapter in this book contains a climate section, because the climate transition is reshaping economic geography in every region we have studied. The IRA's spatial consequences and agricultural water stress in North America (Chapter 4); lithium extraction, green hydrogen, and glacier-fed water systems in Latin America (Chapter 5); coal dependency, green transition, and demographic compounding in East Asia (Chapter 6); BRI climate infrastructure and typhoon exposure across Southeast Asia (Chapter 7); monsoon variability, extreme heat stress, and Himalayan glacier melt in South Asia (Chapter 8); Mediterranean heat stress, North Sea storm exposure, and the EU's Green Deal architecture in Western Europe (Chapter 9); post-industrial energy transition in Central and Eastern Europe (Chapter 10); extreme heat, water scarcity, and petro-state diversification in the Gulf (Chapter 11); the climate-conflict-displacement spiral in MENA's fragile states (Chapter 12); Sahel desertification and the southward march of the arid zone in West Africa (Chapter 13); flood-risk corridors, coastal exposure, and the Lake Victoria basin in East Africa (Chapter 14). These sections documented climate change as a spatial economic phenomenon: one that redistributes comparative advantage across regions, creates new winners and losers, and transforms the institutional landscape through which economic activity is organized. This chapter synthesizes those regional analyses into a cross-regional framework for understanding the spatial economics of the climate transition.

The scale of the required response is staggering. The International Energy Agency estimates that global clean energy investment must reach roughly four trillion dollars per year by 2030 to keep the world on a path consistent with net-zero emissions by 2050 (IEA 2023). That figure — equivalent to about four percent of global GDP — implies an investment reallocation of a magnitude not seen since the post-World War II reconstruction. And unlike the Marshall Plan, which concentrated resources in a single region, the climate investment surge must be globally distributed while simultaneously correcting for the spatial mismatch between where emissions are produced, where climate damages are concentrated, and where investment capital is available.

The central argument of this chapter is that climate change is not primarily a temperature problem or a sea-level problem — it is a spatial reallocation problem. The physical effects of climate change (heat, drought, flooding, sea-level rise, storms) make some locations less productive and less habitable, while making other locations relatively more attractive. The policy responses to climate change (carbon pricing, green subsidies, renewable energy mandates, carbon border adjustments) create new patterns of comparative advantage that reward some regions and penalize others. And the technological responses (electric vehicles, green hydrogen, carbon capture, precision agriculture) concentrate innovation and investment in specific places, generating the agglomeration dynamics that Chapter 1 described. The net effect is a spatial reallocation of economic activity — capital, labor, and institutional capacity — that will reshape the global economic geography over the coming decades more profoundly than any force since the industrial revolution.

Section 15.1 develops a formal framework for analyzing climate-induced spatial reallocation — the movement of capital and labor in response to changing locational advantages. Section 15.2 examines Pacific Island states as extreme cases of climate-driven managed retreat. Section 15.3 identifies "stranded regions" — places whose economic base is threatened by the climate transition — and compares their adaptive capacity across the regions studied in earlier chapters. Section 15.4 analyzes green industrial policy as spatial policy: how carbon border adjustments, green subsidies, and renewable energy mandates redistribute economic activity across regions. Section 15.5 presents a global comparative spread that synthesizes the regional diagnostics from all preceding chapters into a single framework. Chapter 16 then picks up the story by examining how the climate transition interacts with two other transformative forces — the telemigration of services and the fragmentation of the digital commons — to shape the economic geography of the coming decades.

## 15.1 Modeling Climate-Induced Spatial Reallocation

### The Spatial Economics of Changing Locational Advantage

Climate change alters the fundamental parameters of locational advantage that spatial economic theory takes as given. The "first nature" geography that classical location theory treats as fixed — the climate that determines agricultural productivity, the coastlines that define port access, the water resources that sustain urban populations — is being modified by physical climate change on timescales that are fast relative to the infrastructure and institutions that economic activity depends on.

The spatial reallocation framework extends the NEG models of Chapter 1 to incorporate time-varying locational fundamentals. In the standard NEG framework, the spatial equilibrium is determined by the interaction of agglomeration forces (which concentrate activity) and dispersion forces (which spread it out), given fixed locational characteristics. Climate change introduces a third force: locational degradation (or enhancement) that shifts the spatial equilibrium by changing the productivity of specific locations. A city in the Persian Gulf that becomes uninhabitable during summer months without air conditioning loses locational advantage — and as Chapter 11 documented, wet-bulb temperatures in the Gulf are already approaching the physiological limits of human survival during summer months. A port city in the Arctic that becomes ice-free for longer periods gains it: Murmansk and Tromsoe are already positioning for expanded Northern Sea Route traffic. Agricultural regions in southern Europe that lose rainfall shift from productive to marginal, as the desertification of Spain's Murcia region and the salinization of the Po Delta illustrate (Chapter 9). Agricultural regions in Scandinavia and Canada that gain growing-season days shift from marginal to productive, though the soils and infrastructure for large-scale agriculture take decades to develop.

The reallocation operates through three channels:

1. **Capital reallocation.** Investment flows toward locations with improving climate fundamentals and away from locations with deteriorating fundamentals. The $500 billion global annual investment in renewable energy is spatially concentrated in locations with the best solar and wind resources — the Gulf, the Sahara fringe, Patagonia, the Great Plains, offshore Northern Europe — creating new industrial clusters that did not exist twenty years ago. On the disinvestment side, the scale of potentially stranded assets is enormous: Carbon Tracker (2022) estimates that between one and four trillion dollars of fossil fuel assets could become stranded under scenarios consistent with the Paris Agreement's temperature targets. These stranded assets are not evenly distributed — they are concentrated in specific extraction zones and carbon-intensive manufacturing clusters, meaning that capital destruction is as spatially concentrated as capital creation. Meanwhile, green foreign direct investment is generating new geographies of production: China produces roughly 60 percent of the world's solar panels, overwhelmingly in Xinjiang and Jiangsu provinces; the North Sea has emerged as Europe's premier offshore wind cluster, anchored by Denmark, the UK, and the Netherlands; and the Gulf states are pivoting from fossil fuel exporters to green hydrogen producers, with Saudi Arabia's NEOM project alone targeting 1.2 million tonnes of annual green hydrogen output by 2030 (Chapter 11).

2. **Labor reallocation.** Population movements in response to climate stress are already substantial and will accelerate. The World Bank's Groundswell report (Rigaud et al. 2018, updated 2021) provides the most systematic projections, estimating that by 2050, climate change could displace 216 million people within their own countries — 86 million in Sub-Saharan Africa, 49 million in East Asia and the Pacific, 40 million in South Asia, 19 million in North Africa, 17 million in Latin America, and 5 million in Eastern Europe and Central Asia. These estimates cover only *internal* migration; cross-border climate displacement could be substantially larger but is harder to model because it depends on immigration policy regimes that are politically determined. The geography of vulnerability is sharply concentrated: Bangladesh alone faces the prospect of 20 million people displaced by a one-meter sea-level rise, with the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta's dense rural population pushed toward Dhaka and Chittagong (Chapter 8). The climate-conflict-displacement spiral described in Chapter 12 — where climate stress degrades livelihoods, fueling recruitment into armed groups, which triggers displacement, which strains host-region institutions — compounds these numbers in the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, and the Levant. This internal climate migration follows the same gravity model patterns as economic migration: people move from locations with deteriorating conditions to the nearest locations with better conditions, subject to distance costs, institutional barriers, and social networks.

3. **Institutional reallocation.** Climate adaptation requires institutional capacity — the ability to plan, finance, and implement infrastructure investments, regulatory changes, and social protection programs. This capacity is unequally distributed (as every chapter in this book has documented), and the climate transition will further concentrate institutional capacity in places that already have it, while straining it in places that don't. The climate finance architecture illustrates the gap. At the Copenhagen COP in 2009, developed countries pledged to mobilize $100 billion per year in climate finance for developing countries by 2020. Actual delivery reached approximately $83 billion by 2020, with much of it in the form of loans rather than grants, and with adaptation funding consistently underfunded relative to mitigation (OECD 2022). The loss and damage fund agreed at COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh (2022) represented a conceptual breakthrough — developed countries accepting financial responsibility for climate damages in vulnerable nations — but its initial capitalization of roughly $700 million was trivial relative to the estimated annual loss and damage costs of $290–580 billion in developing countries by 2030 (Markandya and Gonzalez-Eguino 2019). National Adaptation Plans (NAPs), which are supposed to guide country-level adaptation investment, remain incomplete for many of the most vulnerable countries, precisely because the institutional capacity needed to write a NAP — technical expertise in climate modeling, cost-benefit analysis, public financial management, stakeholder consultation — is the same capacity needed to implement one. As of 2024, fewer than half of LDCs had submitted complete NAPs to the UNFCCC, and many of those submitted were aspirational documents rather than implementable investment programs.

### Formal Framework: Climate Productivity Shocks in a Spatial GE Model

The recent spatial general equilibrium literature — Desmet and Rossi-Hansberg (2015), Conte, Desmet, Nagy, and Rossi-Hansberg (2021), Cruz and Rossi-Hansberg (2024) — has begun to incorporate climate change into quantitative spatial models. These models divide the world's land surface into a fine grid of locations, assign each location a set of economic fundamentals (productivity, amenities, trade costs to other locations), and solve for the spatial equilibrium distribution of population and economic activity.

Climate change enters these models as a shock to location-specific productivity and amenities. The damage functions typically draw on Burke, Hsiang, and Miguel (2015), who estimate a non-linear relationship between temperature and economic output: productivity peaks at roughly 13 degrees Celsius annual mean temperature and declines steeply at higher temperatures, implying that warming harms tropical regions (which are already hot) far more than temperate regions (which may actually benefit from modest warming). When these damage functions are embedded in the spatial GE framework, the key predictions are:

1. **Aggregate effects are moderate but spatially concentrated.** Global GDP losses from climate change are estimated at 5–20 percent by 2100 under moderate warming scenarios, but the losses are concentrated in tropical and subtropical regions that are already poorer, while some temperate and boreal regions experience net gains.

2. **Migration is a powerful adaptation mechanism — if permitted.** Models that allow free migration produce smaller welfare losses than models that restrict migration, because people can move from damaged to improved locations. The spatial reallocation of population is welfare-improving in aggregate but distributive — it benefits movers at the expense of stayers, and it concentrates population in locations that may face congestion costs.

3. **Coastal exposure dominates the damage distribution.** Sea-level rise threatens a disproportionate share of global economic activity: approximately 10 percent of the world's population and 13 percent of global GDP are located within 10 meters of sea level. The spatial concentration of this exposure — in the Pearl River Delta, the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta, the Mekong Delta, coastal Florida, the Netherlands, and Pacific Island states — means that adaptation costs are highly localized.

### Innovation and Adaptation in Quantitative Spatial Models

The most recent generation of quantitative spatial models has moved beyond treating climate as an exogenous productivity shock to incorporate the endogenous responses — technological innovation, trade adjustment, and migration — that determine actual welfare outcomes. Cruz and Rossi-Hansberg (2024) extend the Desmet and Rossi-Hansberg (2015) framework by adding an innovation interaction: regions that experience climate damages have incentives to develop adaptation technologies, and these technologies can diffuse spatially through trade and knowledge spillovers. The model divides the world's surface into a one-degree grid (roughly 64,800 locations), assigns each location a productivity level, an amenity value, and a set of bilateral trade costs, and then simulates the dynamic response to CMIP6 climate projections over the 21st century.

The key results illuminate the spatial economics of the climate transition in ways that aggregate models cannot. First, the welfare decomposition reveals that migration, innovation, and trade each contribute substantially to adaptation, but their relative importance varies by region. For Sub-Saharan Africa, migration is the dominant adaptation channel — people moving from increasingly arid interiors to more habitable coasts and cities. For East Asia, innovation is dominant — high-income economies investing in heat-resistant crops, cooling technology, and flood infrastructure. For Europe, trade adjustment is significant — shifting import patterns to source agricultural products from regions with improving growing conditions.

Second, and critically, restricting migration roughly doubles the aggregate welfare cost of climate change. When people cannot move from damaged to improved locations, they bear the full brunt of locational degradation rather than relocating to areas where their productivity is higher. This result has profound policy implications: border restrictions, whether between countries or between regions within countries, are not merely migration policy — they are climate adaptation policy, and restrictive migration regimes are implicitly choosing larger welfare losses from climate change.

Third, and most relevant for the stranded regions discussion in Section 15.3, the interaction between innovation and climate damage is spatially heterogeneous. Wealthy regions with strong innovation systems — the Hsinchu-type science parks of Chapter 6, the Fraunhofer networks of Chapter 9 — respond to climate stress by developing new technologies that partially offset the productivity loss. Poor regions without innovation capacity experience the raw damage without technological cushion. This mechanism amplifies the spatial inequality that the physical impacts alone would produce, creating a double divergence: poor regions suffer more physical damage *and* have less capacity to innovate their way out of it.

## 15.2 Pacific Islands: Managed Retreat as Spatial Policy

### SIDS as Extreme Cases

Small Island Developing States (SIDS) in the Pacific — Tuvalu, Kiribati, the Marshall Islands, Tonga, Samoa, Vanuatu — represent the extreme case of climate-induced locational degradation. These states face existential threats from sea-level rise (which threatens to submerge low-lying atolls), ocean acidification (which degrades the coral reef ecosystems that support fisheries and tourism), and intensifying tropical cyclones (which can devastate entire national economies in a single event — Cyclone Pam inflicted damages equivalent to 64 percent of Vanuatu's GDP in a single weekend in 2015). Tuvalu's highest point is 4.6 meters above sea level; Kiribati's is 3 meters. Under moderate warming scenarios, significant portions of these nations' territory may be permanently inundated by 2100.

The spatial economics of SIDS are distinctive because the standard adaptation options — build sea walls, relocate inland, diversify the economy — are constrained by the physical geography. There is no "inland" to relocate to on an atoll. Sea walls protect against storm surge but not against the rising water table that salts the freshwater lens on which agriculture depends. Economic diversification is limited by the tiny domestic market (Tuvalu's population is approximately 12,000) and extreme remoteness from global markets.

The result is that "managed retreat" — the organized relocation of populations from locations that can no longer sustain habitation — is not a last-resort option for SIDS but a central planning challenge. Managed retreat at the national scale means emigration: the population of Tuvalu or Kiribati relocates to New Zealand, Australia, or Fiji. Some institutional frameworks for this already exist: New Zealand's Pacific Access Category provides permanent residence for up to 75 Tuvaluans per year (selected by ballot), and the Australia-Tuvalu Falepili Union, signed in November 2023, offers a more comprehensive pathway. Under the Falepili Union, Tuvaluan citizens gain the right to live, work, and study in Australia, with an initial intake of 280 per year. Crucially, the agreement also commits Australia to support Tuvalu's territorial sovereignty and its claim to maritime zones — a provision with enormous economic stakes, because Tuvalu's Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) of roughly 750,000 square kilometers is the source of fishing license revenues that constitute a major share of government income. If Tuvalu's land territory becomes uninhabitable but its maritime zone remains legally recognized, the country could persist as a sovereign entity whose primary economic asset is ocean, not land — a genuinely novel form of statehood in the international system.

Kiribati has pursued a different strategy: direct land purchase abroad. In 2014, Kiribati acquired approximately 6,000 acres on Vanua Levu, Fiji's second-largest island, ostensibly for agricultural use but widely understood as a potential relocation site. President Anote Tong framed the initiative under the rubric of "migration with dignity" — a phrase that captures the distinction between planned relocation with institutional support and the chaotic displacement that climate change might otherwise produce. The Marshall Islands, for its part, relies on its Compact of Free Association with the United States, which gives Marshallese citizens the right to live and work in the US without a visa. The Compact was originally negotiated in 1986 as partial compensation for the nuclear testing program that rendered Bikini and Enewetak atolls uninhabitable — an earlier, man-made form of territorial loss. The US military base on Kwajalein Atoll, which houses missile testing facilities, provides lease payments that are the largest single source of government revenue, anchoring the Marshall Islands' economy to a geopolitical asset that, ironically, could itself be threatened by the sea-level rise that American emissions helped produce.

Fiji has emerged as the regional anchor for Pacific climate diplomacy. It presided over COP23 in 2017 — the first SIDS presidency of a UN climate conference — and has positioned itself as the hub for regional adaptation coordination. Fiji's relative size (population of roughly 900,000, with significant highlands well above sea level) makes it the natural destination for climate-displaced Pacific Islanders, but absorptive capacity is finite, and land tenure in Fiji is politically fraught: approximately 83 percent of land is communally owned by indigenous iTaukei, limiting the availability of freehold land for resettlement.

### Remittances as Post-Retreat Economics

For Pacific Island states that experience significant out-migration, remittances become the primary economic link between the diaspora and the homeland — paralleling the remittance economies described for conflict-affected MENA states in Chapter 12, but driven by environmental rather than political displacement. Tonga already depends on remittances for approximately 40 percent of GDP, and the Tongan diaspora in New Zealand, Australia, and the United States is larger than the population of Tonga itself. Samoa's remittance share is roughly 25 percent of GDP. These ratios are comparable to the most remittance-dependent economies in the MENA region — Lebanon, Jordan, the West Bank and Gaza — but the Pacific Island pattern is distinctive in that the diaspora is overwhelmingly concentrated in just two destination countries (New Zealand and Australia), creating a bilateral dependency that leaves little room for diversification.

The diaspora functions as a form of climate insurance. When Cyclone Pam devastated Vanuatu in 2015, remittance inflows spiked as overseas relatives sent emergency transfers — a pattern that mirrors the countercyclical remittance behavior documented in the disaster economics literature (Yang 2008). Over longer time horizons, the diaspora provides an institutional bridge: migrants who establish themselves in Australia or New Zealand can sponsor family members, maintain cultural and linguistic ties, and advocate for immigration policy changes that facilitate further movement. The question is whether this gradual diaspora-building can keep pace with the physical deterioration of the atolls.

The spatial economics of remittance-dependent SIDS raise fundamental questions about sovereignty, territory, and nationhood. If a significant portion of Tuvalu's population relocates to New Zealand while continuing to remit to relatives on the atoll, what is Tuvalu as an economic entity? Is it a territorial state with a declining domestic economy, or a transnational community whose economic activity spans two countries? The question is not purely theoretical: Tuvalu's internet domain (.tv), licensed to a US company, already generates revenue that is independent of the physical territory. If the atolls submerge but the EEZ, the domain, and the diaspora remain, Tuvalu would be something genuinely new in the international system — a sovereign entity whose economic base is entirely deterritorialized. These questions, which may seem abstract today, will become urgent for multiple Pacific Island states within the current generation's lifetime.

## 15.3 Stranded Regions: Identifying and Comparing Adaptive Capacity

### What Makes a Region "Stranded"?

A "stranded region" is one whose economic base is threatened by the climate transition — either because its physical environment is deteriorating (heat, drought, sea-level rise) or because its primary economic activity (fossil fuel extraction, carbon-intensive manufacturing, water-intensive agriculture) is rendered unviable by climate policy. The concept parallels the "stranded asset" terminology from climate finance, but applies to places rather than portfolios.

Drawing on the regional analyses of earlier chapters, we can identify several categories of stranded regions:

1. **Fossil fuel extraction zones.** The oil-producing regions of the Gulf (Chapter 11), the coal-mining regions of Appalachia and the Donbas, the oil sands of Alberta, the gas fields of West Siberia, and the Niger Delta (Chapter 13). These regions face demand-side stranding as the global energy transition reduces demand for their primary export. The timeline of stranding depends on the pace of the transition: under IEA's Net Zero by 2050 scenario, oil demand peaks before 2030 and declines by 75 percent by 2050, while under the Stated Policies Scenario, demand plateaus in the mid-2030s and declines gradually — a difference that determines whether stranding is abrupt or manageable.

2. **Carbon-intensive manufacturing zones.** The steel and cement production clusters of China's northeast, India's Jharkhand and Chhattisgarh, Turkey's Marmara region, and the heavy industrial corridors of North Africa (Chapter 13). These face regulatory stranding as carbon border adjustments (the EU's CBAM, potential US and UK equivalents) penalize exports from carbon-intensive production processes. The stranding is compounded when these regions also depend on fossil fuel inputs — Indian steel producers in Jharkhand, for instance, use domestic coking coal and coal-fired electricity, meaning that decarbonization requires simultaneously transforming both the energy input and the production process.

3. **Water-stressed agricultural zones.** The Murray-Darling basin (Australia), the Central Valley (California), the wheat belt of Punjab (India/Pakistan), the irrigated plains of the Nile Delta (Egypt), and the rain-fed savanna systems of the Sahel (Chapter 13). These face physical stranding as water resources decline below the threshold needed for productive agriculture. For glacier-fed systems in South Asia (Chapter 8) and Latin America (Chapter 5), the stranding has a particularly cruel temporal structure: glacial melt initially *increases* water flow, creating a false sense of abundance, before the glacier is depleted and flow collapses permanently — a "peak water" trajectory that mirrors the peak oil concept but with no technological substitute.

4. **Coastal exposure zones.** The Pearl River Delta (China), the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta (Bangladesh/India), South Florida (USA), the Netherlands. These face physical stranding from sea-level rise and storm surge, requiring either massive defensive investment or managed retreat.

### Just Transition Politics

The politics of "just transition" — the idea that communities dependent on fossil fuels deserve public investment to cushion the economic blow of decarbonization — illustrate how stranded-region dynamics play out in practice. Germany's Coal Commission (Kohlekommission), established in 2018, produced a landmark agreement in 2019: a negotiated phase-out of coal-fired power, originally targeted for 2038 and subsequently accelerated to 2030, accompanied by a 40-billion-euro structural adjustment package for the affected Lausitz, Rhineland, and Central German mining regions. The package includes infrastructure investment, research institution relocation (the Fraunhofer and DLR institutes opened satellite facilities in former coal regions), and early retirement provisions for miners. Germany's approach — a corporatist negotiation among federal government, state governments, industry, unions, and environmental groups — reflects the institutional density described in Chapter 9, where layered governance structures enable negotiated adjustment that less institutionally thick regions cannot replicate.

South Africa's Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP), announced at COP26 in Glasgow (2021), pledged $8.5 billion from a consortium of wealthy nations to support Eskom's transition away from coal. The spatial stakes are concentrated in Mpumalanga province, where coal mining and coal-fired power generation account for a large share of local employment and tax revenue. Eskom itself is a profoundly troubled institution — debt-laden, operationally inefficient, and plagued by the state-capture corruption documented under the Zuma administration — making the JETP simultaneously a climate program, an industrial restructuring program, and an institutional reform program. Indonesia ($20 billion) and Vietnam ($15.5 billion) received similar JETP commitments, though in all three cases, disbursement has lagged dramatically behind pledges, and the gap between announced finance and bankable projects remains wide.

### CBAM and Spatial Vulnerability

The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, which Section 15.4 analyzes as a policy instrument, also functions as a source of spatial stranding for exporting regions that cannot readily decarbonize. The mechanism of stranding is straightforward: when CBAM imposes a carbon cost equivalent of 50 to 80 euros per tonne on imports, producers in regions with carbon-intensive energy grids face a cost increase that erodes their competitive position in the EU market. For Indian steel producers concentrated in the Jharkhand-Odisha mineral belt, where blast furnaces depend on domestic coking coal and the electricity grid is 70 percent coal-fired, CBAM charges could add 15 to 25 percent to the delivered cost of steel in Europe — potentially pricing them out of a market that absorbs roughly 15 percent of India's steel exports. Turkey's cement industry, concentrated in the Marmara region and heavily dependent on the EU market, faces a similar cost squeeze of 10 to 15 percent. The pre-war Ukrainian steel industry, centered on Mariupol and Zaporizhzhia — cities that have since been devastated by Russian military assault — offers a cautionary case of compounding stranding: an industry that was already facing CBAM-induced cost pressure was then physically destroyed by conflict, illustrating how climate policy vulnerability and geopolitical vulnerability can interact in the same region.

The CBAM stranding effect is not symmetrical: it hits hardest at middle-income industrial exporters (India, Turkey, Egypt, Morocco) that are large enough to export carbon-intensive goods but too poor to finance rapid decarbonization. The least-developed countries, which produce few carbon-intensive exports, are largely exempt from direct CBAM exposure but may suffer indirect effects if CBAM redirects carbon-intensive production from India and China into their domestic markets — a "carbon dumping" scenario that replicates the trade deflection patterns familiar from anti-dumping economics.

### Water Stress and Irreversible Stranding

The water-stressed agricultural zones deserve particular attention because water stranding can be irreversible in ways that carbon stranding is not. A fossil fuel region can, in principle, reinvent itself — the coal miners retrain, the power plants convert to gas or close, and the landscape recovers. But a region that exhausts its aquifer or loses its glacier-fed river system cannot recover on any human timescale.

Australia's Murray-Darling Basin — the continent's agricultural heartland, producing roughly 40 percent of national agricultural output — has been the site of decades of water politics that preview what other basins will experience. Chronic over-allocation of water licenses, combined with the Millennium Drought (1997-2009), led to the Murray-Darling Basin Plan (2012), which attempted to claw back water from irrigators through a combination of buybacks and infrastructure efficiency investments. The buyback program, funded at 13 billion Australian dollars, was politically explosive: upstream irrigators accused downstream interests of water theft, and rural communities that depended on irrigation saw buybacks as an existential threat. The basin remains over-allocated, and the politics of water reform have calcified along urban-rural and upstream-downstream lines that make further adjustment extraordinarily difficult.

The Aral Sea offers a historical precedent for irreversible water stranding. Soviet-era irrigation diversions for cotton monoculture in Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan reduced the Aral Sea to roughly 10 percent of its 1960 surface area by 2010, destroying the fisheries that supported an entire regional economy and employed tens of thousands, contaminating surrounding agricultural land with salt and pesticide residues, and creating a public health disaster from windblown toxic dust that raised cancer and respiratory disease rates in the surrounding population. The former port city of Moynaq now sits over 100 kilometers from the water's edge, its rusting fleet of fishing boats a monument to spatial stranding in its most literal form. The Aral Sea case demonstrates that water stranding can produce cascading failures across multiple economic sectors simultaneously — and that once the threshold is crossed, recovery is essentially impossible on any policy-relevant timescale.

Punjab's groundwater crisis is arguably the most consequential water-stranding risk in the world today, given the region's role in feeding 1.4 billion Indians. GRACE satellite data show groundwater tables in northwestern India declining at roughly one centimeter per year (Rodell et al. 2009), driven by heavily subsidized electricity for irrigation pumps and a minimum support price regime that incentivizes water-intensive rice cultivation. If the aquifer declines to the point where pumping becomes uneconomical, the spatial consequences would ripple through India's food system and beyond — a stranding event that would dwarf any fossil fuel transition in human impact.

### Cross-Regional Comparison of Adaptive Capacity

The concept of adaptive capacity — the institutional, financial, and human capital resources available for managing the transition — determines which stranded regions will adapt successfully and which will decline. Drawing on the institutional analysis of every preceding chapter, we can identify the factors that distinguish high- and low-adaptive-capacity stranded regions:

| Factor | High Adaptive Capacity | Low Adaptive Capacity |
|---|---|---|
| **Institutional quality** | Strong governance, rule of law, effective bureaucracy (Netherlands, Australia, Scandinavia) | Weak governance, corruption, institutional fragility (Sahel, SIDS, conflict states) |
| **Fiscal resources** | Sovereign wealth, diversified tax base, access to capital markets (Gulf SWFs, EU Cohesion Funds) | Debt-distressed, aid-dependent, narrow tax base (Sri Lanka, Pakistan, Sub-Saharan Africa) |
| **Human capital** | Educated workforce, research universities, innovation capacity (Germany, Japan, South Korea) | Low educational attainment, brain drain, lost generations (Yemen, Somalia, Myanmar) |
| **Economic diversification** | Multiple tradable sectors, services economy, innovation ecosystem (Singapore, Netherlands, Denmark) | Single-commodity dependence, weak private sector (Gulf states, mining economies, commodity exporters) |
| **Social cohesion** | Trust in institutions, social safety nets, political stability (Nordic model, Japan) | Ethnic fragmentation, political polarization, conflict history (MENA fragile states, divided societies) |

The table rewards careful cross-referencing with the chapters from which its cells are drawn. The human capital row captures the difference between Japan's managed demographic decline, where a highly educated but aging workforce adapts through automation and institutional innovation (Chapter 6), and Yemen's compounding collapse, where a generation of children has lost access to schooling during a decade of civil war, permanently degrading the human capital stock available for any future transition (Chapter 12). The fiscal resources row distinguishes between Norway, which converted petroleum wealth into the world's largest sovereign wealth fund ($1.6 trillion) precisely so that the economy could survive the end of oil, and Iraq, which consumed its oil revenues in current expenditure and reconstruction from successive wars, leaving no buffer for the transition. The economic diversification row illuminates why Singapore — a city-state with no natural resources, high physical exposure to sea-level rise, and a $100 billion coastal protection plan — nonetheless ranks among the most adaptive locations on earth: its services-dominated economy has few stranded assets, and its institutional capacity (Chapter 7) allows it to engineer defensive infrastructure at a scale that would overwhelm weaker states.

The cross-regional comparison reveals a cruel irony: the regions most exposed to climate risk are, on average, the least equipped to adapt. Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the MENA conflict states face the most severe physical impacts and have the least institutional, financial, and human capital capacity for adaptation. The developed world faces more moderate physical impacts and has greater adaptive capacity. The climate transition is, in this sense, a spatial inequality amplifier — a mechanism that widens the gap between rich and poor regions through a channel (physical geography) that operates independently of the trade, investment, and institutional channels that conventional development economics focuses on.

## 15.4 Green Industrial Policy as Spatial Policy

### Carbon Border Adjustments: Institutional Design

The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), phased in from 2023, applies a carbon price to imports of carbon-intensive goods (steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen) based on the carbon content of the production process. CBAM's stated purpose is to prevent "carbon leakage" — the relocation of carbon-intensive production from regions with carbon pricing to regions without it. But CBAM is also a spatial policy: it redistributes comparative advantage away from countries with carbon-intensive production processes (typically developing countries with less stringent environmental regulation) and toward countries that have decarbonized their production (typically wealthy countries that have invested in clean energy and energy efficiency).

The phase-in timeline reveals the institutional complexity. The transitional period (October 2023 to December 2025) requires importers to report the embedded emissions in their products but imposes no financial obligations — a learning period designed to build measurement and verification capacity. Financial obligations begin in January 2026, when importers must purchase CBAM certificates at a price linked to the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) allowance price. Full implementation, with complete phase-out of free ETS allowances for covered sectors, arrives by 2034. This decade-long transition reflects a political compromise: domestic industries lobbied to retain free allowances as long as possible, while environmental groups and trade purists pushed for rapid implementation.

The WTO compatibility of CBAM is the subject of intense legal debate. The EU argues that CBAM qualifies under GATT Article XX's general exception for measures "necessary to protect human, animal or plant life or health" or "relating to the conservation of exhaustible natural resources." Critics — including India, China, South Africa, and Brazil — argue that CBAM discriminates against developing-country producers by effectively penalizing them for having less developed energy systems, violating the principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities" enshrined in the UNFCCC. The revenue dimension adds another layer: CBAM revenues flow into the EU budget, and developing countries have argued that some portion should be redirected as climate finance to help exporting countries decarbonize their production. This argument parallels the broader debate over whether climate policy instruments should incorporate distributional transfers — a debate that, in the EU's internal context, was resolved by the Cohesion Funds mechanism (Chapter 9), but that has no equivalent at the global level.

The spatial implications are significant for the developing economies analyzed in this book. India's steel exports to the EU (Chapter 8) will face CBAM charges that increase their effective cost. Turkey's cement exports will be similarly affected. China's aluminum exports face substantial CBAM exposure. For these economies, CBAM creates a choice: invest in decarbonization to avoid the border charge, or redirect exports to markets without carbon border adjustments. Either choice has spatial consequences: decarbonization investment concentrates in the regions with the best renewable energy resources (not necessarily the regions where carbon-intensive production is currently located), while trade redirection changes the geography of export markets. CBAM also interacts with the phase-out of free ETS allowances for EU domestic producers: as free allowances are withdrawn, EU steel and cement producers face the full carbon price, leveling the playing field between domestic and imported goods — but only if the domestic producers have decarbonized enough to survive without the subsidy implicit in free allowances.

### Green Subsidies: The IRA and Its Competitors

The US Inflation Reduction Act (2022), analyzed in Chapter 4, allocated approximately $370 billion in tax credits and subsidies for clean energy, electric vehicles, battery manufacturing, and hydrogen production. The IRA is explicitly spatial: its incentives favor domestic manufacturing, its "energy community" provisions direct investment toward communities with closing coal mines or retiring fossil fuel plants, and its Buy America requirements concentrate supply chain investment within US borders.

The IRA's spatial fingerprint within the United States is already visible. The "Battery Belt" — a corridor stretching from Georgia through Tennessee and Kentucky to Michigan — has attracted tens of billions of dollars in announced battery and EV manufacturing investment, with plants from SK Innovation (Commerce, Georgia), Ford-SK (Stanton, Tennessee), and multiple facilities in Michigan's traditional automotive heartland. The IRA's "energy communities" bonus credit, which provides an additional 10 percentage points of tax credit for investments in communities with closing coal mines, retiring coal plants, or high fossil fuel employment, explicitly directs green investment toward stranded regions — a spatial targeting mechanism that echoes the EU's Cohesion Fund logic (Chapter 9) but operates through the tax code rather than through grants. The domestic content requirements, which bonus tax credits for projects using American-made components, create a further spatial pull toward US manufacturing regions, though they also raise costs and have generated friction with trading partners (the EU and South Korea both challenged the domestic content provisions as discriminatory under WTO rules).

The IRA triggered competitive responses from the EU (the Green Deal Industrial Plan, the Net-Zero Industry Act, the Critical Raw Materials Act), Japan (GX strategy, with $150 billion in green transformation bonds), South Korea (green investment incentives), and India (the Production-Linked Incentive scheme for solar modules and batteries). The EU's Net-Zero Industry Act targets 40 percent domestic manufacturing capacity for key clean technologies by 2030, while the Critical Raw Materials Act aims to reduce dependence on any single third country to no more than 65 percent of supply for any strategic mineral — a provision aimed squarely at China's dominance in rare earth processing. The result is a global subsidy competition for green industrial investment that has clear spatial winners and losers: the countries offering the largest subsidies and most attractive regulatory environments attract the battery factories, solar panel plants, and hydrogen electrolyzers, while countries that cannot afford comparable subsidies (most of the developing world) risk being left out of the green industrial transition.

### The Developing Country Perspective

The green subsidy race raises profound equity questions. Africa contributes less than four percent of global greenhouse gas emissions but faces the highest projected climate costs as a share of GDP. The continent holds extraordinary renewable energy potential — the Sahara alone could theoretically produce several times the world's current electricity demand from solar — but lacks the fiscal capacity to offer the subsidies that would attract manufacturing investment. The Green Climate Fund, discussed in the Institutional Spotlight below, has disbursed roughly $12.5 billion in its first decade of operation — a sum that is dwarfed by the IRA's $370 billion in a single piece of legislation for a single country. The technology transfer mechanisms that developing countries have sought in climate negotiations — compulsory licensing for green technologies, patent pools, publicly funded research shared as global public goods — have largely failed to materialize, with wealthy countries citing intellectual property protection as a higher priority.

The "lithium triangle" of Bolivia, Chile, and Argentina illustrates the tension between resource endowment and institutional capacity in the green transition. These three countries hold roughly 60 percent of the world's known lithium reserves — the critical mineral for electric vehicle batteries — placing them at the center of the green supply chain. But the resource curse logic analyzed in Chapter 5 looms over the lithium boom. Bolivia, which holds the world's largest single lithium deposit in the Salar de Uyuni, has pursued a nationalization strategy under which the state enterprise YLB controls extraction and processing, aiming to capture more of the value chain domestically rather than exporting raw brine. The results have been disappointing: YLB has struggled with the technical challenges of extracting lithium from Uyuni's magnesium-rich brine, and Bolivia's lithium output remains negligible compared to Chile and Australia. Chile, by contrast, granted concessions to private firms (SQM and Albemarle) but imposed royalty structures and, in 2023, announced a new national lithium strategy that would require public-private partnerships for all future extraction. Argentina has taken the most market-friendly approach, with provincial-level concessions attracting substantial FDI. The three countries' divergent strategies represent a real-time experiment in green resource governance: nationalization vs. regulated private concession vs. decentralized liberalization. Whether the lithium triangle becomes a site of green industrialization or merely the latest chapter in Latin America's long history of commodity extraction without structural transformation depends on whether these countries can do what Chapter 5's analysis of the resource curse suggests is hardest — build the institutional capacity to manage a windfall wisely.

The spatial economics are reminiscent of the beggar-thy-neighbor trade policies of the 1930s, but in green: each country's subsidy attracts investment at the expense of competing locations, and the aggregate effect may be an overinvestment in green manufacturing capacity in wealthy countries and an underinvestment in developing countries — precisely the reverse of what the global climate transition requires. The flying-geese framework of Chapter 6 suggests that green technology should eventually diffuse from rich to poor countries as costs fall and production matures — solar panel manufacturing followed exactly this trajectory, migrating from Japan to China to Southeast Asia over two decades. But the subsidy competition risks interrupting this diffusion by artificially holding green manufacturing in high-cost locations, delaying the cost reductions that would make clean energy accessible in the global South.

This finding echoes the central argument of Chapter 2: institutions are not a background condition for economic activity but the primary determinant of spatial economic outcomes.

Second, the table reveals a "double exposure" problem: the regions most exposed to physical climate risk are also the most exposed to climate policy risk. Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of MENA face severe physical impacts (heat, drought, flooding) *and* policy-induced costs from CBAM and green subsidy competition. They suffer the damages of climate change and the adjustment costs of climate policy simultaneously, while having the least capacity to manage either. This double exposure is not coincidental — it reflects the structural position of developing regions in the global economy, where dependence on primary commodity exports (the structural feature that Chapters 5, 11, and 13 analyzed in detail) makes them vulnerable to both physical and regulatory shocks. Breaking the double exposure would require simultaneously diversifying economies, building institutional capacity, and decarbonizing production — a triple transformation that is far harder to achieve than any single element in isolation.

Third, the services trade column reveals an underappreciated dimension of the climate transition. As the goods economy decarbonizes, the geography of services trade will also shift. Green finance, carbon accounting, climate litigation, emissions verification, and adaptation consulting are all rapidly growing service sectors that are spatially concentrated in a handful of cities — London, New York, Singapore, Amsterdam. The climate transition is creating new services agglomerations that may prove as durable as the financial centers they nest within. Chapter 16 takes up this services dimension in detail.

## Data in Depth: The Stranded Region Index

**The question.** Which regions face the greatest risk of economic stranding from the climate transition, and which have the greatest capacity to adapt?

**Construction.** A composite Stranded Region Index (SRI) can be constructed from two sub-indices:

1. **Exposure Index:** The degree to which a region's economic base is threatened by climate change and climate policy.
   - Fossil fuel dependence: Oil/gas/coal rents as % of GDP (WDI)
   - Carbon intensity of exports: CO₂ per dollar of merchandise exports (OECD)
   - Physical climate exposure: Projected temperature change, precipitation change, sea-level rise exposure (IPCC AR6)
   - Water stress: Baseline water stress indicator (WRI Aqueduct)

2. **Adaptive Capacity Index:** The institutional, financial, and human capital resources available for managing the transition.
   - Governance quality: World Bank Governance Indicators (rule of law, government effectiveness)
   - Fiscal space: Government debt-to-GDP ratio, sovereign credit rating
   - Human capital: Mean years of schooling, tertiary enrollment rate
   - Economic diversification: Export concentration (Herfindahl), services share of GDP

$$
SRI_i = \frac{Exposure_i - \min(Exposure)}{\max(Exposure) - \min(Exposure)} - \frac{Capacity_i - \min(Capacity)}{\max(Capacity) - \min(Capacity)}
$$

A high SRI indicates a region with high exposure and low adaptive capacity — the most vulnerable to stranding. A low (or negative) SRI indicates a region with low exposure and high adaptive capacity — the best positioned for the transition.

**Expected findings.** The most vulnerable regions should include: Gulf oil producers with limited diversification (Kuwait, Iraq), coal-dependent developing economies (Mongolia, Mozambique), water-stressed agricultural zones in South Asia and North Africa, and Pacific SIDS. The best-positioned regions should include: diversified northern European economies, resource-rich countries with strong institutions (Norway, Australia), and economies with large renewable energy potential and institutional capacity (Chile, Morocco).

**Caveats.** Composite indices are useful for organizing cross-country comparisons but carry well-known limitations. The equal weighting of sub-components is arbitrary — there is no theoretical reason to weight fossil fuel dependence equally with water stress, and results can be sensitive to the weighting scheme. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) offers a data-driven alternative to equal weighting, but PCA weights reflect variance in the data rather than causal importance, which may not align with the economic question. The physical climate exposure variables are drawn from CMIP6 projections, which diverge sharply across Representative Concentration Pathway (RCP) scenarios after mid-century: under RCP 2.6 (aggressive mitigation), many exposure indicators stabilize, while under RCP 8.5 (business as usual), they accelerate dramatically. The SRI ranking is therefore conditional on the emissions scenario, and students should report results under at least two scenarios. Finally, the institutional component relies partly on the World Bank's Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI), which Langbein and Knack (2010) have shown suffer from measurement error that correlates with income level — wealthier countries receive systematically higher governance scores partly because the perception-based surveys that feed the WGI are influenced by national income. This means the SRI may overstate the adaptive capacity gap between rich and poor countries, though the qualitative pattern — that institutional capacity is higher in wealthier nations — is robust to alternative measurement approaches.

**Student exercise.** Construct the SRI for 20 countries of your choice, using WDI data for fossil fuel rents, export concentration, mean years of schooling, and debt-to-GDP ratios, and WGI data for governance quality. Use WRI Aqueduct for water stress and IPCC AR6 regional projections for physical exposure. Perform a sensitivity analysis: how does the country ranking change when you vary the weights on physical exposure vs. policy exposure vs. adaptive capacity? Try equal weighting, PCA-derived weighting, and a scheme where you double the weight on the dimension you consider most important (justify your choice). Compare your SRI ranking to the NDC ambition ratings published by Climate Action Tracker. What explains the discrepancies? Are the countries with the worst SRI scores also the ones with the least ambitious Nationally Determined Contributions, or is there a disconnect between vulnerability and policy ambition?

## Institutional Spotlight: The Green Climate Fund

The Green Climate Fund (GCF), established at COP16 in Cancun in 2010 and operationalized in 2015 with its headquarters in Songdo, South Korea, was designed to be the primary channel for climate finance from developed to developing countries. Its mandate reflects the UNFCCC principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities": wealthy nations that are historically responsible for the bulk of greenhouse gas emissions would provide financial resources to help poorer nations both mitigate their own emissions and adapt to the climate impacts they did little to cause.

The numbers tell a sobering story of ambition meeting institutional reality. The original goal — $100 billion per year in climate finance from developed to developing countries by 2020 — was never formally assigned to the GCF alone, but the fund was expected to be the centerpiece. In its first decade of operation (2015-2024), the GCF approved approximately $12.5 billion in project funding. The second replenishment (2024-2027) secured $5.6 billion in pledges. These figures are not trivial, but they are a small fraction of the estimated adaptation financing needs of developing countries, which the UN Environment Programme places at $140-300 billion per year by 2030.

The GCF's allocation rules attempt to address the distributive concerns that pervade climate finance. The board targets a 50/50 split between mitigation and adaptation funding — a response to developing countries' complaint that mitigation receives disproportionate attention because it generates measurable emission reductions that wealthy countries can count toward their own targets. Within the adaptation portfolio, 50 percent is allocated to the most vulnerable countries: Least Developed Countries (LDCs), Small Island Developing States (SIDS), and African nations. These targeting rules create a spatial allocation pattern that directs resources toward the regions identified in this book as having the lowest adaptive capacity.

Governance tensions are built into the GCF's structure. The 24-member board is split evenly between developed and developing country representatives — a parity arrangement that guarantees developing countries a voice but also creates decision-making gridlock on contentious issues. The accreditation process, which entities must complete before they can access GCF funding, has been a persistent bottleneck: preparing a GCF proposal requires the kind of institutional capacity (project design, fiduciary management, environmental and social safeguards) that the most vulnerable countries lack, creating a paradox where the countries that need climate finance most are least able to access it. International intermediaries (UNDP, multilateral development banks) can serve as accredited entities on behalf of small countries, but this raises concerns about "country ownership" — whether the projects genuinely reflect national priorities or the intermediary's institutional preferences.

The comparison to the EU's Cohesion Funds (Chapter 9) is both instructive and sobering. Both mechanisms are place-based transfer systems that redistribute resources from wealthy to poor regions in pursuit of convergence. Both face tensions between donor conditionality and recipient autonomy. But the differences are as revealing as the similarities. Cohesion Funds operate within a supranational legal framework — the EU treaties — that gives the European Commission enforcement authority over how funds are spent, including the power to suspend payments for non-compliance with macroeconomic or governance conditionality. The GCF operates in a voluntary international framework without enforcement: developed countries can (and do) fall short of their pledges without legal consequence. The scale difference is equally stark: EU Cohesion Policy allocated approximately 392 billion euros for the 2021-2027 programming period; the GCF has disbursed roughly $12.5 billion in total — a ratio of roughly 30 to 1, despite the climate adaptation needs of developing countries being orders of magnitude larger than the convergence needs of EU member states.

## Conclusion: Toward a Climate-Aware Spatial Economics

The climate transition is the defining spatial reallocation of the 21st century. It will redraw comparative advantage, redirect investment flows, displace hundreds of millions of people, and create new winners and losers across every region analyzed in this book. The analytical tools developed in Chapters 1-3 — agglomeration theory, institutional analysis, gravity models, spatial econometrics — are directly applicable to understanding and managing this transition. But they need to be supplemented with two elements that traditional spatial economics has underemphasized: the physical geography of climate change (which alters the "first nature" that NEG theory takes as given) and the political economy of climate policy (which determines how the costs and benefits of the transition are distributed across regions).

A note on uncertainty is warranted. Climate economics deals in scenarios, not predictions. The CMIP6 ensemble produces a range of outcomes conditional on emission pathways, and the spread between RCP 2.6 and RCP 8.5 is enormous — the difference between a world that stabilizes warming near 1.5 degrees Celsius and one that exceeds 4 degrees. Worse, the economic damage functions that translate warming into GDP losses are themselves deeply uncertain, and several features of the climate system — the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC), permafrost methane release, the West Antarctic Ice Sheet — exhibit threshold behavior that could produce abrupt, irreversible shifts. As Weitzman (2009) argued in his "dismal theorem," the fat-tailed distribution of catastrophic climate outcomes means that standard cost-benefit analysis, which discounts low-probability events, systematically underestimates the expected damage from climate change. The spatial economist's task is not to predict which regions will thrive and which will decline, but to identify the structural vulnerabilities, institutional capacities, and policy levers that will determine outcomes across a range of scenarios.

The political economy of managed decline deserves a final word. No community embraces the label "stranded region." Coal communities in Appalachia, the Lausitz, Mpumalanga, and Shanxi vote for candidates who promise to preserve their industries, not manage their decline. The populist backlash against climate policy — visible in the "geography of discontent" that Chapter 10 analyzed for Brexit, and in the gilets jaunes protests against fuel tax increases in France — reflects a rational calculation by people in exposed regions that the benefits of climate policy accrue elsewhere while the costs land on them. Any climate spatial policy that ignores this political economy will fail, not because it is analytically wrong, but because it cannot build the coalitions needed for implementation. The just transition frameworks discussed in Section 15.3 — Germany's Kohlekommission, South Africa's JETP — are attempts to solve this political economy problem, with mixed results.

The climate transition will reshape the map of comparative advantage. But it is not the only force doing so. Chapter 16 examines two other transformative forces — the telemigration of services and the fragmentation of the digital commons — that will interact with climate change to determine the economic geography of the mid-21st century. A heat-stressed city in South Asia that cannot attract manufacturing may nonetheless become a hub for remotely delivered digital services; a region stranded by decarbonization may reinvent itself as a data center corridor powered by the same wind and solar resources that replaced its coal plants. The future map is not determined by climate alone, but by the interaction of climate, technology, and institutions — the same triad that has shaped economic geography since the beginning.
