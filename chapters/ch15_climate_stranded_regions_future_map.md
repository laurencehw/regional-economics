# Chapter 15: Climate, Stranded Regions, and the Future Map

## Introduction: The Great Spatial Reallocation

Every regional chapter in this book contains a climate section — the IRA's spatial consequences in North America (Chapter 4), lithium extraction and green hydrogen in Latin America (Chapter 5), coal dependency and green transition in East Asia (Chapter 6), BRI climate infrastructure in Southeast Asia (Chapter 7), Sahel desertification in West Africa (Chapter 13), flood-risk corridors in East Africa (Chapter 14). These sections documented climate change as a spatial economic phenomenon: one that redistributes comparative advantage across regions, creates new winners and losers, and transforms the institutional landscape through which economic activity is organized. This chapter synthesizes those regional analyses into a cross-regional framework for understanding the spatial economics of the climate transition.

The central argument is that climate change is not primarily a temperature problem or a sea-level problem — it is a spatial reallocation problem. The physical effects of climate change (heat, drought, flooding, sea-level rise, storms) make some locations less productive and less habitable, while making other locations relatively more attractive. The policy responses to climate change (carbon pricing, green subsidies, renewable energy mandates, carbon border adjustments) create new patterns of comparative advantage that reward some regions and penalize others. And the technological responses (electric vehicles, green hydrogen, carbon capture, precision agriculture) concentrate innovation and investment in specific places, generating the agglomeration dynamics that Chapter 1 described. The net effect is a spatial reallocation of economic activity — capital, labor, and institutional capacity — that will reshape the global economic geography over the coming decades more profoundly than any force since the industrial revolution.

Section 15.1 develops a formal framework for analyzing climate-induced spatial reallocation — the movement of capital and labor in response to changing locational advantages. Section 15.2 examines Pacific Island states as extreme cases of climate-driven managed retreat. Section 15.3 identifies "stranded regions" — places whose economic base is threatened by the climate transition — and compares their adaptive capacity across the regions studied in earlier chapters. Section 15.4 analyzes green industrial policy as spatial policy: how carbon border adjustments, green subsidies, and renewable energy mandates redistribute economic activity across regions. Section 15.5 presents a global comparative spread that synthesizes the regional diagnostics from all preceding chapters into a single framework.

## 15.1 Modeling Climate-Induced Spatial Reallocation

### The Spatial Economics of Changing Locational Advantage

Climate change alters the fundamental parameters of locational advantage that spatial economic theory takes as given. The "first nature" geography that classical location theory treats as fixed — the climate that determines agricultural productivity, the coastlines that define port access, the water resources that sustain urban populations — is being modified by physical climate change on timescales that are fast relative to the infrastructure and institutions that economic activity depends on.

The spatial reallocation framework extends the NEG models of Chapter 1 to incorporate time-varying locational fundamentals. In the standard NEG framework, the spatial equilibrium is determined by the interaction of agglomeration forces (which concentrate activity) and dispersion forces (which spread it out), given fixed locational characteristics. Climate change introduces a third force: locational degradation (or enhancement) that shifts the spatial equilibrium by changing the productivity of specific locations. A city in the Persian Gulf that becomes uninhabitable during summer months without air conditioning loses locational advantage. A port city in the Arctic that becomes ice-free for longer periods gains it. Agricultural regions in southern Europe that lose rainfall shift from productive to marginal. Agricultural regions in Scandinavia and Canada that gain growing-season days shift from marginal to productive.

The reallocation operates through three channels:

1. **Capital reallocation.** Investment flows toward locations with improving climate fundamentals and away from locations with deteriorating fundamentals. The $500 billion global annual investment in renewable energy is spatially concentrated in locations with the best solar and wind resources — the Gulf, the Sahara fringe, Patagonia, the Great Plains, offshore Northern Europe — creating new industrial clusters that did not exist twenty years ago.

2. **Labor reallocation.** Population movements in response to climate stress are already substantial and will accelerate. The World Bank (Groundswell 2021) estimates that by 2050, climate change could displace 216 million people within their own countries — predominantly from Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America. This internal climate migration follows the same gravity model patterns as economic migration: people move from locations with deteriorating conditions to the nearest locations with better conditions, subject to distance costs, institutional barriers, and social networks.

3. **Institutional reallocation.** Climate adaptation requires institutional capacity — the ability to plan, finance, and implement infrastructure investments, regulatory changes, and social protection programs. This capacity is unequally distributed (as every chapter in this book has documented), and the climate transition will further concentrate institutional capacity in places that already have it, while straining it in places that don't.

### Formal Framework: Climate Productivity Shocks in a Spatial GE Model

The recent spatial general equilibrium literature — Desmet and Rossi-Hansberg (2015), Conte, Desmet, Nagy, and Rossi-Hansberg (2021), Cruz and Rossi-Hansberg (2024) — has begun to incorporate climate change into quantitative spatial models. These models divide the world's land surface into a fine grid of locations, assign each location a set of economic fundamentals (productivity, amenities, trade costs to other locations), and solve for the spatial equilibrium distribution of population and economic activity.

Climate change enters these models as a shock to location-specific productivity and amenities. The key predictions are:

1. **Aggregate effects are moderate but spatially concentrated.** Global GDP losses from climate change are estimated at 5–20 percent by 2100 under moderate warming scenarios, but the losses are concentrated in tropical and subtropical regions that are already poorer, while some temperate and boreal regions experience net gains.

2. **Migration is a powerful adaptation mechanism — if permitted.** Models that allow free migration produce smaller welfare losses than models that restrict migration, because people can move from damaged to improved locations. The spatial reallocation of population is welfare-improving in aggregate but distributive — it benefits movers at the expense of stayers, and it concentrates population in locations that may face congestion costs.

3. **Coastal exposure dominates the damage distribution.** Sea-level rise threatens a disproportionate share of global economic activity: approximately 10 percent of the world's population and 13 percent of global GDP are located within 10 meters of sea level. The spatial concentration of this exposure — in the Pearl River Delta, the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta, the Mekong Delta, coastal Florida, the Netherlands, and Pacific Island states — means that adaptation costs are highly localized.

## 15.2 Pacific Islands: Managed Retreat as Spatial Policy

### SIDS as Extreme Cases

Small Island Developing States (SIDS) in the Pacific — Tuvalu, Kiribati, the Marshall Islands, Tonga, Samoa, Vanuatu — represent the extreme case of climate-induced locational degradation. These states face existential threats from sea-level rise (which threatens to submerge low-lying atolls), ocean acidification (which degrades the coral reef ecosystems that support fisheries and tourism), and intensifying tropical cyclones (which can devastate entire national economies in a single event). Tuvalu's highest point is 4.6 meters above sea level; Kiribati's is 3 meters. Under moderate warming scenarios, significant portions of these nations' territory may be permanently inundated by 2100.

The spatial economics of SIDS are distinctive because the standard adaptation options — build sea walls, relocate inland, diversify the economy — are constrained by the physical geography. There is no "inland" to relocate to on an atoll. Sea walls protect against storm surge but not against the rising water table that salts the freshwater lens on which agriculture depends. Economic diversification is limited by the tiny domestic market (Tuvalu's population is approximately 12,000) and extreme remoteness from global markets.

The result is that "managed retreat" — the organized relocation of populations from locations that can no longer sustain habitation — is not a last-resort option for SIDS but a central planning challenge. Managed retreat at the national scale means emigration: the population of Tuvalu or Kiribati relocates to New Zealand, Australia, or Fiji. Some institutional frameworks for this already exist: New Zealand's Pacific Access Category provides permanent residence for up to 75 Tuvaluans per year (selected by ballot), and the Australia–Tuvalu Falepili Union (2023) offers a pathway for Tuvaluan citizens to live and work in Australia.

### Remittances as Post-Retreat Economics

For Pacific Island states that experience significant out-migration, remittances become the primary economic link between the diaspora and the homeland — paralleling the remittance economies described for conflict-affected MENA states in Chapter 12, but driven by environmental rather than political displacement. Tonga already depends on remittances for approximately 40 percent of GDP, and the Tongan diaspora in New Zealand, Australia, and the United States is larger than the population of Tonga itself.

The spatial economics of remittance-dependent SIDS raise fundamental questions about sovereignty, territory, and nationhood. If a significant portion of Tuvalu's population relocates to New Zealand while continuing to remit to relatives on the atoll, what is Tuvalu as an economic entity? Is it a territorial state with a declining domestic economy, or a transnational community whose economic activity spans two countries? These questions, which may seem abstract today, will become urgent for multiple Pacific Island states within the current generation's lifetime.

## 15.3 Stranded Regions: Identifying and Comparing Adaptive Capacity

### What Makes a Region "Stranded"?

A "stranded region" is one whose economic base is threatened by the climate transition — either because its physical environment is deteriorating (heat, drought, sea-level rise) or because its primary economic activity (fossil fuel extraction, carbon-intensive manufacturing, water-intensive agriculture) is rendered unviable by climate policy. The concept parallels the "stranded asset" terminology from climate finance, but applies to places rather than portfolios.

Drawing on the regional analyses of earlier chapters, we can identify several categories of stranded regions:

1. **Fossil fuel extraction zones.** The oil-producing regions of the Gulf (Chapter 11), the coal-mining regions of Appalachia and the Donbas, the oil sands of Alberta, the gas fields of West Siberia. These regions face demand-side stranding as the global energy transition reduces demand for their primary export.

2. **Carbon-intensive manufacturing zones.** The steel and cement production clusters of China's northeast, India's Jharkhand and Chhattisgarh, Turkey's Marmara region. These face regulatory stranding as carbon border adjustments (the EU's CBAM, potential US equivalents) penalize exports from carbon-intensive production processes.

3. **Water-stressed agricultural zones.** The Murray-Darling basin (Australia), the Central Valley (California), the wheat belt of Punjab (India/Pakistan), the irrigated plains of the Nile Delta (Egypt). These face physical stranding as water resources decline below the threshold needed for productive agriculture.

4. **Coastal exposure zones.** The Pearl River Delta (China), the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta (Bangladesh/India), South Florida (USA), the Netherlands. These face physical stranding from sea-level rise and storm surge, requiring either massive defensive investment or managed retreat.

### Cross-Regional Comparison of Adaptive Capacity

The concept of adaptive capacity — the institutional, financial, and human capital resources available for managing the transition — determines which stranded regions will adapt successfully and which will decline. Drawing on the institutional analysis of every preceding chapter, we can identify the factors that distinguish high- and low-adaptive-capacity stranded regions:

| Factor | High Adaptive Capacity | Low Adaptive Capacity |
|---|---|---|
| **Institutional quality** | Strong governance, rule of law, effective bureaucracy (Netherlands, Australia, Scandinavia) | Weak governance, corruption, institutional fragility (Sahel, SIDS, conflict states) |
| **Fiscal resources** | Sovereign wealth, diversified tax base, access to capital markets (Gulf SWFs, EU Cohesion Funds) | Debt-distressed, aid-dependent, narrow tax base (Sri Lanka, Pakistan, Sub-Saharan Africa) |
| **Human capital** | Educated workforce, research universities, innovation capacity (Germany, Japan, South Korea) | Low educational attainment, brain drain, lost generations (Yemen, Somalia, Myanmar) |
| **Economic diversification** | Multiple tradable sectors, services economy, innovation ecosystem (Singapore, Netherlands, Denmark) | Single-commodity dependence, weak private sector (Gulf states, mining economies, commodity exporters) |
| **Social cohesion** | Trust in institutions, social safety nets, political stability (Nordic model, Japan) | Ethnic fragmentation, political polarization, conflict history (MENA fragile states, divided societies) |

The cross-regional comparison reveals a cruel irony: the regions most exposed to climate risk are, on average, the least equipped to adapt. Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the MENA conflict states face the most severe physical impacts and have the least institutional, financial, and human capital capacity for adaptation. The developed world faces more moderate physical impacts and has greater adaptive capacity. The climate transition is, in this sense, a spatial inequality amplifier.

## 15.4 Green Industrial Policy as Spatial Policy

### Carbon Border Adjustments

The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), phased in from 2023, applies a carbon price to imports of carbon-intensive goods (steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen) based on the carbon content of the production process. CBAM's stated purpose is to prevent "carbon leakage" — the relocation of carbon-intensive production from regions with carbon pricing to regions without it. But CBAM is also a spatial policy: it redistributes comparative advantage away from countries with carbon-intensive production processes (typically developing countries with less stringent environmental regulation) and toward countries that have decarbonized their production (typically wealthy countries that have invested in clean energy and energy efficiency).

The spatial implications are significant for the developing economies analyzed in this book. India's steel exports to the EU (Chapter 8) will face CBAM charges that increase their effective cost. Turkey's cement exports will be similarly affected. China's aluminum exports face substantial CBAM exposure. For these economies, CBAM creates a choice: invest in decarbonization to avoid the border charge, or redirect exports to markets without carbon border adjustments. Either choice has spatial consequences: decarbonization investment concentrates in the regions with the best renewable energy resources (not necessarily the regions where carbon-intensive production is currently located), while trade redirection changes the geography of export markets.

### Green Subsidies: The IRA and Its Competitors

The US Inflation Reduction Act (2022), analyzed in Chapter 4, allocated approximately $370 billion in tax credits and subsidies for clean energy, electric vehicles, battery manufacturing, and hydrogen production. The IRA is explicitly spatial: its incentives favor domestic manufacturing, its "energy community" provisions direct investment toward communities with closing coal mines or retiring fossil fuel plants, and its Buy America requirements concentrate supply chain investment within US borders.

The IRA triggered competitive responses from the EU (the Green Deal Industrial Plan, the Net-Zero Industry Act), Japan (GX strategy), South Korea (green investment incentives), and India (the Production-Linked Incentive scheme for solar modules and batteries). The result is a global subsidy competition for green industrial investment that has clear spatial winners and losers: the countries offering the largest subsidies and most attractive regulatory environments attract the battery factories, solar panel plants, and hydrogen electrolyzers, while countries that cannot afford comparable subsidies (most of the developing world) risk being left out of the green industrial transition.

The spatial economics are reminiscent of the beggar-thy-neighbor trade policies of the 1930s, but in green: each country's subsidy attracts investment at the expense of competing locations, and the aggregate effect may be an overinvestment in green manufacturing capacity in wealthy countries and an underinvestment in developing countries — precisely the reverse of what the global climate transition requires.

## Data in Depth: The Stranded Region Index

**The question.** Which regions face the greatest risk of economic stranding from the climate transition, and which have the greatest capacity to adapt?

**Construction.** A composite Stranded Region Index (SRI) can be constructed from two sub-indices:

1. **Exposure Index:** The degree to which a region's economic base is threatened by climate change and climate policy.
   - Fossil fuel dependence: Oil/gas/coal rents as % of GDP (WDI)
   - Carbon intensity of exports: CO₂ per dollar of merchandise exports (OECD)
   - Physical climate exposure: Projected temperature change, precipitation change, sea-level rise exposure (IPCC AR6)
   - Water stress: Baseline water stress indicator (WRI Aqueduct)

2. **Adaptive Capacity Index:** The institutional, financial, and human capital resources available for managing the transition.
   - Governance quality: World Bank Governance Indicators (rule of law, government effectiveness)
   - Fiscal space: Government debt-to-GDP ratio, sovereign credit rating
   - Human capital: Mean years of schooling, tertiary enrollment rate
   - Economic diversification: Export concentration (Herfindahl), services share of GDP

$$
SRI_i = \frac{Exposure_i - \min(Exposure)}{\max(Exposure) - \min(Exposure)} - \frac{Capacity_i - \min(Capacity)}{\max(Capacity) - \min(Capacity)}
$$

A high SRI indicates a region with high exposure and low adaptive capacity — the most vulnerable to stranding. A low (or negative) SRI indicates a region with low exposure and high adaptive capacity — the best positioned for the transition.

**Expected findings.** The most vulnerable regions should include: Gulf oil producers with limited diversification (Kuwait, Iraq), coal-dependent developing economies (Mongolia, Mozambique), water-stressed agricultural zones in South Asia and North Africa, and Pacific SIDS. The best-positioned regions should include: diversified northern European economies, resource-rich countries with strong institutions (Norway, Australia), and economies with large renewable energy potential and institutional capacity (Chile, Morocco).

## Conclusion: Toward a Climate-Aware Spatial Economics

The climate transition is the defining spatial reallocation of the 21st century. It will redraw comparative advantage, redirect investment flows, displace hundreds of millions of people, and create new winners and losers across every region analyzed in this book. The analytical tools developed in Chapters 1–3 — agglomeration theory, institutional analysis, gravity models, spatial econometrics — are directly applicable to understanding and managing this transition. But they need to be supplemented with two elements that traditional spatial economics has underemphasized: the physical geography of climate change (which alters the "first nature" that NEG theory takes as given) and the political economy of climate policy (which determines how the costs and benefits of the transition are distributed across regions).

Chapter 16 concludes the book by examining the future of the global economic geography: the telemigration hypothesis, the Splinternet, and the frontier methods that will define the next generation of spatial economic analysis.
