## Introduction: Diversification as Spatial Engineering

In 2016, Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman announced Vision 2030 — a national transformation plan that proposed, among dozens of other initiatives, the construction of a new city called NEOM on the northwest coast of the Red Sea. NEOM would span 26,500 square kilometers of desert and mountain terrain, cost an estimated $500 billion, and include a 170-kilometer-long linear city called The Line, designed to house nine million residents in a mirrored structure 200 meters wide and 500 meters tall, powered entirely by renewable energy, connected by high-speed rail, and free of cars and streets. No aspect of NEOM's design — its location, its scale, its physical form, its technological ambitions — bore any relation to the spatial economics of Saudi Arabia as it actually existed. There was no existing population center at the site, no industrial cluster to anchor agglomeration, no natural resource (beyond sun and wind) to attract investment, and no market logic that would lead a private developer to build there. NEOM was spatial economics from scratch: the application of sovereign wealth to conjure an economy where none previously existed.

The question for regional economics is not whether NEOM will succeed or fail — predictions about specific mega-projects are unreliable and largely beside the point — but what the attempt reveals about the spatial economic challenges facing resource-dependent economies in the Middle East and North Africa. These challenges are rooted in a structural condition that the development economics literature calls the "resource curse" and that Chapter 2's institutional framework helps to explain: economies that derive a large share of their national income from the extraction of a single natural resource (oil, natural gas, minerals) tend to develop weak non-resource sectors, concentrated spatial patterns of economic activity, and institutional arrangements that impede the diversification necessary for long-term development.

The MENA region contains some of the most extreme cases of this structural condition. Six Gulf Cooperation Council states (Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman) collectively hold approximately one-third of global proven oil reserves and one-quarter of proven natural gas reserves. Oil and gas revenues have funded living standards that place several of these states among the world's richest by per capita income, but the underlying economies are narrow: in Saudi Arabia, oil accounted for approximately 40 percent of GDP and 60 percent of government revenue in 2023; in Kuwait, the shares were approximately 50 percent and 90 percent respectively. The non-oil economies of these states are dominated by government services, construction, and retail — sectors that depend on oil revenue for their demand — rather than by the diversified tradable sectors that sustain growth in non-resource economies.

This chapter examines the spatial economics of resource dependence and the diversification strategies that MENA states have adopted. Section 11.1 analyzes rentier state theory and the resource curse through a spatial economics lens. Section 11.2 examines the mega-project model of diversification — NEOM, KAEC, Masdar City — as attempts to engineer agglomeration through sovereign investment. Section 11.3 analyzes sovereign wealth funds as instruments of spatial economic policy. Section 11.4 compares the Gulf model with Australia's experience of resource wealth management — a case of "resource boom without the curse." Section 11.5 examines the Gulf's emergence as a global services hub through aviation, logistics, finance, and medical tourism. Section 11.6 addresses the climate dimension: extreme water scarcity and the green hydrogen gambit. Section 11.7 connects the analysis to Lab 5's synthetic control methodology for estimating the economic impact of resource shocks and conflicts.

## 11.1 Rentier State Theory and the Spatial Resource Curse

### The Institutional Logic of Rentierism

Rentier state theory, developed by Beblawi and Luciani (1987) and extended by subsequent scholars, identifies a distinctive institutional logic in states that derive a substantial share of their revenue from external rents (resource royalties, transit fees, foreign aid) rather than from domestic taxation. The core insight is that the relationship between taxation and institutional quality runs through representation: governments that tax their citizens must provide services and accountability to maintain legitimacy and compliance, creating an institutional feedback loop between revenue extraction and governance quality. Governments that receive external rents can finance public services without taxing citizens, severing this feedback loop and reducing the institutional pressure for transparency, accountability, and efficient public administration.

The spatial dimensions of the rentier model are underappreciated in the literature but central to understanding MENA's economic geography. Resource extraction is inherently spatial: oil fields, gas deposits, and mineral reserves are located in specific places, and the infrastructure for extraction (wells, pipelines, refineries, export terminals) creates concentrated industrial zones that generate employment, demand for services, and urbanization. But the rents from extraction flow to the central government, which redistributes them through public employment, subsidies, and construction spending in the capital city and a few secondary urban centers. The result is a spatial pattern where the resource-extraction zone generates the wealth but the capital city captures the rents — a form of internal core-periphery relationship mediated by the fiscal system rather than by market forces.

Saudi Arabia's spatial economy illustrates this pattern clearly. The Eastern Province, where the giant Ghawar, Safaniyah, and Shaybah oil fields are located, generates the bulk of the kingdom's export revenue. But the economic activity funded by that revenue — government ministries, military installations, healthcare facilities, universities, retail centers — is concentrated in Riyadh (the political capital), Jeddah (the commercial hub and gateway to Mecca and Medina), and the Dammam-Dhahran-Khobar metropolitan area in the Eastern Province itself. The vast interior of the country — the desert regions of Najd, Hail, and the Empty Quarter — receives government services and subsidies but has no significant non-oil economic activity. The spatial economy is simultaneously hyperconcentrated (in three metropolitan areas) and hyperextended (across a territory the size of Western Europe).

### Dutch Disease and Spatial Crowding Out

The resource curse operates partly through the Dutch Disease mechanism: resource export revenues appreciate the real exchange rate, making non-resource tradable sectors (manufacturing, agriculture, tradable services) less competitive internationally. The spatial corollary is that Dutch Disease crowds out economic diversification in precisely the locations where it would otherwise be most likely to occur. The coastal cities of the Gulf — Dubai, Doha, Manama, Muscat — have the locational advantages (port access, proximity to markets in South Asia and East Africa, established trading networks) that should, in principle, support diversified tradable-services economies. But the appreciation of the real exchange rate driven by oil revenues makes these cities expensive relative to competitors (Mumbai, Nairobi, Colombo) and directs investment toward non-tradable sectors (real estate, construction, domestic retail) that benefit from the oil-fueled domestic demand rather than competing in international markets.

The Gulf states have attempted to address Dutch Disease through institutional engineering: free zones (Dubai's JAFZA, Jebel Ali Free Zone; Bahrain's BIFC) that offer regulatory environments distinct from the domestic economy, allowing foreign firms to operate under different rules with respect to taxation, labor law, and corporate governance. These free zones are, in effect, institutional enclaves designed to create pockets of competitive conditions within economies that are otherwise shaped by resource-rent dynamics. The success of Dubai's free zones — which host thousands of multinational regional headquarters and generate substantial non-oil economic activity — demonstrates that institutional design can partially overcome Dutch Disease, but at the cost of creating a dual economy: a globally connected free-zone sector that operates under one set of rules and a domestic economy that operates under another.

## 11.2 Mega-Projects: Engineering Agglomeration from Scratch

### NEOM, KAEC, and the Saudi Model

Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 includes several mega-projects that represent attempts to create new economic clusters through sovereign investment. NEOM is the most ambitious, but it is not alone: King Abdullah Economic City (KAEC), located on the Red Sea coast north of Jeddah, was announced in 2005 as a new industrial and residential city designed to house two million residents and generate employment in manufacturing, logistics, and services. The Red Sea Project (now The Red Sea) is a luxury tourism development along 200 kilometers of coastline. Qiddiya is a planned entertainment and sports complex near Riyadh. Together, these mega-projects represent hundreds of billions of dollars in planned investment — funded primarily from the Public Investment Fund (PIF), Saudi Arabia's sovereign wealth fund — and they share a common spatial logic: create new urban nodes that will generate the agglomeration economies necessary for economic diversification.

The spatial economics of mega-project-based diversification are theoretically uncertain. The NEG literature (Chapter 1) identifies agglomeration forces — labor market pooling, input sharing, knowledge spillovers — that are self-reinforcing once they reach sufficient scale. But these forces require a critical mass of firms and workers to activate: a few anchor tenants and a government-funded infrastructure backbone are not enough to generate the spontaneous coordination, knowledge exchange, and entrepreneurial dynamism that characterize successful clusters. The question is whether sovereign investment can reach the tipping point beyond which agglomeration becomes self-sustaining, or whether it creates a permanently subsidized enclave that depends on continued government spending to survive.

The evidence from KAEC is cautionary. After nearly two decades of development and billions of dollars of investment, KAEC's population in 2023 was approximately 10,000 — far short of the planned two million. The industrial zone hosts a modest number of firms (a pharmaceutical plant, a building materials factory, a logistics facility) but nothing approaching the diversified industrial cluster that was envisioned. The residential districts are partially built but sparsely occupied. The fundamental problem is that firms and workers locate in cities for reasons that precede the physical infrastructure: proximity to customers, access to a deep labor market, availability of specialized suppliers, institutional familiarity. KAEC offered the infrastructure but not the market logic, and the market logic has not followed the infrastructure.

### Masdar City and the Abu Dhabi Model

Abu Dhabi's Masdar City, announced in 2006, represents a more modest but analytically instructive variant of the mega-project model. Originally planned as a zero-carbon, zero-waste city of 50,000 residents, Masdar was conceived as a demonstration project for sustainable urban development and a hub for clean energy research and technology. The Masdar Institute (now part of the Khalifa University of Science and Technology), the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) headquarters, and several clean energy companies are located on the site.

Masdar's scale is more realistic than NEOM's — hundreds of millions rather than hundreds of billions of dollars — and its specialization (clean energy) is more focused. But it shares the fundamental challenge: it is a planned cluster in a location where the market would not spontaneously have created one. The clean energy companies at Masdar are there because of government incentives and the IRENA anchor, not because Abu Dhabi is the optimal location for clean energy R&D. If the incentives were withdrawn, most would relocate to locations with larger talent pools and more diversified innovation ecosystems.

## 11.3 Sovereign Wealth Funds as Spatial Policy Instruments

### The Investment Geography of Gulf SWFs

The Gulf states' sovereign wealth funds — the Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA, approximately $990 billion), the Kuwait Investment Authority (KIA, approximately $800 billion), the Qatar Investment Authority (QIA, approximately $475 billion), and the Saudi Public Investment Fund (PIF, approximately $925 billion) — collectively manage over $3 trillion in assets. These funds serve the primary function of converting a depletable resource (oil in the ground) into a renewable asset (a diversified investment portfolio), but they also function as instruments of spatial economic policy in ways that the standard sovereign wealth fund literature does not fully capture.

The PIF's domestic investment program illustrates the spatial policy function most explicitly. Under Vision 2030, the PIF was transformed from a passive holding company for government-owned enterprises into an active investor with a mandate to drive economic diversification. The PIF's domestic investments — in NEOM, The Red Sea, Qiddiya, the new national airline Riyadh Air, the entertainment company SEVEN, the sports investment program — are spatial investments: they are located in specific places (Riyadh, the Red Sea coast, the northwest) and are designed to create economic activity in those places. The PIF is, in effect, a spatial development authority with a sovereign wealth fund's balance sheet.

The external investment programs of Gulf SWFs also have spatial dimensions. ADIA's portfolio, which is globally diversified across asset classes and geographies, does not target specific spatial outcomes, but its investments in real estate (including significant holdings in London, New York, and Asian financial centers) connect the Gulf to global APS networks. The QIA's high-profile investments — stakes in Volkswagen, Glencore, the London Stock Exchange Group, Barclays — create institutional linkages between Qatar and the global corporate headquarters cities where these firms are based.

### SWFs and the Intergenerational Transfer Problem

The spatial economics of sovereign wealth funds involve an intergenerational transfer problem with geographic dimensions. The current generation of Gulf citizens benefits from oil revenue that will eventually be exhausted. SWFs are designed to transform current oil revenue into financial assets that generate returns for future generations. But the geographic location of the investments matters: assets invested abroad generate financial returns that flow back to the Gulf, but they do not create the domestic economic activity, institutional capacity, or human capital that future generations will need if the oil runs out. Assets invested domestically (through the PIF's domestic program) create economic activity and potentially build institutional capacity, but they are exposed to the concentration risk of a single national economy that is itself dependent on the resource it is trying to diversify away from.

The optimal spatial allocation of sovereign wealth is not a problem that the standard finance literature addresses, because the standard framework treats all returns as fungible regardless of where they are earned. But from a spatial economics perspective, a dollar of return earned from a domestic investment that builds institutional capacity, creates employment for nationals, and generates knowledge spillovers is more valuable for long-term development than a dollar of return earned from a foreign portfolio investment — even if the financial return is identical.

## 11.4 Comparative Sidebar: Australia — Resource Boom Without the Curse

Australia provides the most instructive comparison for the Gulf states because it has experienced multiple resource booms — gold in the 1850s, wool and wheat through the early 20th century, iron ore, coal, and LNG from the 1960s to the present — without succumbing to the resource curse. Australia's GDP per capita is comparable to the Gulf states', its resource sector is large (minerals and energy accounted for approximately 65 percent of merchandise exports in 2023), and it faces similar Dutch Disease pressures on its non-resource tradable sectors.

The key difference is institutional. Australia had established democratic institutions, an independent judiciary, a professional civil service, and a diversified economy before the resource booms began. The institutional framework was not built by resource revenue — it was inherited from British colonial administration and developed through a century of democratic governance — and it proved resilient to the distortions that resource wealth creates. When the mining boom of the 2000s generated political pressure for fiscal expansion, the institutional checks — an independent central bank (the RBA), a functioning parliamentary opposition, a free press, and established bureaucratic norms — constrained the worst excesses of procyclical fiscal policy, even if they did not eliminate them entirely.

The spatial dimensions of Australia's resource economy are revealing. Mining activity is concentrated in remote regions — the Pilbara in Western Australia (iron ore), the Bowen Basin in Queensland (coal), the Browse and Carnarvon basins offshore (LNG) — while the diversified service economy is concentrated in the five state capitals (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth, Adelaide). The mining regions experience dramatic boom-bust cycles: populations swell during construction phases, housing costs spike, social infrastructure is strained, and then activity contracts when construction ends and production enters a lower-employment operational phase. The capital cities experience milder versions of the same cycle through the demand for mining-related professional services (engineering, geology, environmental consulting, legal services) and the wealth effects of mining investment.

Australia's Future Fund, established in 2006 with initial capital from the sale of the government's remaining stake in Telstra, is a sovereign wealth fund modeled on international best practice. Its mandate is narrower than the Gulf SWFs — it was designed primarily to fund future government superannuation liabilities — and its governance is more transparent: investment performance is reported publicly, the board is independent of government, and investment decisions are shielded from political direction. The contrast with the PIF, which is chaired by the Crown Prince and directed by royal decree, illustrates the institutional difference that determines whether sovereign wealth serves long-term development or short-term political objectives.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: The Opacity of SWF Investment Data**
>
> Sovereign wealth fund investment portfolios are among the least transparent major asset pools in the global financial system. While the Santiago Principles (2008) established voluntary transparency standards for SWFs, compliance varies enormously. Norway's Government Pension Fund Global publishes its complete portfolio annually, down to individual stock holdings. ADIA publishes a brief annual review with aggregate asset allocation but no details on individual investments. The PIF publishes even less: its domestic investments are announced through government media, but its international portfolio is largely opaque.
>
> For spatial analysis, this opacity is a binding constraint. Estimating the domestic economic impact of SWF investment — which sectors receive how much capital, which regions benefit from SWF-funded projects, how SWF investment interacts with private sector activity — requires data that the funds themselves do not publish. Researchers resort to partial sources: media reports of announced deals (which may not be completed as announced), commercial databases of FDI and private equity transactions (which capture some SWF activities but miss others), and government budget documents (which may consolidate SWF spending with other public investment). The result is a measurement landscape where the largest investors in many MENA economies are the least transparent.

## 11.5 The Gulf as Global Services Hub

### Aviation and Logistics: Dubai's State-Engineered Connectivity

Dubai's transformation from a small trading port to a global aviation and logistics hub is the most successful case of services-led diversification in the MENA region. The strategy, pursued by the Maktoum ruling family since the 1980s, centered on using state-owned enterprises — Emirates airline (founded 1985), DP World (the global ports operator), Dubai Duty Free, and the free zones that host thousands of multinational firms — to create a connectivity node that generates revenue from transit, trade facilitation, and business services rather than from oil extraction.

The spatial economics are distinctive. Dubai occupies a geographic position — roughly equidistant from London and Singapore, within an eight-hour flight of two-thirds of the world's population — that gives it a natural advantage as a transit hub. But geographic position alone does not create a hub; institutional investment does. Emirates' route network (over 150 destinations), Dubai International Airport's capacity (the world's busiest airport for international passengers before COVID), and the Jebel Ali Free Zone's infrastructure (the largest free zone in the region, hosting over 8,000 companies) are all products of state investment and state-directed strategy.

The hub model generates a specific spatial economic pattern: a very small geographic area (the Dubai emirate is 4,114 square kilometers, smaller than the county of Devon) with an exceptionally high density of economic activity in aviation, logistics, professional services, and tourism. Dubai's non-oil GDP accounts for approximately 95 percent of its total — a diversification ratio that no other Gulf economy approaches. But the model depends on continued connectivity investment and on Dubai's ability to maintain institutional advantages (regulatory efficiency, personal safety, infrastructure quality, lifestyle amenities) over competitors. Singapore, Istanbul, Doha (which has invested heavily in Qatar Airways and Hamad International Airport), and Addis Ababa (through Ethiopian Airlines' hub strategy) are all competing for the same transit flows.

### Financial Services: DIFC, QFC, and ADGM

The Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC), the Qatar Financial Centre (QFC), and the Abu Dhabi Global Market (ADGM) represent a distinctive institutional model for financial services development. Each is a "financial free zone" — a geographically defined area within the emirate or country that operates under its own legal system (typically English common law, administered by its own courts), its own financial regulatory authority, and its own corporate governance framework. DIFC, for example, operates under a legal framework modeled on English common law, regulated by the Dubai Financial Services Authority (an independent regulator modeled on the UK's FCA), and adjudicated by the DIFC Courts (staffed by judges from the UK, Singapore, and other common law jurisdictions).

This model creates what Chapter 2's institutional framework would call an institutional enclave: a pocket within the national territory where the rules of the game are fundamentally different from those that apply in the rest of the country. The enclave model solves the credibility problem that prevents Gulf economies from attracting international financial services firms under their domestic legal and regulatory frameworks (which are based on Sharia-influenced civil law, lack the precedent-based predictability of common law, and have historically offered weaker protections for minority shareholders and creditors). But it creates a dualism that may impede broader institutional development: the best regulatory capacity, the most experienced judges, and the most sophisticated legal professionals are concentrated in the free zones, while the domestic legal system continues to operate under different and generally less predictable rules.

### Medical Tourism

The Gulf states have invested heavily in healthcare infrastructure, attracting leading international hospital groups (Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi, Johns Hopkins-affiliated facilities, Bumrungrad-managed hospitals) and positioning themselves as medical tourism destinations. The medical tourism model is Mode 2 services trade (consumption abroad): patients travel from neighboring MENA countries, the Indian subcontinent, and Africa to consume healthcare services in Gulf facilities. The patients are drawn by the combination of quality healthcare infrastructure, geographic accessibility (within a few hours' flight of most of the region's population), and the absence of visa restrictions for medical tourists from many source countries.

The spatial economics of medical tourism in the Gulf parallel the analysis of telemedicine in Chapter 8: the service is high-value, requires physical co-presence (for surgery, complex diagnostics, and intensive care), and concentrates in locations that combine medical expertise with infrastructure quality and connectivity. Dubai and Abu Dhabi have positioned themselves as the premium tier; Bangkok, Singapore, and Istanbul compete for cost-conscious patients from the same source markets.

## 11.6 Climate: Water Scarcity and the Green Hydrogen Gambit

### The Spatial Economics of Water in the Gulf

The MENA region is the most water-scarce region in the world. The Gulf states receive less than 100 millimeters of annual rainfall, and their freshwater resources are almost entirely dependent on desalination — an energy-intensive process that currently accounts for approximately 25 percent of Saudi Arabia's total domestic energy consumption. The spatial economics of water scarcity are circular: cities need desalinated water to sustain their populations; desalination requires energy (overwhelmingly from fossil fuels); fossil fuel combustion contributes to the climate change that is reducing the region's already minimal rainfall. The sustainability of the Gulf's urban agglomerations depends on breaking this circularity — either by reducing water consumption, by switching desalination to renewable energy, or by reducing the size of the populations that need to be served.

The green hydrogen strategy represents the most ambitious attempt to address this circularity. Saudi Arabia's NEOM Green Hydrogen project (a joint venture between NEOM, ACWA Power, and Air Products) aims to produce green hydrogen at scale using solar and wind energy from the NEOM site. The hydrogen would be used for desalination, industrial processes, and export — potentially creating a new export commodity that replaces oil revenue with hydrogen revenue. The spatial economics are suggestive: the NEOM site has exceptional solar irradiance (among the highest in the world) and consistent wind resources, making it one of the lowest-cost locations globally for renewable energy production. If green hydrogen production at scale proves technically and economically viable, the Gulf's geographic advantages in solar energy could underpin a post-carbon economic model as effectively as its geological advantages in hydrocarbon reserves have underpinned the current model.

The uncertainty is enormous. Green hydrogen is not yet cost-competitive with grey hydrogen (produced from natural gas) for most applications. The infrastructure for hydrogen transport, storage, and end-use is largely unbuilt. The international market for green hydrogen is speculative — demand projections depend on policy decisions in Europe, Japan, and Korea that have not yet been finalized. But the spatial economics are clear: if the transition occurs, the regions with the lowest-cost renewable energy will have a new comparative advantage, and the Gulf's solar endowment positions it well.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: GDP Data Quality in Conflict Zones**
>
> The MENA region contains several states — Syria, Yemen, Libya, Iraq — where civil conflict has degraded or destroyed the statistical infrastructure needed to produce reliable economic data. Syria's Central Bureau of Statistics effectively ceased publishing national accounts data after 2010. Yemen's Central Statistical Organization has produced only sporadic estimates since 2015. Libya's Bureau of Statistics has operated intermittently.
>
> Researchers working on conflict-affected economies must rely on proxy indicators: satellite-measured night-lights as a proxy for economic activity (Henderson, Storeygard & Weil 2012), mobile phone records as a proxy for population movement, commodity prices as a proxy for informal market conditions, and cross-border trade data from partner countries as a proxy for the affected country's external economic activity. Each proxy has well-documented limitations (night-lights saturate in dense urban areas and fail to capture underground or indoor economic activity; mobile phone data depends on network infrastructure that may be damaged), but they represent the best available evidence for economies where the conventional data-generating process has collapsed.

## 11.7 Lab 5 and the Synthetic Control Framework

Lab 5 applies the Synthetic Control Method (Abadie and Gardeazabal 2003; Abadie, Diamond, and Hainmueller 2010) to MENA economies, constructing counterfactual trajectories that estimate what a country's economic path would have been in the absence of a specific shock — a conflict, an oil price collapse, or a major policy change.

**Exercise 1: Conflict Impact on GDP.** Students construct a synthetic control for a conflict-affected state (Syria or Yemen) using a donor pool of non-conflict MENA and comparator economies. The synthetic control is a weighted average of donor countries whose pre-conflict economic characteristics (GDP per capita, population growth, trade openness, oil dependence) closely match the treated country. The gap between the synthetic control's trajectory and the treated country's actual (estimated) trajectory measures the causal impact of the conflict on economic output.

**Exercise 2: Oil Price Shock.** Students construct a synthetic control for a Gulf economy around the 2014–2016 oil price collapse, estimating the counterfactual GDP trajectory under sustained high oil prices. The exercise illustrates the vulnerability of resource-dependent economies to commodity price shocks and provides a baseline for evaluating whether diversification strategies (Vision 2030, Dubai's services model) have reduced oil-price sensitivity.

**Exercise 3: Placebo Tests.** Students conduct in-space placebo tests — applying the synthetic control method to each donor country as if it were the treated unit — to assess whether the estimated treatment effect for the actual treated country is unusually large relative to the distribution of placebo effects. This is the permutation inference approach that Lab 6's Moran's I analysis used for spatial autocorrelation.

**Exercise 4: Event-Study Extension.** Students estimate the spatial diffusion of conflict shocks by measuring the impact on neighboring economies (Jordan's GDP after the Syrian civil war began, Oman's economy after the Yemen conflict intensified) using a difference-in-differences framework with spatial spillover controls. The exercise connects the synthetic control methodology to the spatial econometrics of Chapter 3-A.

## Data in Depth: Measuring Diversification in Resource-Dependent Economies

**The question.** How diversified are MENA economies, and is diversification increasing over time?

**Data sources.**
- **World Bank World Development Indicators (WDI):** Oil rents as a percentage of GDP, manufacturing value-added, services value-added, merchandise export concentration (Herfindahl index).
- **IMF Article IV Reports:** Staff assessments of non-oil GDP growth, fiscal breakeven oil prices, and structural reform progress for individual MENA countries.
- **UNCTAD:** Export diversification indices, service trade data.
- **OECD STRI:** Regulatory restrictiveness for service sectors in MENA countries covered by the index.

**Construction.**
1. From WDI, download oil rents (% GDP), manufacturing VA (% GDP), services VA (% GDP), and the Herfindahl index of export concentration for all MENA countries, 2000–2023.
2. Compute a composite diversification index for each country-year:

$$
D_{it} = 1 - HHI_{it}^{exports}
$$

where $HHI_{it}^{exports}$ is the Herfindahl index of merchandise export concentration.

3. Plot the diversification index over time for Gulf states versus non-Gulf MENA countries (Turkey, Morocco, Tunisia, Jordan) to compare trajectories.
4. Compute the non-oil GDP share and its trend for each Gulf state.
5. Correlate diversification trends with SWF domestic investment data (where available) to test whether sovereign investment is associated with measurable diversification.

**Expected findings.** Gulf states should show persistently high export concentration (low diversification) relative to non-oil MENA countries. Within the Gulf, the UAE (particularly Dubai) should show the highest diversification, reflecting its services-hub strategy. Saudi Arabia should show a modest diversification trend beginning around 2016 (post-Vision 2030 launch), though the magnitude may be small relative to the still-dominant oil sector.

## Institutional Spotlight: The Public Investment Fund — Sovereign Wealth as National Strategy

The Public Investment Fund (PIF) of Saudi Arabia, established in 1971 as a development finance institution and transformed after 2015 into one of the world's largest sovereign wealth funds, is the most explicit example of a SWF deployed as an instrument of spatial economic transformation.

**The pre-2015 PIF** was a passive holding company for government stakes in domestic enterprises — Saudi Aramco, SABIC (the petrochemicals conglomerate), Saudi Telecom, the National Commercial Bank. Its role was custodial: it held assets that the government owned and received dividends that flowed to the treasury. It did not make investment decisions designed to shape the economy's structure or geography.

**The post-2015 PIF** is a different institution. Under Vision 2030, the PIF was given a mandate to grow its assets from approximately $150 billion (2015) to $2 trillion (target: 2030), to invest domestically in sectors that would diversify the economy away from oil dependence, and to serve as the financial vehicle for the government's mega-projects. The PIF's board is chaired by the Crown Prince, and its investment decisions reflect the government's strategic priorities rather than a purely financial optimization.

**The domestic investment program** includes: NEOM ($500 billion planned), The Red Sea ($5 billion phase 1), Qiddiya ($8 billion), Riyadh Air (a new national carrier to compete with Emirates), SEVEN (an entertainment company building multiplex cinemas and theme parks), and stakes in dozens of Saudi startups and growth companies. Each investment is spatially targeted: NEOM on the northwest coast, The Red Sea on the western coast, Qiddiya near Riyadh. The PIF is, in effect, the spatial planning authority for the largest physical transformation program in the history of the Middle East.

**The international investment program** is more conventional: stakes in Uber, Lucid Motors, Jio Platforms (India), NIO (Chinese electric vehicles), and various private equity and venture capital funds. These investments generate financial returns and create diplomatic and commercial linkages to the technology economies that Vision 2030 aims to emulate.

**The spatial policy lesson.** The PIF demonstrates that sovereign wealth funds can be powerful instruments of spatial economic policy — but only in states where the concentration of political authority allows the fund to be directed toward strategic rather than purely financial objectives. The PIF's effectiveness as a diversification tool depends on whether the mega-projects it funds can overcome the spatial economics challenges described in this chapter: the absence of market logic, the difficulty of engineering agglomeration, and the institutional weaknesses that the resource curse creates. The comparison with Norway's Government Pension Fund Global — which is explicitly prohibited from making domestic investments or pursuing non-financial objectives — illustrates the institutional choice between financial prudence and strategic ambition.

## Conclusion: The Spatial Paradox of Resource Wealth

Resource wealth creates a spatial paradox for MENA economies. The resource endowment generates income that can fund diversification investment — infrastructure, education, technology, urbanization — at a scale that few other developing regions can match. But the same resource endowment creates the institutional conditions (rentierism, Dutch Disease, spatial concentration, weak non-oil private sectors) that make diversification difficult. The Gulf states are simultaneously the best-funded and the most structurally constrained diversification projects in the world.

The evidence from this chapter suggests that institutional design mediates the relationship between resource wealth and spatial development more powerfully than any other factor. Australia's success in managing resource booms without succumbing to the curse reflects pre-existing institutional strength, not superior resource management technique. Dubai's success in building a services hub reflects deliberate institutional innovation (free zones, state-owned airlines, regulatory enclaves) that partially insulated the diversification effort from the distortions of the domestic economy. KAEC's failure reflects the absence of the market logic that institutional scaffolding cannot substitute for.

Chapter 12 turns to the other pole of MENA's spatial economics: the fragile states where conflict has destroyed the institutional fabric necessary for economic activity, and where the spatial economics of reconstruction must contend with ongoing violence, displaced populations, and the collapse of the data infrastructure needed to diagnose the problem.
