# Chapter 10: The North-South Divide and Dis-Integration

---

## Introduction: When Integration Reverses

Drive south from Valencia along the AP-7 motorway and you pass through what might be called the archaeology of the eurozone crisis. The *urbanizaciones* of Castellón province — vast housing developments built during Spain's construction boom of 2000–2008 — sit half-completed and largely empty, their unfinished concrete shells and abandoned cranes a physical record of capital misallocation on a continental scale. At the peak, Spain was building more houses per year than Germany, France, and the UK combined, financed by capital flows from northern European banks seeking higher yields in a newly unified monetary space. When the capital stopped flowing, construction employment in the Valencian Community fell by 60 percent, the regional unemployment rate exceeded 28 percent, and the mortgage defaults that followed threatened not only Spanish banks but the German and French institutions that had funded the lending. The abandoned *urbanizaciones* of Castellón are not a Spanish problem; they are a spatial manifestation of a eurozone-wide institutional design flaw.

The European convergence story told in Chapter 9 has a shadow narrative. While Poland, the Czech Republic, and the Baltic states converged toward Western European income levels during the 2000s and 2010s, a different dynamic unfolded along the Mediterranean periphery. Greece's GDP per capita, which had reached 94 percent of the EU average by 2008, collapsed to 67 percent by 2014 — a divergence of a magnitude more commonly associated with war or state failure than with membership in the world's most advanced regional integration project. Spain's unemployment rate peaked at 26 percent in 2013; youth unemployment in Andalusia and Extremadura exceeded 55 percent. Italy's Mezzogiorno — the six southern regions that have been the object of development policy since the Cassa per il Mezzogiorno was established in 1950 — experienced negative growth for most of the 2010s, and in 2023 the GDP per capita of Calabria stood at roughly 55 percent of the EU average, barely higher than in 2000.

This chapter examines three dimensions of European dis-integration — forces that have fragmented, reversed, or disrupted the convergence process described in Chapter 9. Section 10.1 analyzes the eurozone crisis as an institutional mismatch between a monetary union and a collection of divergent fiscal and labor market regimes. Section 10.2 examines post-socialist integration — the distinctly spatial pattern by which Central and Eastern European economies have integrated into Western European production networks, creating a "Factory Germany" manufacturing belt and a parallel nearshore services hub that represents a different kind of integration from the Cohesion Fund-driven convergence of Section 9.2. Section 10.3 turns to Brexit as a natural experiment in dis-integration: the deliberate severing of a major economy from the Single Market, motivated by the "geography of discontent" in regions that integration had left behind.

The common thread is that integration creates both winners and losers, and that the losers — the Mediterranean periphery, the deindustrialized regions of northern England and Wales, the aging rural communities of southern Italy — are geographically concentrated in ways that generate political pressure for dis-integration. The spatial economics framework of earlier chapters — agglomeration, institutional distance, the gravity model — explains why integration's benefits are unevenly distributed. This chapter shows what happens when the political consequences of that uneven distribution feed back into the integration process itself.

The analytical logic connects directly to Chapters 1 and 2. Chapter 1's NEG framework predicts that reducing trade costs between unequal regions can, under specific parameter conditions, accelerate spatial concentration rather than promote convergence — the "core-periphery" outcome where mobile factors relocate toward the larger, more productive region, leaving the periphery with fewer resources to compete. Chapter 2's institutional framework explains why: convergence requires not just market access but absorptive capacity — the human capital, governance quality, and institutional thickness that enable regions to exploit the opportunities that integration creates. Where those institutional foundations are absent, integration's centripetal forces dominate its centrifugal ones, and the result is not convergence but polarization. Each of this chapter's three case studies — the eurozone crisis, post-socialist integration, and Brexit — illustrates a different facet of this dynamic.

## 10.1 The Eurozone Crisis: Monetary Union Without Fiscal Union

### The Architecture of Asymmetry

The euro, introduced in 1999 as a unit of account and in 2002 as physical currency, was designed to complete the Single Market by eliminating exchange rate risk and reducing transaction costs for cross-border trade and investment. The theory — rooted in Mundell's (1961) Optimal Currency Area (OCA) framework — held that a common currency would generate endogenous convergence: by increasing trade integration and synchronizing business cycles, the euro would gradually create the conditions that would make the eurozone a well-functioning currency area, even if it was not one at inception.

The OCA theory identifies several prerequisites for a successful currency union: labor mobility (so that workers can move from depressed to booming regions), fiscal transfers (so that a central budget can cushion asymmetric shocks), price and wage flexibility (so that adjustment can occur through internal devaluation rather than exchange rate changes), and business cycle synchronization (so that a single monetary policy is appropriate for all members). The eurozone met none of these criteria adequately at inception, and — contrary to the "endogenous OCA" hypothesis — it has not converged substantially toward meeting them in the quarter-century since. Labor mobility across European countries was and remains far lower than labor mobility across US states: approximately 4 percent of eurozone citizens live in a member state other than their country of birth, compared to roughly 30 percent of Americans who live in a state other than their state of birth. Language barriers, cultural differences, non-portable pension rights, housing market frictions, and the sheer psychological cost of leaving one's national community all impede cross-border migration. Fiscal transfers were limited to the Cohesion Fund mechanism described in Chapter 9, which was designed for long-term convergence rather than short-term stabilization. Price and wage flexibility was constrained by labor market institutions — collective bargaining agreements, employment protection legislation, minimum wages — that varied enormously across member states. And business cycle synchronization, far from being endogenous, proved to be contingent on the very asymmetric shocks that the currency union was supposed to manage.

Krugman (1993), writing before the euro's launch, warned precisely about this scenario. His argument was that monetary union would increase, not decrease, the specialization of member state economies — because the elimination of exchange rate risk would encourage firms to concentrate production in the location with the strongest comparative advantage rather than diversifying across countries as a hedge against currency fluctuations. Greater specialization would in turn increase the frequency and severity of asymmetric shocks (a recession in the auto sector would hit Germany disproportionately; a collapse in tourism would hit Greece and Spain), while the common currency would eliminate the shock absorber (exchange rate adjustment) that had previously cushioned these asymmetries. The argument was largely ignored in the political enthusiasm for monetary union, but events would vindicate it almost perfectly.

The convergence play that followed the euro's introduction seemed, for a decade, to refute the skeptics. Interest rate spreads between German and peripheral government bonds compressed from approximately 600 basis points in the early 1990s to fewer than 20 basis points by 2007 — a convergence that markets interpreted as reflecting the elimination of exchange rate risk and the implicit fiscal guarantee that shared currency membership was thought to provide. Capital flowed massively from northern Europe (where aging populations and mature economies generated excess savings) to the periphery (where younger demographics, housing shortages, and catch-up growth generated excess investment demand). Spain's current account deficit reached 10 percent of GDP in 2007; Greece's exceeded 15 percent. Germany, simultaneously, ran a current account surplus exceeding 6 percent of GDP — the mirror image, within a currency union, of the peripheral deficits. The spatial economics of this flow were clear: a monetary union without fiscal transfers or labor mobility was channeling adjustment through capital markets, and the capital markets were channeling it toward real estate speculation and consumption rather than productivity-enhancing investment.

The spatial economics of the eurozone's design flaw are straightforward. When Germany and the Netherlands experienced export booms in the early 2000s (driven by wage moderation, the Hartz labor market reforms, and the competitive advantages of their manufacturing sectors in a globalized economy), the ECB's single interest rate was too low for their conditions — fueling asset price inflation in already-booming economies. When Greece, Spain, and Ireland experienced capital inflows and credit expansions (driven by the elimination of exchange rate risk and the compression of interest rate spreads after euro adoption), the same interest rate was too low for their conditions — fueling housing bubbles and current account deficits that would prove unsustainable. The single monetary policy was procyclical in both directions: too loose for the periphery during the boom, and too tight for the periphery during the bust.

### The Crisis and the Spatial Pattern of Adjustment

The 2010–2015 eurozone crisis was triggered by the revelation that Greece's fiscal deficits were substantially larger than previously reported, but the underlying cause was the cumulative divergence of competitiveness across eurozone members during the pre-crisis boom. Between 1999 and 2008, unit labor costs in Germany fell by approximately 15 percent relative to the eurozone average (through wage moderation and productivity gains), while unit labor costs in Greece, Spain, Portugal, and Italy rose by 20–30 percent relative to the average (through wage increases that outpaced productivity growth). Under a flexible exchange rate regime, the periphery countries would have devalued their currencies, restoring competitiveness at the cost of higher import prices. Under the euro, devaluation was impossible, and adjustment had to occur through "internal devaluation" — cutting wages, reducing public spending, and deflating the domestic economy until competitiveness was restored.

The spatial consequences of this adjustment were devastating. Internal devaluation operates through unemployment: wages fall when labor markets are slack, and labor markets become slack through recession. The regions most affected were those with the weakest labor markets to begin with — the Mediterranean periphery, where pre-crisis unemployment was already high, where the construction sector (which had boomed during the housing bubble) represented a disproportionate share of employment, and where public sector employment (which contracted as fiscal austerity took hold) was a major source of middle-class income. The spatial dimension of internal devaluation is often underappreciated: unlike a nominal devaluation (which affects all wages and prices simultaneously across the national territory), internal devaluation operates through sector-specific and region-specific unemployment. The regions where wages fall first and furthest are the regions with the least bargaining power, the most elastic labor supply, and the weakest institutional protections — typically the periphery, where small firms, informal employment, and weak unions predominate.

Greece is the most extreme case. Between 2008 and 2013, Greek GDP fell by approximately 25 percent in real terms — a contraction larger than the US experienced during the Great Depression. The spatial distribution of the shock was uneven even within Greece: Athens, as the national capital with a diversified service economy, recovered faster than the peripheral regions (Thessaly, Western Greece, North Aegean) that depended on agriculture, tourism, and public sector employment. Youth unemployment in some Greek regions exceeded 60 percent — levels that raised questions about whether an entire generation's human capital formation had been permanently impaired by the crisis. The result was a dual divergence: Greece diverged from the eurozone core, and Greece's periphery diverged from Athens — a pattern that mirrors the within-country inequality dynamics described for India in Chapter 8 and for the CEE accession states in Section 10.2.

Spain experienced a similar but less extreme pattern. The construction bust eliminated approximately 2 million jobs in the sector between 2008 and 2013, concentrated in the regions (Andalusia, Valencia, Murcia) where construction had been most intensive during the boom. The spatial pattern of recovery was equally uneven: Madrid, Barcelona, and the Basque Country recovered fastest (driven by business services, technology, and advanced manufacturing), while the southern and interior regions — Extremadura, Castilla-La Mancha, the Canary Islands — experienced prolonged stagnation. The Basque Country's resilience — its unemployment rate peaked at 16 percent, compared to 36 percent in Andalusia — reflects the institutional thickness that Chapter 9's VoC analysis would predict: the Mondragon cooperative network absorbed demand shocks through wage flexibility rather than layoffs; the Basque government's fiscal autonomy (a unique feature of the Spanish constitutional settlement) allowed countercyclical spending when the central government was consolidating; and the region's diversified manufacturing base was less exposed to the construction cycle than the southern regions that had bet heavily on tourism and real estate.

Italy's experience was less dramatic in annual terms but more corrosive over time. Italy's GDP per capita in 2023 was barely above its 1999 level in real terms — a quarter-century of near-zero growth that constitutes a lost generation in economic terms. The spatial distribution of this stagnation was profoundly uneven: Lombardy, Veneto, and Emilia-Romagna — northern regions integrated into European and global manufacturing networks — maintained unemployment rates of 6–8 percent and modest positive growth through much of the period. Calabria, Campania, and Sicily experienced unemployment rates above 20 percent (and youth unemployment above 50 percent) for most of the 2010s, with GDP per capita in Calabria falling to approximately 55 percent of the EU average — a level that, in any other context, would qualify the region for the most intensive category of development assistance. The Italian case demonstrates that internal devaluation operates asymmetrically within countries as well as between them: the same austerity policies that allowed northern Italian firms to regain competitiveness through cost compression devastated the labor-intensive, public-sector-dependent economies of the south.

The emigration channel added a further spatial dimension to the crisis. Approximately 500,000 Greeks — disproportionately young, educated, and professionally qualified — left the country between 2010 and 2019, with Germany, the UK, and Australia as the primary destinations. Spain and Italy experienced comparable outflows: an estimated 700,000 Spaniards and over one million Italians emigrated during the crisis decade. These flows represented a transfer of human capital from the periphery to the core that compounded the economic divergence: the regions that lost their most productive workers were precisely the regions that most needed productive workers to generate the growth that would restore fiscal sustainability. Portugal's trajectory, by contrast, illustrates that internal devaluation can eventually work — but at enormous cost. After implementing one of the deepest austerity programs in the eurozone and experiencing GDP contractions through 2013, Portugal's economy recovered through a combination of tourism growth (which exploited the country's newly competitive cost structure), technology sector expansion (Lisbon emerged as a European startup hub), and export diversification. By 2023, Portugal's GDP per capita had recovered to its pre-crisis level — a modest achievement that nonetheless took fifteen years and cost a generation of emigration and forgone investment.

### The Institutional Response: Banking Union and Next Generation EU

The eurozone's institutional response to the crisis has been incremental but significant. The Banking Union (2014) created a Single Supervisory Mechanism (transferring supervision of the eurozone's 120 largest banks from national regulators to the ECB) and a Single Resolution Mechanism for failing banks, partially addressing the "doom loop" between sovereign debt and banking crises that had amplified the peripheral crisis. The doom loop operated through a vicious cycle: when sovereign creditworthiness declined, the value of government bonds held by domestic banks fell, weakening banks' balance sheets; when banks needed recapitalization, the fiscal cost fell on the sovereign, further weakening its creditworthiness. This feedback mechanism was particularly devastating in countries like Ireland (where bank bailout costs amounted to approximately 40 percent of GDP) and Spain (where the Bankia rescue crystallized the connection between a real estate bubble and a sovereign debt crisis). The European Stability Mechanism (ESM) established a permanent fiscal backstop for member states facing funding crises. And most significantly, the Next Generation EU recovery fund (2020) — a €750 billion program financed by common EU debt — represented the first step toward the fiscal transfer capacity that the euro's architects had deferred in 1992.

The spatial economics of Next Generation EU are revealing. The fund's allocation formula directs the largest per-capita transfers to the countries most affected by COVID-19 and with the lowest pre-pandemic GDP per capita — effectively Italy, Spain, Greece, and the Central and Eastern European accession states. Italy is the largest recipient (approximately €191 billion in grants and loans), and the Italian government's National Recovery and Resilience Plan directs 40 percent of spending to the Mezzogiorno — a deliberate attempt to use the COVID recovery as a vehicle for addressing the North-South divide that has defined Italian regional economics since unification.

The detail of Mario Draghi's intervention in July 2012 deserves attention, because it illustrates how institutional improvisation can substitute — temporarily — for institutional design. Draghi's three words ("whatever it takes") were not backed by any existing treaty provision, any formal ECB program, or any authorization from national governments. They represented a unilateral commitment by the central bank to act as lender of last resort for sovereign debt — a function that the ECB's founding treaty had explicitly excluded. The OMT program that followed was challenged in the German Constitutional Court and referred to the European Court of Justice, which upheld it only by interpreting the ECB's mandate expansively. The episode demonstrates that the eurozone's institutional architecture has been completed not by treaty revision (which requires unanimity and is politically impossible on fiscal matters) but by crisis-driven improvisation — a pattern that Chapter 2's institutional analysis would recognize as path-dependent institutional evolution under constraint.

The Fiscal Compact (2012) — which required member states to enshrine balanced-budget rules in national legislation — represented the German-led response to the crisis: tighter fiscal discipline to prevent future divergence. But the Compact's procyclical logic (forcing austerity on countries in recession) intensified the spatial divergence it was meant to prevent, and by the mid-2010s even the European Commission was applying the rules with increasing flexibility, recognizing that rigid fiscal targets were incompatible with recovery.

Next Generation EU (NGEU), agreed in July 2020, represented a far more significant institutional innovation. The €750 billion fund — €390 billion in grants, €360 billion in loans — was financed by common EU debt, marking the first time the Union had borrowed collectively on capital markets. The governance structure required each recipient country to submit a National Recovery and Resilience Plan with specific milestones (527 milestones and targets for Italy alone), creating a framework of conditionality that resembled an IMF program more than a traditional Cohesion Fund allocation. The comparison to US fiscal federalism is instructive: in the United States, automatic fiscal stabilizers — unemployment insurance, progressive federal taxation, Medicaid — transfer approximately 30 cents of every dollar of state-level income loss to the federal level, cushioning asymmetric shocks without political negotiation. The eurozone's equivalent mechanism, even after NGEU, transfers less than 5 cents — a gap that explains why eurozone recessions are deeper and more spatially concentrated than American ones (see Chapter 4).

Whether Next Generation EU represents a permanent shift toward fiscal union or a one-off emergency response remains to be seen. Germany, the Netherlands, Austria, and the other "frugal" member states agreed to common debt issuance only under the extraordinary circumstances of a global pandemic, and they have resisted suggestions that the mechanism should be repeated or made permanent. The political economy of fiscal union — which requires net contributors to accept permanent transfers to net recipients without the democratic accountability that national fiscal systems provide — remains the fundamental obstacle to completing the eurozone's institutional architecture.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: Measuring Real Exchange Rate Divergence Within a Currency Union**
>
> In a flexible exchange rate regime, competitiveness differences between countries are partly reflected in the nominal exchange rate: an uncompetitive country's currency depreciates, providing an automatic (if partial) adjustment mechanism. Within a currency union, the nominal exchange rate is fixed at unity, and competitiveness divergence must be measured through the real effective exchange rate (REER) — which incorporates differences in unit labor costs or consumer prices relative to trading partners.
>
> The challenge is that REER measures depend on which price index is used. A REER based on consumer prices (HICP) captures the cost of living but may be influenced by non-tradable goods prices and tax changes. A REER based on unit labor costs (ULC) captures production costs but depends on how labor costs and productivity are measured — and both vary significantly depending on sector composition and the treatment of self-employment. The Eurostat ULC-based REER showed Greek competitiveness deteriorating by 30 percent between 1999 and 2009, but the magnitude depends on whether manufacturing or whole-economy ULC is used, and whether productivity is measured per worker or per hour.
>
> For spatial analysis at the sub-national level, the data challenges multiply. Regional price levels within the eurozone are available from Eurostat's regional purchasing power parities but only for selected years and with limited sectoral detail. Regional ULC data must be constructed from separate employment and output series that may use different methodological standards. The result is that the real exchange rate divergence that drove the eurozone crisis can be measured with reasonable confidence at the country level but only approximately at the regional level. A further complication arises from the Balassa-Samuelson effect: countries undergoing real convergence (rising productivity in the tradable sector) will experience rising relative prices in the non-tradable sector (because wages in non-tradables rise to match tradables, but productivity in non-tradables grows more slowly). This means that some of the measured "competitiveness deterioration" in peripheral countries may actually reflect catch-up growth rather than misalignment — a distinction with significant policy implications. If rising relative prices in Greece and Spain between 1999 and 2008 reflected genuine convergence-driven Balassa-Samuelson effects, the appropriate policy response was to wait for productivity to catch up to wages; if they reflected unsustainable wage inflation driven by credit booms, the appropriate response was early intervention to cool domestic demand. The data did not — and arguably could not — distinguish between these two interpretations in real time.

## 10.2 Post-Socialist Integration: Factory Germany and the Nearshore Services Belt

### The Manufacturing Track: Visegrád as Factory Germany's Workshop

The integration of Central and Eastern European (CEE) economies into the EU after the 2004 and 2007 enlargements followed a distinctive spatial pattern. The manufacturing-intensive Visegrád countries — Poland, the Czech Republic, Slovakia, and Hungary — integrated primarily through Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) in manufacturing, driven by Western European (predominantly German) firms seeking lower-cost production locations within the Single Market. The pattern resembles the flying-geese model described in Chapter 6, but with a critical difference: the "geese" are flying within a single regulatory and institutional framework rather than across sovereign boundaries, and the labor-cost advantage of the CEE countries is partially offset by lower productivity, weaker infrastructure, and the adjustment costs of post-socialist institutional transformation. Unlike the East Asian developmental states, which used strategic industrial policy to channel FDI toward technology acquisition and domestic capability-building (Chapter 6's account of Korea's MOTIE and Taiwan's ITRI), the CEE countries integrated primarily as recipients of FDI on terms set by the investing firms — a distinction with significant implications for whether the convergence is self-sustaining or dependent on continued cost advantages.

The speed and scale of this integration were remarkable. Between 2000 and 2015, the Visegrád countries received approximately €200 billion in cumulative FDI inflows, the overwhelming majority from Western European (and especially German, Austrian, and Dutch) firms. By 2023, foreign-owned firms accounted for over 50 percent of manufacturing output in the Czech Republic and Slovakia — a level of foreign ownership that would have been politically unthinkable in Western European economies and that distinguishes the CEE integration model from both the German CME (where the Mittelstand is domestically owned) and the East Asian developmental state (where foreign investment was welcomed but strategically channeled to build domestic capabilities).

The spatial economics of "Factory Germany" — the network of German-controlled supply chains that extends from Stuttgart and Munich through the Czech Republic, Slovakia, and into Poland and Hungary — are classic NEG. German automotive firms (Volkswagen, BMW, Daimler, Audi) and their tier-1 suppliers located assembly and component manufacturing in CEE regions that offered the combination of lower wages, adequate infrastructure (particularly proximity to the Autobahn network and its extensions), a skilled workforce (the legacy of communist-era technical education), and institutional stability (post-accession regulatory alignment with EU standards). The resulting spatial pattern is a manufacturing corridor that runs from Bavaria through Saxony into western Bohemia, through the Bratislava-Vienna-Budapest triangle, and into the Wielkopolska and Silesia regions of Poland.

The convergence effects have been remarkable. Slovakia's automotive sector, which barely existed in 1990, produced over one million vehicles in 2019 — more per capita than any other country in the world. The Czech Republic's GDP per capita reached 92 percent of the EU average by 2023, up from approximately 70 percent at accession in 2004. Poland's economic transformation is the single most successful convergence story in the post-2004 EU — and perhaps the most successful in any post-communist economy. Between 2004 and 2023, Polish real GDP more than doubled, poverty rates halved, infrastructure was transformed (Poland built over 4,000 km of motorways and expressways, largely financed by Cohesion Funds), and a genuine consumer middle class emerged. Poland is the only EU member state that avoided recession during the 2008–2009 global financial crisis — a resilience attributable to its diversified economic structure, the flexible exchange rate of the złoty (Poland chose not to adopt the euro), and the countercyclical stimulus that Cohesion Fund flows provided during the downturn.

But the spatial distribution of these gains within the CEE countries mirrors the pattern described for India in Chapter 8. The regions that have converged fastest are the national capitals (Prague, Warsaw, Bratislava, Budapest) and the western regions adjacent to the German and Austrian borders. Eastern Poland, eastern Hungary, and the rural peripheries of all four Visegrád countries have lagged behind, creating within-country inequality patterns that resemble the Italian Mezzogiorno rather than the German model of distributed specialization.

Nölke and Vliegenthart (2009) conceptualize this pattern as a "Dependent Market Economy" (DME) — a distinctive variety of capitalism where the coordinating mechanism is neither the market (as in LMEs) nor domestic institutions (as in CMEs) but rather the strategic decisions of foreign multinational corporations. In a DME, the key complementary institution is not the apprenticeship system or the stock market but the FDI regime: the combination of low corporate taxes, flexible labor regulations, investment incentives, and EU regulatory alignment that attracts foreign manufacturing investment. The spatial consequence is that the geography of economic activity is determined not by domestic institutional networks (as in Germany's Mittelstand model) but by the locational preferences of foreign firms — preferences that favor proximity to the western border, access to motorway networks, and availability of technical labor, creating a steep west-to-east gradient within each country.

The spatial granularity of the automotive integration is revealing. The Czech Republic's auto production is not diffused across the country in the German pattern but concentrated in a narrow corridor: Škoda's main plant at Mladá Boleslav (60 km northeast of Prague), Hyundai's facility at Nošovice (in the Moravian-Silesian region, chosen for its proximity to the Polish and Slovak borders and its legacy of heavy industrial workforce), and the TPCA joint venture (Toyota-Peugeot-Citroën) at Kolín. Tier-1 and tier-2 suppliers cluster around these anchor plants in a spatial pattern that replicates the keiretsu-style supply chain geography described in Chapter 6 for Japan and Korea, but with the critical difference that the controlling firms are foreign — meaning that the highest-value functions (design, R&D, brand management, strategic planning) remain in Wolfsburg, Munich, and Stuttgart rather than migrating to the CEE production sites.

The EV transition poses a fundamental threat to this integration model. An internal combustion engine contains approximately 2,000 moving parts; a battery electric vehicle contains roughly 200. The CEE supply chain that produces gaskets, fuel injection systems, exhaust components, and transmission parts faces technological obsolescence as the European market transitions to electric vehicles under the EU's 2035 ban on new internal combustion engine sales. The spatial implications are severe: regions that built their convergence on automotive component manufacturing — Mladá Boleslav, Trnava (Slovakia), Győr (Hungary) — must either attract battery and EV component production (which has different locational requirements, including access to critical minerals and gigafactory-scale facilities) or face deindustrialization. The parallel to the flying-geese dynamics of Chapter 6 is direct: just as the shift from DRAM to foundry logic disrupted the established spatial hierarchy of East Asian semiconductor production, the shift from ICE to EV disrupts the spatial hierarchy of European automotive production.

Wage convergence has been real but incomplete. Average manufacturing wages in the Visegrád countries have risen from approximately 20 percent of the German level in 2004 to 35–40 percent by 2023 — substantial progress, but a gap that remains wide enough to sustain the cost advantage that drives the integration model. The question is whether the convergence will continue to the point where the cost advantage disappears (as happened with Japan and Korea relative to the US, as Chapter 6 describes) or whether institutional differences — weaker trade unions, less generous social insurance, more flexible labor regulations — will sustain a permanent wage differential within the Single Market. The political dynamics are already shifting: as CEE wages rise and labor markets tighten (Polish unemployment fell below 3 percent in 2023, creating genuine labor scarcity in manufacturing), some firms have begun considering further eastern relocation — to Ukraine (before the war), Moldova, or even non-EU North Africa — while others have invested in automation to reduce their dependence on low-cost labor. The spatial economics of "Factory Germany" may thus be entering a new phase: from labor arbitrage within the EU to a more complex pattern of automation, near-shoring, and selective re-shoring that reflects both rising CEE costs and the geopolitical imperative (sharpened by Russia's invasion of Ukraine and COVID supply chain disruptions) to reduce supply chain vulnerability.

### The Services Track: CEE as Europe's Nearshore Services Hub

Alongside the manufacturing integration described above, a parallel and less analyzed integration has occurred in business services. Warsaw, Bucharest, Prague, Budapest, Kraków, Wrocław, and Brno have emerged as nearshore services hubs — hosting shared services centers (SSCs), business process outsourcing (BPO) operations, and IT development centers for Western European and global firms. This is the European counterpart to the Indian IT-BPO sector described in Chapter 8, but operating within the Single Market's institutional framework rather than across an institutional divide.

The nearshore services model has distinctive spatial economics. Unlike the offshore model (Bangalore serving New York across a 12-hour time zone difference), the nearshore model operates within one to two time zones of the client (Warsaw to London, Bucharest to Frankfurt), enabling real-time collaboration and same-day travel for face-to-face meetings when needed. The workers are university-educated, multilingual (English plus the client's language — a significant advantage when the client is German, French, or Scandinavian), and available at wage levels 40–60 percent below Western European equivalents.

The functional composition of CEE nearshore services has evolved along the same trajectory as India's — from routine BPO (data entry, transaction processing, customer service) to higher-value functions (financial analysis, risk management, software development, R&D). Kraków's shared services sector, for example, has evolved from routine accounting and payroll processing (for firms like Capgemini, Shell, and HSBC in the mid-2000s) to advanced analytics, cybersecurity operations, and AI development (for Google, IBM, and Cisco in the 2020s). The smile curve dynamics of Chapter 8 apply directly: as CEE nearshore centers move up the value chain, they become more spatially concentrated in cities with the strongest university systems and deepest talent pools.

The scale of the CEE nearshore services sector is now substantial. Poland alone hosts approximately 430,000 workers in shared services centers and BPO operations, spread across more than 1,700 centers — making it the largest nearshore services hub in Europe and comparable in scale to mid-tier Indian IT cities like Pune or Hyderabad. The institutional enablers of this growth are distinctive: EU membership guarantees regulatory alignment and data protection compliance (critical for financial services outsourcing under GDPR); the Bologna Process produces a steady flow of university graduates with English proficiency and technical skills; and the time-zone proximity to Western European clients — a one-hour difference between Warsaw and Frankfurt, compared to the four-and-a-half-hour difference between Bangalore and London — enables synchronous collaboration that the offshore model cannot replicate.

The spatial concentration within Warsaw is itself revealing. The Mokotów district, south of the city center, has emerged as Warsaw's equivalent of Bangalore's Electronic City — a dense cluster of glass-and-steel office towers housing the European operations centers of Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Standard Chartered, Procter & Gamble, and Samsung, among dozens of others. The agglomeration economics are classic: the concentration of financial services talent in Mokotów attracts more financial services firms, which attract more specialized training programs at local universities (the Warsaw School of Economics has redesigned its curriculum around quantitative finance and data analytics), which produce more qualified graduates, which attract more firms. The result is a localized ecosystem that Chapter 1's increasing returns framework would predict — and that is extremely difficult for smaller Polish cities to replicate.

Kraków's trajectory illustrates the upgrading dynamics. The city's shared services sector began in the early 2000s with routine accounting and payroll processing — functions that required little more than English proficiency and basic numeracy. By the mid-2010s, firms like Capgemini, Shell, and HSBC had upgraded their Kraków operations to include financial analysis, risk modeling, and regulatory compliance. By the 2020s, Google, IBM, and Cisco were running advanced analytics, cybersecurity operations, and AI development from Kraków offices, drawn by the Jagiellonian University's computer science and mathematics programs and the density of technical talent that two decades of BPO growth had cultivated. The trajectory mirrors the Indian IT sector's evolution from body-shopping to product development described in Chapter 8, but compressed into a shorter timeframe and operating within the Single Market's institutional framework.

The coexistence of manufacturing and services integration within the same countries creates a distinctive labor market tension. Manufacturing FDI in automotive and component production competes with services FDI for the same pool of technically educated workers — and as services wages have risen faster than manufacturing wages (reflecting the higher margins and lower capital intensity of knowledge-intensive services), the services sector has begun to draw talent away from manufacturing, potentially undermining the cost advantage that attracted manufacturing FDI in the first place.

The services-track integration creates a different set of inter-regional linkages than the manufacturing track. Manufacturing integration links CEE factory towns to German corporate headquarters through physical supply chains — components flow east to west and finished goods flow west to east. Services integration links CEE metropolitan office complexes to Western European corporate functions through digital channels — financial data flows west to east for processing and returns east to west as analysis. The two tracks serve different parts of the European economy, employ different workers, and have different spatial footprints.

## 10.3 Brexit and the Geography of Discontent

### The Spatial Pattern of the Vote

The June 23, 2016 referendum in which 51.9 percent of British voters chose to leave the European Union was, at its core, a spatial economic event. The geography of the Leave vote maps almost perfectly onto the geography of economic marginalization described by the "left-behind" literature (Rodríguez-Pose 2018, McCann 2016). Leave vote shares were highest in the deindustrialized towns of northern England and the Midlands (Stoke-on-Trent: 69.4 percent Leave; Mansfield: 70.9 percent; Boston, Lincolnshire: 75.6 percent) and in the coastal communities and rural areas of eastern England. Remain vote shares were highest in London (59.9 percent Remain), university cities (Cambridge, Oxford, Bristol, Edinburgh), and areas with younger, more educated, and more ethnically diverse populations.

The spatial economics of the Leave vote can be decomposed into three channels, each connected to the theoretical frameworks of this book. First, **trade exposure**: regions whose tradable sectors competed directly with EU imports (manufacturing, agriculture) were more likely to vote Leave than regions whose tradable sectors benefited from EU market access (financial services, professional services, higher education). The Leave vote was concentrated in regions that had experienced net employment losses from trade integration, not in regions that had experienced net gains. Second, **fiscal transfers**: the UK was a net contributor to the EU budget, and the Leave campaign's claim that the UK sent "£350 million a week" to Brussels (a figure that omitted the UK's rebate and the EU spending that flowed back to the UK) resonated particularly in regions that perceived themselves as receiving little benefit from EU membership. Third, **immigration exposure**: the 2004 enlargement and subsequent migration from the A8 countries created visible demographic change in regions — particularly the agricultural communities of eastern England — that had not previously experienced significant immigration.

Goodwin and Heath (2016) demonstrate that of these channels, educational composition was the single most powerful predictor of the Leave vote at the local authority level — more powerful than income, unemployment, age, or immigration exposure. Areas where fewer than 25 percent of adults held a degree voted Leave by an average margin of 25 percentage points; areas where more than 40 percent held a degree voted Remain by an average margin of 20 points. The education gradient captures multiple mechanisms simultaneously: lower education correlates with employment in tradable sectors exposed to import competition, with lower geographic mobility (and therefore less benefit from freedom of movement), with lower exposure to the cosmopolitan networks that Erasmus and Bologna created (Chapter 9), and with stronger attachment to local identity and place.

Los, McCann, Springford, and Thissen (2017) identify a paradox that deepens the spatial analysis: the regions that voted most heavily for Leave were, in many cases, the regions most economically dependent on EU trade. Sunderland — which voted 61.3 percent Leave — was home to the Nissan automobile plant, the UK's largest car factory, producing 500,000 vehicles per year almost entirely for EU export. The workers whose jobs depended most directly on Single Market access voted to leave it. The paradox dissolves once one recognizes that the relevant comparison is not between regions' current EU trade exposure and their vote, but between their *trajectory* — whether they perceived their economic prospects as improving or declining — and their vote. Sunderland's economy had improved relative to the 1980s (when shipbuilding collapsed) but had not converged toward London's prosperity; the Nissan plant provided employment but not the rising wages and expanding opportunities that integration was supposed to deliver. The vote was, in this reading, a verdict not on EU membership per se but on the spatial distribution of its benefits.

### Dis-Integration as a Natural Experiment

Brexit provides a natural experiment — imperfect, as all natural experiments are — in the economic consequences of severing a major economy from a regional integration project. The experiment is particularly informative for services trade, because the UK was the EU's largest services exporter — running a services trade surplus with the EU of approximately £30 billion per year before the referendum — and the Single Market for services was the framework through which British firms (especially in London) served European clients. Unlike goods trade, where the UK-EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA, agreed December 2020) preserved tariff-free and quota-free access, services trade received virtually no substantive provisions in the TCA — a deliberate choice by both sides that reflected the difficulty of negotiating services access outside the Single Market framework and the EU's determination to prevent the UK from retaining Single Market benefits without accepting its obligations.

The UK's exit from the EU came in two stages — the political departure on January 31, 2020, and the economic departure at the end of the transition period on December 31, 2020 — and the timing, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic, has complicated efforts to isolate Brexit's effects from the pandemic's. Nevertheless, the structural changes to UK-EU economic relations are clear, and they provide evidence on several theoretical questions that spatial economics has debated in the abstract but rarely observed in practice: What happens to services trade when institutional distance increases suddenly? How quickly do agglomeration economies unravel when regulatory barriers are imposed? Does the polycentric redistribution of functions from a dominant center prove stable or transitional?

The immediate consequence was the loss of "passporting" rights — the regulatory mechanism by which a financial services firm authorized in one EU member state could provide services throughout the Single Market without separate authorization in each country. Before Brexit, a bank licensed by the UK's Financial Conduct Authority could operate across all 27 EU member states; after Brexit, it needed separate authorization in each. The passporting loss forced financial institutions to establish EU-authorized subsidiaries, relocating staff, capital, and legal entities to EU member states.

The relocation pattern illuminated the gravity forces that operate in services trade. Financial services firms did not relocate randomly — they chose destinations based on a combination of regulatory environment (proximity to the ECB and EU financial regulators), language (English-speaking Dublin attracted technology and fintech), existing institutional infrastructure (Frankfurt's Finanzplatz, Amsterdam's trading platforms), and government incentives (Paris offered tax breaks and regulatory fast-tracking through the "Choose Paris" initiative). Each city attracted a specific functional niche:

- **Dublin:** Technology company headquarters, insurance, fintech. Advantages: English language, common law tradition, low corporate tax rate, existing technology cluster.
- **Frankfurt:** Banking operations requiring ECB proximity, euro clearing, settlement and custody. Advantages: ECB and Single Supervisory Mechanism headquarters, established banking infrastructure, BaFin regulatory relationship.
- **Amsterdam:** Trading platforms, electronic market-making, treasury operations. Advantages: existing trading infrastructure (Euronext Amsterdam), English proficiency, liberal business environment.
- **Paris:** Asset management, advisory services, corporate banking. Advantages: aggressive government attraction campaign, large existing financial sector, cultural prestige.
- **Luxembourg:** Fund management, back-office operations, holding company structures. Advantages: existing fund domiciliation infrastructure, favorable regulatory environment, multilingual workforce.

The result is a polycentric redistribution of functions that no single city orchestrated. The services geography that had been monocentrically organized around London became polycentrically distributed across five continental cities, each specializing in different functions. The total volume relocated — an estimated 7,000–10,000 jobs and roughly €1 trillion in assets — was significant but represented a fraction of London's financial services employment (approximately 400,000), suggesting that London retained a substantial critical mass even after the Single Market exit.

Springford (2022) provides the most rigorous aggregate estimate of Brexit's economic impact using a synthetic control method — constructing a "doppelgänger UK" from a weighted combination of countries that best matched the UK's pre-referendum economic trajectory and comparing actual UK GDP to the counterfactual. His central estimate is that by 2022, UK GDP was approximately 5 percent below where it would have been without Brexit — a cumulative loss equivalent to roughly £100 billion per year, disproportionately concentrated in trade-intensive and services-intensive sectors. The estimate is contested (attribution is always uncertain with synthetic controls), but its order of magnitude is consistent with multiple independent analyses and with the gravity model predictions that Chapter 3-B's framework would generate.

The regulatory divergence that followed Brexit has created a natural experiment in the relationship between institutional distance and services trade. As the UK develops its own regulatory frameworks for financial services, data protection, product standards, and professional qualifications — diverging from the EU acquis that British firms had previously operated under — the institutional distance between the UK and the EU increases, and the gravity model predicts that bilateral trade will fall correspondingly. The Northern Ireland Protocol (and its successor, the Windsor Framework) created the paradoxical situation of a UK territory that remains within the Single Market for goods while the rest of the UK has left it — effectively creating a regulatory border within a sovereign state. Northern Ireland's experience as a dual-market territory, with access to both the UK and EU goods markets, provides a real-time experiment in the trade effects of differential institutional alignment — and early evidence suggests that Northern Ireland has benefited from its unique position, with inward investment and goods trade growing faster than in the rest of the UK, though at the cost of political tensions that have periodically paralyzed the devolved government in Stormont.

### The Political Economy of Left-Behind Regions

The deeper lesson of Brexit for spatial economics is about the political sustainability of integration — a theme that connects to every regional case study in this book. The regions that voted Leave were, by most economic measures, net beneficiaries of EU membership: they received Structural Fund transfers, benefited from EU agricultural subsidies (Common Agricultural Policy payments were significant in rural England), and their consumers benefited from lower prices on imported goods. But these diffuse economic benefits were outweighed — in political terms — by the concentrated costs of adjustment: factory closures that could be attributed (rightly or wrongly) to EU trade competition, immigration that changed the character of small communities, and a sense of political powerlessness that the Leave campaign channeled into a vote against the institutional embodiment of integration.

Rodríguez-Pose's (2018) "revenge of the places that don't matter" thesis captures the political economy precisely. Decades of spatial policy that favored metropolitan agglomeration — concentrating public investment, infrastructure spending, and institutional capacity in London and a handful of other cities — left peripheral regions with declining economies, aging populations, and weakened civic institutions. These regions did not lack economic activity entirely; they lacked the kind of economic activity (knowledge-intensive services, high-value manufacturing, innovation-driven growth) that generates rising incomes and social optimism. The Brexit vote was, in part, a rejection of the development model that had produced this spatial outcome — even if the alternative (leaving the EU) offered no obvious mechanism for improving the economic prospects of left-behind regions.

Rodríguez-Pose's (2018) analysis connects the Brexit pattern to a broader comparative framework. The "geography of discontent" is not unique to the UK: the spatial concentration of support for Marine Le Pen in France's peripheral departments, the rise of the AfD in eastern Germany, the League's dominance in Italy's north (and the Five Star Movement's in the south), and the appeal of Orbán's Fidesz in rural Hungary all share a common spatial logic. In each case, regions that perceive themselves as left behind by integration, globalization, and metropolitan concentration channel their dissatisfaction into political movements that promise to reverse — or at least slow — the integration process. The spatial economics of these movements connect directly to Chapter 1's core-periphery model: when the benefits of agglomeration accrue primarily to the core and the costs of adjustment fall primarily on the periphery, the periphery's consent to the integration project erodes.

The spatial economics literature has engaged seriously with this diagnosis. McCann (2016, 2020) has documented how the UK's uniquely centralized governance structure — far more centralized than Germany, Spain, or even France — concentrated economic policy decisions in London, leaving peripheral regions with neither the institutional capacity nor the fiscal resources to design and implement their own development strategies. The data are stark: sub-national government spending in the UK accounts for approximately 5 percent of GDP, compared to 23 percent in Germany, 16 percent in Spain, and 11 percent in France. English local authorities have less fiscal autonomy than municipalities in virtually any other OECD country — they cannot set their own tax rates, borrow independently for investment, or design industrial policy without central government approval. The result is that regions like the North East, Yorkshire, and the East Midlands have been dependent on central government allocation decisions that have consistently favored London and the South East — not necessarily through deliberate policy but through the gravitational logic of a system where transport investment follows demand, research funding follows excellence, and cultural institutions follow talent, all of which concentrate in the metropolis.

The UK government's post-Brexit "Levelling Up" agenda, which promised to direct public investment toward the north and midlands, was an explicit acknowledgment that spatial inequality had become a political crisis. The 2022 Levelling Up White Paper proposed twelve "missions" — from improving educational attainment to expanding broadband to dispersing R&D spending — but the program's implementation has been uneven, its funding modest relative to the scale of the challenge, and the fundamental structural centralisation of the UK state has not been reformed. The comparison to Chapter 4's analysis of the US Rust Belt is instructive: both the UK and the US have confronted the political consequences of spatially concentrated deindustrialization, and both have responded with place-based initiatives (Levelling Up, Opportunity Zones) that operate at a scale far smaller than the structural forces they are designed to counteract.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: Measuring Services Trade After Dis-Integration**
>
> Measuring the impact of Brexit on UK-EU services trade presents significant data challenges. Services trade statistics are compiled from balance of payments surveys, enterprise surveys, and customs declarations (for Mode 3 and Mode 4), all of which have different coverage, timing, and definitions. The UK's Office for National Statistics changed its methodology for estimating services trade with the EU after Brexit, making pre- and post-Brexit comparisons unreliable.
>
> Additionally, some of the "relocation" from London to EU cities does not appear in trade statistics at all. When a bank moves a team of 200 traders from London to Frankfurt, the output they produce shifts from UK GDP to German GDP — but this is not recorded as a change in bilateral services trade, because the bank's internal transfers are not cross-border services transactions in the balance of payments sense. The result is that the measured impact of Brexit on UK-EU services trade may understate the actual reallocation of economic activity, because a significant portion of the reallocation occurs through FDI and intra-firm reorganization rather than through arm's-length trade flows.
>
> Researchers studying Brexit's services impact have therefore supplemented trade statistics with alternative data sources: job postings (to track financial services hiring in continental Europe), commercial real estate leases (to track office space uptake in Dublin, Frankfurt, and Amsterdam), regulatory filings (to track new EU authorizations for UK-origin firms), and LinkedIn data (to track the geographic movement of financial services professionals). Each source has limitations, but together they provide a more comprehensive picture than trade statistics alone.

## Data in Depth: The Geography of Discontent — Mapping Leave Vote Shares Against Economic Indicators

**The question.** Which local economic characteristics predict the Leave vote, and how does the spatial pattern relate to the integration benefits and costs described in this chapter?

**Data sources.**
- **Electoral Commission** official results of the June 2016 EU referendum at local authority level. Available from the Electoral Commission's published results database.
- **ONS Annual Population Survey** for local authority-level economic indicators: employment rate, median earnings, educational attainment, immigration share, age structure. Available from NOMIS.
- **BEIS Local Authority Level Business Data** for sectoral composition of local employment. Available from the Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy.
- **EU Structural Funds** allocation data for UK regions, from the European Commission's Open Data Portal.

**Construction.**
1. Download referendum results at local authority level (382 counting areas in England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland). Compute Leave share as a percentage of valid votes.
2. Merge with ONS economic indicators at the local authority level for the most recent pre-referendum year (2015 or 2016).
3. Construct variables capturing the three channels described in Section 10.3:
   - **Trade exposure:** Manufacturing employment share; change in manufacturing employment 2000–2015.
   - **Fiscal perception:** EU Structural Fund receipts per capita; perceived benefit from EU membership (if available from survey data).
   - **Immigration exposure:** Share of population born in other EU member states; change in EU-born population 2004–2015.
4. Estimate a cross-sectional regression:

$$
\text{Leave}_{i} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{ManufShare}_i + \beta_2 \text{EUBornShare}_i + \beta_3 \text{Degree}_i + \beta_4 \text{MedianAge}_i + \varepsilon_i
$$

5. Test for spatial autocorrelation in the residuals using Moran's I with a contiguity-based weight matrix. Significant spatial autocorrelation in the residuals would suggest that the model omits spatially structured factors — neighborhood effects, regional identity, media market exposure — that influence the Leave vote beyond individual-level economic characteristics.

**Expected findings.** Based on the extensive political geography literature on Brexit, manufacturing employment share, lower educational attainment, higher median age, and higher EU-born population share should all predict higher Leave vote shares. The spatial autocorrelation test should be significant, reflecting the clustering of Leave votes in contiguous regions of the industrial north, the east coast, and the rural midlands.

**Caveats.** The ecological inference problem is severe in this context: the regression relates local-area characteristics to local-area vote shares, but individuals within each area voted for heterogeneous reasons. A local authority with high manufacturing employment that voted Leave may have cast its Leave votes from service-sector workers, not manufacturing workers. Individual-level survey data (the British Election Study) provides a complement but introduces selection and recall biases. Students should be explicit about the ecological nature of their inferences and should avoid causal language for what is fundamentally a cross-sectional correlation.

**Student exercise.** Extend the regression to include a spatial lag of the Leave vote share (the average Leave share in neighboring local authorities, using a queen contiguity weight matrix). If the spatial lag coefficient is significant and positive, it suggests that neighborhood effects — regional identity, shared media markets, social network contagion — influence voting behavior beyond what individual-area economic characteristics can explain. Compare the OLS and spatial lag model estimates and discuss what the spatial dependence implies for the "geography of discontent" thesis.

## Institutional Spotlight: The European Central Bank — Monetary Policy for a Heterogeneous Currency Area

The European Central Bank (ECB), established in 1998 and headquartered in Frankfurt, is the institution most directly responsible for the asymmetric adjustments that characterize the eurozone crisis. Its mandate — price stability, defined as inflation "below, but close to, 2 percent" over the medium term — is deliberately narrow, excluding the employment and financial stability objectives that the US Federal Reserve and other central banks pursue alongside their inflation targets.

**The one-size-fits-all problem.** The ECB sets a single interest rate for 20 eurozone member states with different inflation rates, different output gaps, different debt levels, and different financial structures. When the ECB kept rates low during the 2002–2007 period (responding to low inflation in Germany and the core countries), the policy was simultaneously appropriate for Germany (which was experiencing slow growth and subdued inflation) and excessively stimulative for Spain and Ireland (which were experiencing housing booms and rising inflation). The result was that the ECB's single policy rate was simultaneously too tight for some regions and too loose for others — a spatial mismatch that the OCA theory had predicted but that the euro's architects had hoped would self-correct.

**The crisis response.** The ECB's response to the eurozone crisis evolved from orthodox monetary policy (interest rate adjustments, standard liquidity operations) to unconventional measures that amounted to quasi-fiscal intervention. Mario Draghi's July 2012 pledge to do "whatever it takes" to preserve the euro was followed by the Outright Monetary Transactions (OMT) program — a commitment to buy unlimited quantities of sovereign bonds for countries in ESM programs. The OMT was never activated, but its announcement collapsed the interest rate spreads between peripheral and core government bonds, ending the acute phase of the crisis.

The subsequent quantitative easing program (2015–2019, restarted 2020) involved the ECB purchasing over €4 trillion in government and corporate bonds, compressing yields across the eurozone and providing financing conditions that enabled peripheral countries to refinance their debts at sustainable rates. The Pandemic Emergency Purchase Programme (PEPP, 2020–2022) added another €1.85 trillion, with explicit flexibility to concentrate purchases on countries facing the greatest market stress — a de facto fiscal transfer mechanism operating through the central bank's balance sheet.

**The governance tension.** The ECB's Governing Council consists of six Executive Board members plus the governors of the 20 national central banks — a structure where each country has one vote regardless of economic size. Germany (29 percent of eurozone GDP) has the same formal voting weight as Malta (0.1 percent). In practice, the system operates by consensus, and the Bundesbank's institutional weight — rooted in Germany's creditor status, its anti-inflationary tradition, and the ECB's Frankfurt location — gives German preferences disproportionate influence. The resignation of Bundesbank president Jens Weidmann in 2021, after years of opposing the ECB's expansionary policies, symbolized the tension between the ECB's de facto role as a quasi-fiscal institution managing spatial heterogeneity and its de jure mandate as a price-stability-focused monetary authority.

The Transmission Protection Instrument (TPI), announced in July 2022, represents the latest stage in the ECB's institutional evolution. Designed to prevent "unwarranted, disorderly market dynamics" from creating divergent borrowing costs across eurozone members, the TPI effectively gives the ECB unlimited capacity to purchase the bonds of specific countries facing market stress — provided those countries comply with EU fiscal rules and structural reform commitments. The TPI formalizes what OMT had improvised: a mechanism for spatial differentiation within a nominally uniform monetary framework. Its connection to the institutional analysis of Chapter 2 is direct: the ECB has evolved from a rules-based institution (set the interest rate, maintain price stability) into a discretionary one (choose which countries' bonds to buy, under what conditions), and this evolution reflects the impossibility of governing a heterogeneous economic space with spatially uniform rules.

**The spatial policy lesson.** The ECB's evolution from orthodoxy to quasi-fiscal intervention illustrates a fundamental point about monetary unions: a single monetary policy cannot manage a heterogeneous economic space. The institutional innovations forced by the crisis — OMT, QE, PEPP, TPI, the Banking Union — are all mechanisms for introducing spatial differentiation into a system designed on the principle of spatial uniformity. The question for the eurozone's future is whether these crisis-forged institutions can be formalized and made permanent, or whether they will remain emergency measures that are politically contested and legally fragile.

## Conclusion: The Dialectic of Integration and Dis-Integration

This chapter has traced three forces that have disrupted the convergence narrative of Chapter 9. The eurozone crisis demonstrated that monetary integration without fiscal union creates asymmetric adjustment mechanisms that punish peripheral regions. Post-socialist integration created genuine convergence for CEE countries as a whole but concentrated the gains in metropolitan areas and western border regions, reproducing within the new member states the core-periphery patterns that Cohesion Funds were designed to reduce. And Brexit showed that the political sustainability of integration depends on whether its benefits reach the regions that feel most threatened by the changes it produces.

Italy's Mezzogiorno stands as the most sobering case study in the limits of integration and redistribution. Seventy years of continuous regional development policy — from the Cassa per il Mezzogiorno (1950–1992) through successive EU Cohesion Fund programming periods — have transferred hundreds of billions of euros to southern Italy without closing the North-South gap. Calabria's GDP per capita relative to the EU average is barely higher in 2023 than it was in 2000. The diagnosis connects every theme of this chapter and the last: weak institutional capacity prevents effective absorption of transfers (Section 9.3); emigration of the most talented workers compounds the human capital deficit (Section 10.1); organized crime raises transaction costs and deters private investment; and the political clientelism that distributes public resources on the basis of patronage rather than productivity ensures that even well-designed programs are captured by incumbent interests. The Mezzogiorno is not a failure of policy ambition; it is a failure of institutional foundations — and it stands as a warning for every region that hopes integration and transfers alone will deliver convergence.

The dialectic between integration and dis-integration is not unique to Europe. Chapter 4's analysis of USMCA showed how the North American integration project has shifted from efficiency-maximizing free trade to compliance-intensive managed trade, partly in response to the spatial concentration of integration's costs in the Rust Belt. Chapter 7 showed how China's BRI creates dependencies and backlash in recipient countries. Chapter 14 will show how the AfCFTA must navigate the centrifugal forces of national sovereignty and uneven institutional capacity.

What makes the European case analytically distinctive is the depth of the institutional infrastructure. The EU has instruments — Cohesion Funds, the ECB's balance sheet, Next Generation EU, the EIB — that no other regional arrangement possesses. If any institution can manage the spatial tensions of integration, the EU can. The fact that it has struggled — that the euro nearly broke apart in 2012, that the UK actually left in 2020, and that populist movements in Italy, France, and Hungary continue to challenge the integration project — suggests that the spatial tensions inherent in deep integration are more powerful than even the most sophisticated institutional architecture can fully resolve.

The fundamental choice that the EU faces is between deeper integration and greater heterogeneity. Deeper integration — completing the banking union, creating a permanent fiscal capacity, harmonizing services regulation, building common defense procurement — would address the institutional incompleteness that made the eurozone crisis so destructive and that continues to fragment the Single Market. But deeper integration requires political legitimacy that comes from inclusive growth across the entire European territory, and the evidence of this chapter is that integration has not delivered inclusive growth. The alternative — accepting greater heterogeneity through variable geometry, multi-speed integration, or looser institutional coupling — would reduce the strain on regions that cannot keep pace with the integration frontier, but would also reduce the economic gains that integration generates. There is no resolution to this tension, only management — and the quality of that management depends on the institutional capacity, political leadership, and spatial policy imagination that the EU can mobilize.

A new dimension has entered this calculation since 2022. Russia's invasion of Ukraine has forced the EU to confront the security implications of spatial economic interdependence — the same theme that Chapter 6 analyzed for East Asian semiconductor supply chains. European energy dependence on Russian gas, defense industrial fragmentation across 27 national procurement systems, and the need to absorb millions of Ukrainian refugees have created pressures for integration in domains (defense, energy, migration) where sovereignty concerns had previously blocked progress. Whether these pressures produce a new wave of institution-building — as the postwar period and the post-Maastricht period did — or merely add another layer of ad hoc crisis management remains the defining question of European spatial economics in the 2020s.

Chapter 11 shifts the analysis to the Middle East and North Africa, where the intersection of resource rents, authoritarian governance, and climate vulnerability creates a distinctly different set of spatial economic challenges — and where the EU's role as a neighboring economic bloc shapes the region's integration possibilities in ways that connect directly to the gravity model and institutional distance frameworks of this book.
