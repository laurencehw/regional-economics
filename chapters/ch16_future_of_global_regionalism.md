# Chapter 16: The Future of Global Regionalism and Services Trade

---

## Introduction: Three Futures

Consider two software developers, both proficient in Python, both capable of deploying a machine-learning pipeline to production. One sits in a co-working space in Nairobi's Westlands district; the other occupies a standing desk in San Francisco's SoMa neighborhood. They are bidding on the same contract on Upwork. The Nairobi developer's rate is $25 per hour; the San Francisco developer charges $200. The client — a logistics company in Hamburg — cannot tell them apart from their portfolios. This is the spatial economics of services trade made visceral: identical skills, identical platform, an 8:1 wage ratio that reflects not productivity differences but the geography of where each developer happens to live. The forces that will determine whether that wage ratio narrows, persists, or widens are the subject of this chapter — and, in a sense, of this entire book.

The stakes are large. Global digital services trade reached approximately $3.8 trillion in 2022 (WTO 2023), growing at roughly twice the rate of goods trade over the preceding decade. Services now account for more than two-thirds of GDP in most OECD countries and an increasing share of GDP in the developing world. Yet the spatial economics of services trade remains far less understood than the spatial economics of goods trade, in part because services are harder to measure (they cross borders as bytes rather than containers), in part because the institutional frameworks governing services trade are newer and more fragmented, and in part because the discipline's core spatial models — from von Thunen's rings to Krugman's core-periphery — were built for a world of tangible commodities with positive transport costs. This book has traced how goods trade, investment flows, and institutional frameworks organize economic activity in space across fourteen regions. But the fastest-growing segment of international trade operates under different spatial logic. This chapter asks whether the forces that shaped the geography of goods trade will shape services trade in the same way, or whether digital delivery, regulatory fragmentation, and geopolitical rivalry will produce a fundamentally different economic map.

The spatial economics of the next quarter-century will be shaped by three forces that are already visible but whose trajectories remain uncertain. The first is telemigration — the possibility that digital technology will enable workers in developing countries to perform service-sector tasks for employers in developed countries without physically crossing a border, fundamentally altering the geography of labor markets. The second is the fragmentation of the digital commons — the "Splinternet" — into competing regulatory blocs (US, EU, China) with different rules for data flows, platform governance, and digital trade, creating new borders in the seemingly borderless digital economy. The third is the evolution of regional integration itself: whether the future holds hyper-regionalized "fortress blocs" organized around geopolitical rivalry, or flexible networks that allow countries to participate in multiple overlapping trade arrangements. Each of these forces connects, in turn, to the climate transition analyzed in Chapter 15: decarbonization will reshape the geography of energy-intensive services (data centers, logistics hubs, financial centers insuring stranded assets), and the green industrial policies of the IRA, the EU's Green Deal, and China's Five-Year Plans will alter the comparative advantage of regions in ways that interact with every trend examined here.

Each of these forces has clear spatial economic implications, and each connects to the theoretical and empirical frameworks developed throughout this book. Telemigration extends the task-trading framework of Chapter 3-B to its logical conclusion: if tasks can be traded across borders as easily as goods, the spatial distribution of services employment will change as dramatically as the spatial distribution of manufacturing employment changed during the first wave of globalization. The Splinternet extends the institutional distance framework of Chapter 2 to the digital economy: data localization requirements and platform regulations create new forms of distance that the gravity model must incorporate. And the fortress-versus-flexibility question extends the regional integration analysis of every preceding chapter to ask whether the political forces that drove Brexit (Chapter 10), the USMCA renegotiation (Chapter 4), and the US-China technology rivalry (Chapter 7) will fragment the global economy into competing blocs or whether the economic logic of interconnection will prevail.

This chapter proceeds in five sections. Section 16.1 examines the telemigration hypothesis and its spatial implications. Section 16.2 analyzes the Splinternet and the geography of digital regulation. Section 16.3 presents a global comparative matrix of services trade frameworks across the major regional integration agreements. Section 16.4 examines the fortress-blocs-versus-flexible-networks question. Section 16.5 surveys the methodological frontier of spatial economics — the tools that will define the next generation of research in the field.

## 16.1 The Telemigration Hypothesis

### Baldwin's Globotics Upheaval

Richard Baldwin's (2019) *The Globotics Upheaval* argued that advances in communication technology, machine translation, and remote collaboration platforms would enable a new wave of services globalization — "telemigration" — in which skilled workers in developing countries would compete directly with service-sector workers in developed countries for white-collar jobs. Unlike the BPO revolution described in Chapter 8, which was mediated by corporate outsourcing contracts, telemigration would operate through digital platforms that connect individual freelancers with individual employers, disintermediating the multinational firms and outsourcing companies that currently manage cross-border services trade.

The COVID-19 pandemic provided an unexpected natural experiment for the telemigration hypothesis. The sudden shift to remote work in 2020 demonstrated that a substantial fraction of service-sector work — software development, graphic design, data analysis, customer support, content creation, financial accounting — could be performed from any location with adequate internet connectivity. Althoff et al. (2022) estimated that approximately 30 percent of US jobs could be performed remotely, and that the shift to remote work during the pandemic was concentrated in high-wage, high-skill occupations — precisely the occupations most exposed to telemigration competition from lower-wage countries.

The spatial economics of telemigration are potentially transformative. If an American software company can hire a developer in Lagos, Bangalore, or Bucharest at a fraction of the San Francisco salary, the comparative advantage of expensive metropolitan locations in developed countries — which rests on the concentration of skilled workers who must be physically present — erodes. The agglomeration economies that sustain Silicon Valley, the City of London, and the financial districts of New York and Tokyo depend on the co-location of talented workers; telemigration threatens to disperse that talent across global geography.

### Evidence and Limits

The evidence for telemigration to date is suggestive but not conclusive. Digital freelancing platforms (Upwork, Fiverr, Toptal) have grown rapidly, with cross-border work constituting a significant share of platform transactions. The Oxford Internet Institute's Online Labour Index, which tracks demand for gig work across platforms, shows that demand for remote workers has grown steadily since 2016, with particularly sharp increases during and after COVID-19. India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, the Philippines, and Ukraine are the largest suppliers of platform-mediated remote work; the United States, United Kingdom, and Australia are the largest demanders.

But the telemigration hypothesis faces several constraints that Chapter 2's institutional framework helps to identify. First, **trust and contracting.** Cross-border freelancing through digital platforms involves principal-agent problems (the employer cannot directly observe the worker's effort) and contracting challenges (legal enforcement of contracts across jurisdictions is costly and uncertain). These institutional frictions favor platform-mediated transactions for standardized, codifiable tasks (web development, data entry, translation) and disfavor them for complex, tacit-knowledge-intensive tasks (strategic consulting, relationship management, creative direction) — the same task-complexity gradient that the Grossman-Rossi-Hansberg framework predicts.

Second, **time zones.** While telemigration eliminates geographic distance for asynchronous work, it cannot eliminate the coordination costs of working across time zones for synchronous tasks. A software team that needs real-time collaboration works more effectively when its members share overlapping working hours — which favors nearshore locations (the CEE nearshore services belt described in Chapter 10) over distant offshore locations for tasks requiring frequent communication.

Third, **regulatory barriers.** Employment law, tax law, and professional licensing create institutional barriers to cross-border remote work that digital technology does not eliminate. An American company that hires a remote worker in Nigeria faces questions about which country's labor law applies, where social security contributions are owed, and whether the arrangement creates a "permanent establishment" for corporate tax purposes. These questions are unresolved in most jurisdictions, and the regulatory uncertainty adds friction to cross-border remote employment.

### Platform Scale and the "Missing Middle"

The platform evidence, while growing, provides useful quantitative benchmarks. Upwork reported approximately $3.8 billion in gross services volume in 2022, with cross-border transactions accounting for a substantial and growing share. Fiverr, a platform oriented toward smaller discrete tasks, generated roughly $337 million in revenue in the same year. These numbers are small relative to global services trade — together they represent less than 0.2 percent of the $3.8 trillion total — but their growth trajectory is steep, and they capture only the most visible segment of platform-mediated cross-border work. The ILO (2023) estimates that approximately 14 million workers worldwide perform cross-border digital platform work, a figure that excludes the much larger number of workers employed through traditional BPO contracts and corporate remote-work arrangements. The Oxford Internet Institute's Online Labour Index reveals that India, Bangladesh, and the Philippines are the dominant supplier countries, with software development, creative and multimedia, and writing and translation as the most traded task categories.

The distribution of telemigration exposure across tasks is not uniform but bimodal — a pattern that might be called the "missing middle." At one end, very simple, fully codifiable tasks (data entry, image tagging, basic transcription) are easily traded on platforms but command low wages and face increasing competition from automation. At the other end, highly complex, relationship-intensive tasks (strategy consulting, executive coaching, complex litigation, C-suite advisory) resist platform mediation because they depend on trust, reputation, tacit knowledge, and the kind of nuanced interpersonal judgment that the Storper and Venables (2004) "buzz" framework from Chapter 1 identifies as the irreducible advantage of co-location. The middle range — tasks of moderate complexity that are codifiable enough to be specified in a platform contract but valuable enough to command decent wages (web application development, financial modeling, architectural drafting, data analysis) — is the zone most exposed to telemigration. This is precisely the segment that the Grossman and Rossi-Hansberg (2008) task-trading framework from Chapter 3-B predicts will be offshored first: tasks where communication costs are manageable and factor price differences are large enough to motivate cross-border sourcing.

### Education and Healthcare as Telemigration Frontiers

Two service sectors — education and healthcare — illustrate the opportunities and limits of telemigration with particular clarity. In education, the platform revolution arrived early: Coursera reported over 130 million registered learners by 2023, and edX, Udacity, and scores of smaller platforms offer courses from elite universities to anyone with an internet connection. Mode 1 delivery (cross-border digital) is technically feasible for much of higher education, but completion rates of 5 to 15 percent for MOOCs suggest that purely digital delivery is a poor substitute for the structured incentives, peer interaction, and credentialing that physical institutions provide. The more consequential spatial flow in education remains Mode 2 — consumption abroad — with US education exports exceeding $40 billion per year as international students pay tuition, housing, and living expenses in university towns that function as service export clusters. The branch campus model analyzed in Chapter 11 (Education City in Doha, NYU Abu Dhabi, INSEAD Singapore) represents a hybrid Mode 2/Mode 3 arrangement: the educational institution establishes commercial presence abroad while students consume the service in a foreign location. Whether digital delivery will erode or complement these physical flows remains an open question, but the evidence to date suggests complementarity — online courses serve as a funnel for physical enrollment, not a substitute.

Healthcare presents a mirror image. Telemedicine expanded dramatically during the COVID-19 pandemic, with India's eSanjeevani platform recording over 100 million consultations and the Apollo Hospitals network operating more than 900 telemedicine centers across rural India (Chapter 8). But Zeltzer et al. (2023) find that telemedicine adoption in Israel reinforced geographic concentration at specialist hubs rather than dispersing care to peripheral locations: patients in underserved areas gained access to specialists, but those specialists were overwhelmingly located in existing urban centers of excellence. The mechanism is instructive for services trade geography more broadly — digital delivery enables access but does not redistribute the underlying human capital. Specialist physicians remain concentrated in Tel Aviv, Mumbai, and Houston because the agglomeration economies that produce them (research hospitals, training programs, peer networks) are place-specific. Mode 1 digital delivery thus complements rather than substitutes for Mode 2 medical tourism (the Gulf medical tourism flows analyzed in Chapter 11) and Mode 4 physician migration. The spatial implication is sobering: telemedicine may improve welfare by extending specialist access to underserved regions, but it does not create a new geography of healthcare — it reinforces the existing one.

### The Distributional Politics of Telemigration

Telemigration creates winners and losers whose identities are predictable from the framework developed in this book. The winners include skilled workers in developing countries who can access global wages through platforms — the Nairobi developer earning $25 per hour instead of the $8 that the local Kenyan market might offer. They also include employers and consumers in developed countries who benefit from lower service costs. The losers are mid-skill service workers in developed countries who face new competition from lower-wage countries — a dynamic that parallels the manufacturing offshoring story analyzed in Chapter 4 (NAFTA's labor market effects), Chapter 7 (ASEAN's integration into East Asian production networks), and Chapter 10 (the impact of CEE integration on Western European manufacturing workers). The Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013) analysis of the "China shock" in US manufacturing — which documented concentrated adjustment costs in specific communities, political backlash, and inadequate policy responses — provides a cautionary template for the services analog. If telemigration concentrates adjustment costs in specific occupations and locations (mid-skill service workers in expensive metropolitan areas), the political response could follow the same trajectory: demands for protection, restrictions on digital trade, and regulatory barriers to cross-border remote work. The EU's proposed platform work directive, which would reclassify many gig workers as employees and impose social security obligations on platforms, is arguably the first legislative response to this distributional tension.

### Spatial Implications: Delventhal, Kwon, and Parkhomenko (2022)

Delventhal, Kwon, and Parkhomenko (2022) developed a quantitative spatial general equilibrium model to analyze the long-run effects of remote work on the spatial distribution of economic activity within the United States. Their model predicts that a permanent increase in remote work capacity would reduce the population of expensive cities (New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles) by 5–15 percent and increase the population of smaller cities and suburban areas. The mechanism is straightforward: when workers do not need to commute to a central office, the premium they are willing to pay for proximity to the city center declines, and cheaper locations with better amenities (space, nature, lower cost of living) become more attractive.

The international extension of this model — which has not yet been fully developed — would predict a similar reallocation across countries: telemigration-exposed jobs would relocate from expensive developed-country cities to cheaper developing-country cities, while telemigration-resistant jobs (those requiring physical presence, tacit knowledge, or real-time coordination) would remain in their current locations. The net effect would be a partial equalization of service-sector wages across countries — a "factor price equalization for services" that the Heckscher-Ohlin framework predicts for goods trade but that has never been observed for services because the factors of production (skilled workers) have been immobile.

## 16.2 The Splinternet: Digital Sovereignty and New Borders

### Three Digital-Regulatory Models

The global internet, which was conceived as a borderless network and which enabled the services trade patterns described throughout this book, is fragmenting into distinct regulatory zones with different rules for data flows, platform governance, content moderation, and digital commerce. Three models are emerging:

**The American model** emphasizes private-sector innovation, light-touch regulation, and free data flows. Under this model, technology companies (Google, Amazon, Meta, Apple, Microsoft) operate globally with minimal government interference, data flows freely across borders, and market competition is the primary mechanism for disciplining platform behavior. The spatial economic consequence is that American technology companies dominate global digital markets, and the data generated by users worldwide flows to and is processed in American data centers and corporate headquarters.

**The European model** emphasizes data protection, consumer rights, and regulatory sovereignty. GDPR (Chapter 9), the Digital Services Act, the Digital Markets Act, and the AI Act create a regulatory framework that prioritizes individual privacy, limits platform power, and requires data processing to comply with European rules regardless of where the processing company is headquartered. The spatial economic consequence is that companies operating in the EU must either comply with European rules (which may differ from their home-country rules) or exit the European market — creating a regulatory barrier to cross-border digital services trade that functions analogously to a tariff.

**The Chinese model** emphasizes state control, data sovereignty, and digital autarky. China's Great Firewall blocks access to foreign platforms (Google, Facebook, Twitter, Wikipedia), and the Cybersecurity Law, Data Security Law, and Personal Information Protection Law require that data generated in China be stored in China and submitted to government oversight. The result is a self-contained digital ecosystem (WeChat, Alibaba, Baidu, Douyin) that operates under Chinese rules and does not interoperate with the global internet.

### Cloud Infrastructure Geography

The physical geography of cloud computing — the locations of data centers operated by AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Alibaba Cloud, and other providers — is both a cause and a consequence of the Splinternet. Data centers must be located in jurisdictions that satisfy the data sovereignty requirements of the customers they serve: a European company subject to GDPR may require that its data be stored in a European data center, not an American one. The result is that cloud providers replicate their infrastructure across regulatory zones, building parallel data center networks in the US, Europe, and Asia — a physical manifestation of digital borders.

The spatial economics of cloud infrastructure create a new form of locational advantage. Countries that host data centers benefit from the construction investment, the employment (though data centers are highly automated and employ relatively few workers), the demand for electricity (which can be substantial — data centers account for approximately 1–2 percent of global electricity consumption), and the network effects of being a node in the global digital infrastructure. Countries that lack data centers — particularly developing countries in Sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and the Pacific — face higher latency, lower reliability, and greater dependence on cross-border data flows that may be subject to foreign regulatory requirements.

### DFFT and the Attempt to Bridge Digital Divides

The Japanese government, under Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, introduced the concept of "Data Free Flow with Trust" (DFFT) at the 2019 G7 summit in Osaka — an attempt to find a middle ground between the American model (unfettered data flows) and the European model (data protection as a fundamental right). DFFT proposes that data should flow freely across borders provided that sending and receiving jurisdictions meet baseline standards of data protection, security, and privacy. In principle, DFFT could become the WTO of data governance — a multilateral framework that enables cross-border digital services trade while respecting legitimate regulatory objectives. In practice, DFFT has made limited progress. China's exclusion from the G7 framework means that the world's second-largest digital economy is outside the system. The EU insists that DFFT must be compatible with GDPR adequacy determinations, which effectively requires other countries to adopt European-style data protection or face restrictions. And the United States, despite endorsing DFFT rhetorically, has shown limited interest in the domestic data protection legislation that would be a prerequisite for credible participation. The result is that DFFT remains an aspiration rather than an operational framework — a symbol of the difficulty of multilateral digital governance.

### India as a Fourth Model

The US-EU-China trichotomy, while analytically useful, obscures a fourth digital governance model that is emerging in India and attracting interest across the developing world. India Stack — the combination of Aadhaar (biometric identity for 1.4 billion residents), UPI (Unified Payments Interface, which processed approximately 117 billion transactions in 2023), and DigiLocker (document verification) — represents digital public infrastructure built on open-source protocols and available for private-sector innovation. India combines data sovereignty concerns that resemble the Chinese approach (the Reserve Bank of India mandates that payment data be stored domestically) with open architectural standards that resemble the American approach (UPI is interoperable, non-proprietary, and accessible to any licensed fintech). The result is a model that does not fit neatly into the three-bloc framework: India localizes sensitive data but does not wall off its digital ecosystem; it regulates platforms but does not ban foreign ones; it builds public digital infrastructure but invites private participation. Chapter 8's analysis of India's IT services sector provides the institutional context — the same NASSCOM-nurtured ecosystem that made Bangalore a global software hub is now building digital public goods that other developing countries (Brazil, Thailand, several African Union members) are exploring as alternatives to both the American platform-dominated model and the Chinese state-controlled model.

### The Deepening Geography of Cloud

The cloud infrastructure gap is widening even as providers expand. As of 2024, AWS operated 33 regions globally, Microsoft Azure more than 60, and Google Cloud approximately 40. But the distribution is radically uneven: Africa had roughly five data center regions across all major providers (concentrated in Johannesburg, Cape Town, and Lagos), compared to dozens in the United States and Europe. This means that an African firm running latency-sensitive applications — fintech, e-commerce, real-time analytics — faces a structural disadvantage: its data must travel further, its costs are higher, and its dependence on European or American cloud infrastructure creates legal and regulatory exposure. The Africa data center deficit mirrors the physical infrastructure gap documented in Chapters 13 and 14: just as inadequate roads and ports constrain goods trade, inadequate data center capacity constrains digital services trade. The analogy is imperfect — a data center can be built faster than a port — but the underlying dynamic is the same: infrastructure investment follows demand, and demand follows infrastructure investment, creating the self-reinforcing agglomeration logic that Chapter 1's core-periphery model predicts.

### AI and Compute Sovereignty

The emergence of large language models and generative AI is adding a new layer to the Splinternet by making compute capacity a strategic asset — "compute sovereignty" as the digital analog of energy sovereignty. Training a frontier foundation model requires thousands of specialized GPUs (overwhelmingly NVIDIA's A100 and H100 chips), months of computation time, and electricity bills measured in millions of dollars. The geography of this supply chain is even more concentrated than the semiconductor supply chain it depends on: NVIDIA designs its chips in Santa Clara, TSMC fabricates them in Hsinchu (Chapter 6), and the data centers that house them are concentrated in a handful of US states (Virginia's "Data Center Alley" in Loudoun County), with secondary clusters in Ireland, the Netherlands, and Singapore. The US government's October 2022 export controls on advanced AI chips to China — tightened in October 2023 — represent the first direct application of the "weaponized interdependence" logic (Farrell and Newman 2019) from Chapter 6 to the AI domain. If compute capacity determines which countries can train frontier AI models, and if frontier AI models determine which countries can provide advanced digital services (legal research, medical diagnostics, financial analysis, software development), then the geography of GPU access becomes a first-order determinant of services trade comparative advantage. The irony is acute: the technology most likely to enable telemigration (AI-powered translation, code generation, content creation) is also the technology whose supply chain is most geographically concentrated and most subject to geopolitical restriction.

The matrix reveals a clear pattern: services trade liberalization is most advanced in agreements among developed countries (USMCA, EU, CPTPP) and least advanced in agreements among developing countries (RCEP, AfCFTA, SAARC). The gap reflects the institutional infrastructure that services liberalization requires — regulatory agencies capable of enforcing mutual recognition, legal systems that protect foreign service providers, and data governance frameworks that facilitate cross-border information flows. Building this infrastructure is the central challenge for the developing-country agreements, and the speed at which they do so will determine whether the global services economy becomes more or less fragmented over the coming decades.

Three patterns in the matrix deserve particular attention. The first is the **universal weakness of Mode 4** — the movement of natural persons. Across every framework in the table, Mode 4 is the most restricted mode of services delivery. Even USMCA, which eliminated tariffs on virtually all goods and created deep Mode 1 and Mode 3 liberalization, restricts Mode 4 to narrow TN visa categories. Even the EU Single Market, the most ambitious services integration project in history, restricts free movement of labor to EU citizens — and even within that framework, the Posted Workers Directive creates friction. Goods can cross borders with minimal restriction; capital flows somewhat freely; data flows increasingly freely (subject to the regulatory fragmentation documented in Section 16.2); but people cannot. This is the binding constraint on services trade globalization, and it has profound spatial implications: the geography of services production is ultimately determined by where people are allowed to live and work, and immigration policy remains the domain of national sovereignty even within the most integrated regional agreements.

The second pattern is the **AfCFTA's untapped potential**. The AfCFTA Services Protocol, adopted as part of Phase II negotiations, covers financial services, communication, transport, tourism, and business services across 54 countries with a combined population of 1.4 billion. If fully implemented, it would create the world's largest services market by population — and the one with the most to gain from liberalization, precisely because it starts from the lowest base. Intra-African services trade is currently minimal: the World Bank estimates that intra-African trade accounts for less than 15 percent of the continent's total trade, and the services share is even lower. The barriers are institutional rather than technological — licensing requirements that do not recognize qualifications across borders, payment systems that do not interoperate, data governance frameworks that are absent or incompatible (Chapter 14). The AfCFTA's progressive positive-list approach is pragmatic given these constraints: rather than committing to comprehensive liberalization and hoping institutional capacity follows, it allows countries to liberalize sector by sector as their regulatory capacity develops. Whether this gradualism produces meaningful integration or merely institutionalizes the status quo will depend on the implementation timeline and the political commitment of member states.

The third is **RCEP's quiet significance**. Despite weaker services provisions than CPTPP — a positive-list approach, limited data-flow disciplines, no mutual recognition agreements — RCEP creates a single rules-of-origin framework for services trade covering fifteen countries and approximately 30 percent of global GDP. For ASEAN members that are also CPTPP signatories (Vietnam, Malaysia, Singapore, Brunei), RCEP provides a parallel track that includes China, Japan, and South Korea in a single framework. RCEP's positive-list approach allows gradual liberalization without the political shock of negative listing — each member commits only to the specific sectors it is prepared to open, with built-in review mechanisms for expanding commitments over time. The trade-off is obvious (slower liberalization) but the political economy is more sustainable: RCEP's inclusive approach may produce broader — if shallower — integration than CPTPP's more demanding standards.

## 16.4 Fortress Blocs Versus Flexible Networks

### The Friend-Shoring Thesis

The "friend-shoring" thesis — articulated by US Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen in 2022 and reflected in the CHIPS Act, the IRA, and the EU's economic security strategy — holds that the future of international economic integration lies not in global free trade but in deep integration within blocs of geopolitically aligned countries, combined with selective decoupling from geopolitical adversaries. Under this thesis, the US and its allies would build supply chains that exclude China (and potentially Russia), while China would build parallel supply chains with its own partners. The result would be a bifurcated global economy — "two worlds, two systems" — with separate technology standards, separate financial systems, and separate services trade frameworks.

The spatial economics of friend-shoring are a NEG story with geopolitical parameters. Instead of firms locating where agglomeration economies are strongest, they locate where agglomeration economies are strongest within their permitted geopolitical bloc. The result is suboptimal from a pure efficiency perspective — the best location for semiconductor fabrication might be Taiwan, but if geopolitical risk makes Taiwan unreliable, the second-best location (Arizona, Kumamoto, Saxony) receives the investment instead. The welfare cost is the difference between the first-best and second-best locations, multiplied across thousands of investment decisions.

### The Flexibility Alternative

The alternative to fortress blocs is the flexible-networks model — exemplified by countries like Singapore, the UAE, Switzerland, and Vietnam that participate in multiple overlapping trade arrangements and maintain economic relationships with all major blocs simultaneously. These countries bet on connectivity rather than alignment: their strategy is to be indispensable nodes in global supply chains, too useful to be excluded by any bloc.

The spatial economics of the flexibility model favor small, well-governed economies with strategic geographic positions and strong institutional foundations. Singapore's participation in CPTPP, RCEP, and bilateral agreements with both the US and China gives it access to markets in all major blocs. The UAE's free-zone model (Chapter 11) allows it to serve as a business hub for Western firms, Chinese firms, and Indian firms simultaneously. Switzerland's bilateral agreements with the EU give it Single Market access while preserving the sovereignty to negotiate independently with other partners.

The question is whether the flexibility model can survive in an increasingly polarized geopolitical environment. If the US demands that its allies choose sides — restricting technology sales to China, excluding Chinese firms from critical infrastructure, requiring supply chain transparency that prevents dual-alignment — then the flexible-networks model becomes untenable for countries that depend on the US security umbrella. The spatial economic consequence would be the forced clustering of the global economy into blocs, with the flexible-network countries absorbed into one bloc or the other.

### The CHIPS Act as Canonical Friend-Shoring

The US CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 — which allocated $52 billion in subsidies for domestic semiconductor fabrication and research — is the clearest example of friend-shoring as industrial policy. BCG and SIA (2021) estimated that complete US-China semiconductor decoupling would require $350 to $450 billion in duplicative investment and raise chip costs by 35 to 65 percent. The CHIPS Act's $52 billion is a down payment on this decoupling — sufficient to incentivize TSMC's Arizona fab, Samsung's Taylor (Texas) fab, and Intel's Ohio expansion, but far short of the investment required for full supply chain independence. The spatial economics are instructive: the United States is paying a premium to relocate production from the first-best location (Taiwan, where TSMC's Hsinchu cluster benefits from three decades of agglomeration economies, a specialized labor pool, and proximity to the ITRI ecosystem documented in Chapter 6) to second-best locations (Arizona, where TSMC has faced construction delays, labor disputes, and yield challenges attributable to the absence of the tacit knowledge embedded in the Hsinchu cluster). The welfare cost of friend-shoring is precisely this gap between first-best and second-best locations, multiplied across thousands of investment decisions in semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, critical minerals, and advanced manufacturing.

### Supply Chain Opacity and the Transparency Imperative

Friend-shoring's implementation faces a fundamental information problem: most firms do not know their own supply chains beyond tier-1 suppliers. A study by the Economist Intelligence Unit found that fewer than half of global firms had visibility into their tier-2 suppliers, and fewer than a quarter could map their tier-3 supply base. This opacity makes it nearly impossible to verify that a supply chain is genuinely "friend-shored" — a semiconductor may be assembled in a US-allied country using materials processed in China using minerals extracted in the DRC. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive and the US Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act attempt to mandate supply chain transparency, requiring firms to trace inputs and verify that they do not involve forced labor or originate in prohibited jurisdictions. But implementation is enormously challenging: the supply chain for a single smartphone involves hundreds of suppliers across dozens of countries, and the documentation required to verify compliance at every tier exceeds what most firms' procurement systems can produce.

### Vietnam and the Swing-State Strategy

Vietnam has emerged as the paradigmatic swing state in the friend-shoring landscape — simultaneously a member of RCEP (which includes China) and CPTPP (which excludes China), a recipient of massive FDI from both US-allied companies (Samsung's smartphone complex in Bac Ninh, Intel's assembly plant in Ho Chi Minh City) and Chinese firms relocating production to circumvent US tariffs. Vietnam's GDP growth of 6 to 8 percent annually in recent years depends substantially on its ability to play both sides of the geopolitical divide. Its geographic position — sharing a border with China while maintaining strong economic relationships with the United States, Japan, and South Korea — makes it a natural connector between the two emerging blocs. Chapter 7's analysis of ASEAN positioning anticipated this dynamic: Vietnam, like Thailand and Indonesia, benefits from being too useful to exclude from either bloc's supply chains.

The Vietnamese strategy has an institutional parallel in the developing-world pattern of non-alignment as economic policy. India is a Quad member (with the US, Japan, and Australia) but purchases discounted Russian oil and resists sanctions compliance that would jeopardize its energy security. Indonesia leverages ASEAN centrality to maintain equidistance between the US and China while attracting investment from both (Chapter 7). Saudi Arabia is simultaneously an OPEC+ partner with Russia, a participant in the Abraham Accords normalization process, and a sovereign wealth investor whose Public Investment Fund holds stakes in everything from Lucid Motors to Newcastle United (Chapter 11). Brazil is a BRICS founding member but also a long-standing US security partner with deep trade ties to both China (its largest export market) and the EU. These countries resist binary alignment not out of ideological conviction but out of developmental pragmatism: their growth depends on access to markets, technology, and capital from multiple blocs, and choosing sides would foreclose opportunities that they cannot afford to lose. The non-aligned strategy works as long as neither superpower forces a choice — a condition that the escalating US-China technology rivalry (Chapter 6) is progressively undermining.

### The Services Dimension of Decoupling

The friend-shoring debate has focused overwhelmingly on goods supply chains — semiconductors, critical minerals, pharmaceuticals, advanced manufacturing. But the services dimension of decoupling may be more consequential and more difficult. A factory can be relocated from Shenzhen to Ho Chi Minh City — the physical infrastructure can be replicated, the supply chain can be redirected, the process technology can be transferred (at a cost). But a cloud services architecture built on AWS cannot easily be migrated to Alibaba Cloud, and vice versa. An enterprise that has built its data analytics, customer relationship management, cybersecurity, and digital commerce on the API ecosystem of a single cloud provider faces lock-in effects that make switching enormously costly — not just in dollars but in organizational knowledge, workflow integration, and retraining. The stickiness of digital infrastructure creates path dependencies that make services decoupling substantially more costly than goods decoupling.

The same logic applies to platform ecosystems. An Indian software development team that collaborates with US clients through GitHub, communicates via Slack, manages projects on Jira, and deploys on AWS is embedded in an American digital services ecosystem in ways that go far beyond any single contractual relationship. Redirecting these workflows to Chinese or domestically developed alternatives would require simultaneous migration across dozens of interdependent tools — a coordination problem that deters switching even when geopolitical incentives favor it. The implication for the fortress-versus-flexibility debate is that digital services integration, once established, is much harder to undo than goods trade integration. The friend-shoring thesis may be implementable for physical supply chains, but the digital services architecture of the global economy is more deeply entangled than the goods supply chains it increasingly supports.

## 16.5 The Discipline's Frontier: Methods for the Next Generation

As supply chains fracture and digital trade displaces physical trade, the standard gravity models of Chapter 3-B are no longer sufficient. A model built to explain bilateral flows of manufactured goods between countries — with distance, GDP, and a handful of institutional variables as predictors — cannot capture the network effects of platform-mediated services trade, the spatial spillovers of industrial policy, the heterogeneous treatment effects of place-based interventions, or the real-time dynamics of digital economies. The discipline is responding with a new methods frontier that this section surveys — not as a comprehensive review but as a map of the toolkit required to study the new era that the preceding sections have described.

### Machine Learning for Spatial Prediction

The application of machine learning (ML) methods to spatial economic problems is the most active methodological frontier in the field. ML methods — random forests, gradient boosting, neural networks, and their spatial variants — excel at prediction in high-dimensional settings where the researcher has many potential predictors and limited theoretical guidance about which ones matter. Spatial economic applications include:

- **Predicting regional economic outcomes** from satellite imagery, social media data, and other non-traditional data sources. Jean et al. (2016) used convolutional neural networks to predict poverty from daytime satellite imagery, achieving prediction accuracy comparable to survey-based estimates at a fraction of the cost. This approach is particularly valuable for the data-scarce environments described in Chapters 12 and 13, where conflict, institutional fragmentation, or state failure have degraded conventional statistical systems.

- **Foundation models for geospatial analysis.** Microsoft's Planetary Computer and Google Earth Engine now provide petabyte-scale access to satellite imagery with pre-trained models that can classify land use, detect urban expansion, estimate agricultural productivity, and measure nighttime light intensity — the same VIIRS data deployed in Lab 6 for the Africa panel. These platforms enable researchers to generate economic indicators for regions with no statistical infrastructure whatsoever, transforming the data landscape for exactly the settings (Sub-Saharan Africa, conflict zones, small island states) where traditional data is weakest. The combination of satellite imagery and ML is not a substitute for ground-truth surveys, but it provides a high-frequency, spatially granular baseline that surveys cannot match.

- **Estimating heterogeneous treatment effects** of spatial policies. Causal forests (Athey and Imbens 2016; Wager and Athey 2018) can estimate how the effect of a policy (e.g., EU Cohesion Funds, as in Lab 4) varies across regions with different characteristics, identifying which types of regions benefit most from intervention without requiring the researcher to specify the interaction terms in advance. The key question for regional policy — "which types of regions benefit from Cohesion Funds?" — is precisely the kind of heterogeneous treatment effect that causal forests are designed to estimate. The answer matters for policy design: if convergence funds work only in regions with pre-existing institutional capacity (as Chapter 9's evidence suggests), then targeting should reflect this heterogeneity rather than applying uniform allocation formulas.

- **Nowcasting economic activity** using high-frequency, spatially granular data. Mobile phone records, credit card transactions, electricity consumption, and traffic patterns can be combined with ML methods to produce estimates of local economic activity at daily or weekly frequencies — far more timely than the quarterly or annual data that traditional statistical systems produce.

- **Synthetic data for data-scarce settings.** Where survey access is restricted by conflict, institutional failure, or government censorship — the statistical collapse documented in Chapter 12 for Syria, Yemen, and Libya — ML methods can generate realistic synthetic microdata by learning distributional patterns from pre-conflict surveys, neighboring countries, or administrative records. The synthetic data approach does not replace measurement; it provides a structured framework for inference in settings where measurement has failed.

### Causal Inference with Spatial Interference

The standard assumption in causal inference — the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA) — holds that the treatment of one unit does not affect the outcomes of other units. In spatial economics, this assumption is routinely violated: treating one region (with a subsidy, a free zone, an infrastructure investment) affects neighboring regions through trade, migration, and knowledge spillovers. The emerging literature on causal inference with interference (or "spillovers") develops methods for estimating treatment effects when SUTVA fails:

- **Spatial difference-in-differences** models that allow treated regions to affect untreated neighbors. Butts (2021) extends the standard difference-in-differences framework to explicitly model treatment diffusion — the process by which the effects of a spatially concentrated intervention (a free trade zone, a transport infrastructure investment, a broadband rollout) propagate to neighboring regions through market linkages and knowledge spillovers. These models decompose the total effect of a treatment into a direct effect (on the treated region) and an indirect effect (on neighbors), using the spatial weight matrix framework from Chapter 3-A. The decomposition matters for policy evaluation: a program that appears to have a small direct effect may have large indirect effects that a conventional evaluation would miss entirely.

- **Instrumental variables with interference.** Huber and Steinmayr (2021) develop IV methods that remain valid when treatment of one unit affects outcomes of other units — a setting that arises whenever the instrument operates through spatially correlated channels. The standard exclusion restriction (the instrument affects the outcome only through the treatment) must be strengthened to account for the possibility that the instrument affects nearby units through spatial spillovers of the treatment itself.

- **Partial identification** approaches that bound the treatment effect without assuming SUTVA, providing a range of plausible effect sizes rather than a point estimate (Manski 2013).

- **Design-based approaches** that exploit the randomization or quasi-randomization of treatment assignment to identify causal effects even in the presence of interference (Aronow and Samii 2017).

The interference problem connects directly to every applied lab in this book. Lab 1's SAR model explicitly captures spatial spillovers through the spatial lag term. Lab 4's RDD estimates the effect of EU Cohesion Fund eligibility at an income threshold, but must worry about spillovers across the eligibility boundary — a treated region just below the threshold may affect an untreated region just above it, biasing the RDD estimate. Lab 5's synthetic control method assumes that the treated unit (a conflict-affected country) does not affect the donor pool (neighboring countries), but refugee flows and trade disruption routinely violate this assumption. The methods frontier surveyed here is not merely of academic interest; it addresses the identification challenges that students encountered in the labs.

### Network Models of Trade and Services

The gravity model — the workhorse of trade empirics and the foundation of Lab 7 — treats trade relationships as bilateral: each country pair has a trade flow that depends on their characteristics and the distance between them. Network models extend this framework to capture higher-order relationships: the trade between countries A and B may depend not just on their bilateral characteristics but on their positions in the global trade network (how many other partners they have, how central they are, whether they belong to the same trade community).

For services trade, network effects are particularly important. The value of a services hub (London, Singapore, Dubai) depends not just on its bilateral relationships but on its position in the global APS network — the fact that it is connected to many other hubs, and that those hubs are themselves well-connected. The Globalization and World Cities Research Network (GaWC) has developed the most systematic empirical approach to measuring these network positions. Taylor's (2004) interlocking model maps the global network of advanced producer services (APS) firms — law firms, accounting firms, management consultancies, financial services — by recording which cities host offices of the same firm. The resulting network reveals a clear hierarchy: London and New York at the apex, followed by Hong Kong, Singapore, Tokyo, Shanghai, and Dubai, with a long tail of secondary and tertiary cities whose global connectivity depends on their connections to these primary hubs.

Network-based trade models (Chaney 2014, Bernard and Moxnes 2018) capture these effects and produce predictions about trade patterns that the bilateral gravity model misses. The key insight is that the gravity model's bilateral structure treats each country pair as independent, but services trade operates through hub-and-spoke networks: a multinational law firm's London office serves clients throughout Europe, its Singapore office serves Southeast Asia, and its Dubai office serves the Middle East — and the value of each hub depends on the others. Multilayer network models extend this further by representing trade networks, financial networks, and migration networks as interconnected layers, with community detection algorithms revealing economic blocs that do not correspond to formal trade agreements. These methods are computationally demanding but increasingly accessible, and they offer a natural framework for studying the fortress-versus-flexibility question posed in Section 16.4.

### Quantitative Spatial Models

The quantitative spatial models (QSMs) surveyed by Redding and Rossi-Hansberg (2017) represent the most ambitious attempt to integrate spatial economics into a general equilibrium framework. These models combine NEG-style agglomeration forces, trade costs (including the gravity model's distance and institutional variables), labor mobility, and amenity differences into a unified framework that can be calibrated to data and used for counterfactual analysis. Their key advantage over reduced-form econometric approaches is the ability to answer "what if" questions: What if CBAM had been implemented in 2010 — how would the geography of carbon-intensive manufacturing have changed? What if AfCFTA achieves full services liberalization — which African cities would gain and which would lose? What if telemigration exposure doubles over the next decade — how would the spatial distribution of service-sector employment shift?

The dynamic extensions of these models — Allen and Arkolakis (2014) with forward-looking agents, Caliendo, Dvorkin, and Parro (2019) for trade policy analysis with labor mobility — are particularly relevant for the questions raised in this chapter. Static models cannot capture the adjustment dynamics that make trade policy politically contentious: a tariff change may improve long-run welfare but impose concentrated short-run costs on specific regions, and the political economy depends on the time path of adjustment, not just the steady-state outcome. Desmet and Rossi-Hansberg (2015) extend the QSM framework to incorporate climate change, allowing researchers to study how rising temperatures, changing agricultural productivity, and sea-level rise will interact with migration, trade, and agglomeration to reshape the spatial economy — a direct connection to the climate transition analysis of Chapter 15.

### Data Frontiers

The methods frontier is inseparable from the data frontier. The most powerful econometric method is useless without data at the appropriate spatial and temporal resolution, and the data landscape is changing as rapidly as the methods.

**Administrative data linking** — the combination of tax records, employment records, customs declarations, and social program data into integrated longitudinal datasets — is transforming spatial economics in countries with strong statistical infrastructure. The Scandinavian registries (which link every resident's employment, income, education, health, and migration history) have enabled foundational contributions to labor economics and spatial analysis. In the United States, the Census-IRS linkage and the Longitudinal Employer-Household Dynamics (LEHD) program provide spatially granular data on employment flows, firm dynamics, and income distribution. India's Goods and Services Tax Network (GSTN) generates transaction-level data on domestic trade flows that, if made accessible to researchers, would provide unprecedented insight into the spatial structure of the Indian economy. The challenge is access: administrative data is governed by privacy and confidentiality regulations that vary across jurisdictions, and the institutional arrangements for researcher access (secure data rooms, anonymization protocols, data use agreements) are as important as the data itself.

**Call detail records (CDR)** from mobile phone networks provide high-frequency, spatially granular data on population mobility, communication patterns, and economic activity. Blumenstock, Cadamuro, and On (2015) demonstrated that mobile phone metadata could predict poverty and wealth at fine spatial resolution in Rwanda, using calling patterns, mobility, and network structure as predictors. CDR data is particularly valuable for the developing-country settings covered in Chapters 8, 12, 13, and 14, where conventional data is sparse but mobile phone penetration is high.

**Firm-level customs data** — transaction-level records of imports and exports by individual firms — reveals the within-firm structure of global value chains that aggregate trade data conceals. Databases like BACI (CEPII) and Chinese customs data have enabled a generation of research on firm heterogeneity in trade (Melitz 2003), multi-product firms, and the GVC structure that the MRIO frameworks of Chapter 3-B measure at the sector level. The spatial dimension of firm-level trade data is especially valuable: it shows which regions within a country are integrated into global supply chains and which are disconnected — a question that aggregate national trade data cannot answer.

**Satellite imagery combined with ML** — the combination that enables Lab 6's nighttime lights analysis of African urbanization — represents perhaps the most transformative data frontier for spatial economics. The ability to measure economic activity from space, at arbitrary spatial resolution, at regular temporal intervals, for any location on Earth, regardless of local statistical capacity, is a qualitative advance in the discipline's observational capabilities. The challenge is validation: satellite-derived indicators must be benchmarked against ground-truth data, and the relationship between nighttime light intensity and economic output varies across regions, economic structures, and electrification rates (Henderson, Storeygard, and Weil 2012). But for the data-scarce settings that constitute much of this book's empirical terrain, satellite data is not merely a supplement to conventional statistics — it is the primary data source.

The next step beyond Henderson, Storeygard, and Weil's aggregate luminosity proxy is already visible. Yang, Ai, and Arkolakis (2025) combine ambient air-pollutant concentrations, surface temperature, and nighttime radiance to detect individual steel plants and estimate their output, achieving detection rates of 40-70 percent and output prediction $R^2$ exceeding 0.8. The advance is not merely incremental: it moves satellite-based economic measurement from aggregate regional proxying to industry-specific, plant-level, near-real-time observation. For the data-scarce settings analyzed in Chapters 12 and 13 — where conflict or institutional fragmentation has degraded statistical systems — the ability to track industrial production from orbit without relying on national statistical offices is genuinely transformative. The approach generalizes beyond steel: any industry with a distinctive spectral or thermal signature (cement, refining, power generation) becomes observable, opening a research agenda in which satellite imagery serves not as a proxy for GDP but as a direct measurement of spatially disaggregated production.

These frontier methods are increasingly accessible outside well-funded research labs. The open-source geospatial stack — OSMnx for network analysis (Boeing 2025), rasterio and geopandas for raster and vector processing, scikit-learn and PyTorch for the ML layer — makes satellite-based economic measurement feasible for researchers without ESRI licenses or cloud computing budgets. For scholars in the developing-country settings where satellite data is most needed, the democratization of tools matters as much as the democratization of data: the binding constraint is shifting from "Can I access the imagery?" to "Can I ask the right question?"

There is a pleasing reflexivity here. Nagaraj (2021) demonstrates that public geospatial data has measurable private-sector impacts: the Landsat program's free data release more than doubled gold discoveries and shifted market share from incumbents to entrants. Data infrastructure reshapes the economy it measures. But the geospatial ecosystem is itself subject to the oligopolistic structures this book analyzes — ESRI dominates GIS software, Google dominates consumer mapping, and the compute required for petabyte-scale analysis concentrates in a handful of cloud providers. The tools of spatial economic measurement are products of the spatial economy they claim to observe, a circularity that researchers would do well to acknowledge even as they exploit the extraordinary opportunities these tools provide.

## Data in Depth: The Services Trade Gravity Model

The gravity model for services trade — deployed in Lab 7's `gravity_services_scaffold.py` — extends the goods-trade gravity framework of Chapter 3-B to bilateral services flows. The basic specification is identical:

$$
\ln(X_{ij}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \ln(GDP_i) + \beta_2 \ln(GDP_j) + \beta_3 \ln(d_{ij}) + \gamma Z_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}
$$

where $X_{ij}$ is bilateral services trade from country $i$ to country $j$, $d_{ij}$ is distance, and $Z_{ij}$ is a vector of institutional and cultural variables (common language, colonial ties, shared legal origin, regional trade agreement membership). The key innovation for services is the inclusion of the OECD Services Trade Restrictiveness Index (STRI) — a composite measure of regulatory barriers to services trade across specific sectors (financial services, telecommunications, professional services, digital services, transport). The STRI ranges from 0 (completely open) to 1 (completely closed) and is computed separately for each country-sector pair, allowing researchers to estimate the tariff-equivalent trade cost of services regulation.

Kimura and Lee (2006) and Head, Mayer, and Ries (2009) established two empirical regularities that define the services gravity literature. First, the distance elasticity for services trade is often larger in absolute value than for goods trade — a counterintuitive finding given that services delivered digitally have zero transport costs. The explanation is that distance proxies for institutional and cultural barriers (time zone differences, legal system compatibility, language similarity, trust) that matter more for services than for goods. Second, language, colonial ties, and regulatory similarity have substantially larger effects on services trade than on goods trade, confirming that institutional distance is the dominant friction in services markets.

Several caveats are essential when working with services trade gravity models. Balance of payments (BOP) services trade data — the primary source for bilateral services flows — captures Mode 1 (cross-border delivery) and Mode 2 (consumption abroad, primarily travel) reasonably well but substantially undercounts Mode 3 (commercial presence). Foreign Affiliates Trade in Services (FATS) data, which captures Mode 3, exists for only approximately 30 countries, overwhelmingly OECD members. The gravity model estimated on BOP data therefore captures at best two-thirds of global services trade, with the missing third biased toward the commercial-presence mode that is most important for advanced producer services. The zero-flow problem is also more severe for services than for goods: many bilateral country pairs report zero services trade, which may reflect true zeros (no services trade occurs) or measurement failure (the trade occurs but is not captured in BOP statistics). Poisson pseudo-maximum likelihood (PPML) estimation — implemented in Lab 7's `ppml_estimator.py` — addresses this problem by estimating the gravity equation in levels rather than logs, naturally accommodating zeros without dropping observations. Finally, mirror statistics — where country A reports different services trade with country B than country B reports with country A — are common and often substantial, reflecting differences in measurement methodology, coverage, and classification across national statistical offices.

## Institutional Spotlight: The WTO and the Stalled Services Revolution

The General Agreement on Trade in Services (GATS), negotiated during the Uruguay Round and entering into force in 1995, was the first multilateral framework for services trade. Its architecture was deliberately cautious: a positive-list approach in which each WTO member specified the sectors it committed to liberalize, the modes of supply it would open, and the limitations it retained. Progressive liberalization — the expectation that commitments would deepen over successive negotiating rounds — was the dynamic mechanism that was supposed to transform the GATS from a modest first step into a comprehensive services trade regime.

That mechanism never engaged. The Doha Round, launched in 2001 with services as a central pillar, collapsed under the weight of agricultural subsidies disputes, North-South disagreements over development flexibilities, and the rise of bilateral and regional agreements that offered faster results. By 2013, a coalition of twenty-three WTO members — representing approximately 70 percent of global services trade — launched the Trade in Services Agreement (TISA) negotiations as a plurilateral alternative, hoping to achieve among a willing subset what multilateral consensus could not deliver. TISA negotiations progressed through 21 rounds and produced draft texts on financial services, telecommunications, e-commerce, professional services, and domestic regulation. But the negotiations collapsed in 2016, undone by three irreconcilable positions. The United States insisted on negative listing and strong disciplines on state-owned enterprises (SOEs), which developing-country participants resisted as constraints on industrial policy space. The EU demanded that any data-flow provisions be compatible with GDPR, a requirement that the United States and several Asian participants viewed as disguised protectionism. And the exclusion of China from the negotiations undermined the agreement's commercial relevance — a services trade framework covering 70 percent of global trade but excluding the world's second-largest economy was of questionable value.

The most recent multilateral achievement — the Joint Initiative on Services Domestic Regulation, concluded in 2021 among 67 WTO members — is modest in scope but symbolically important. It establishes disciplines on licensing procedures, qualification requirements, and technical standards for services suppliers, reducing the administrative barriers that the OECD STRI measures. The provisions are procedural rather than substantive: they do not require countries to open new sectors or deepen existing commitments, but they require that whatever regulations countries maintain be transparent, non-discriminatory, and administered in a reasonable manner. As the first multilateral services outcome in a quarter-century, it demonstrates that WTO-based services negotiation is not entirely dead — but its limited ambition also demonstrates how far the institution has fallen from the progressive-liberalization vision of the GATS.

The institutional irony is sharp. Multilateral services liberalization has stalled while bilateral and regional agreements proliferate — USMCA's Chapter 19, the EU Services Directive and Digital Single Market, CPTPP's comprehensive services provisions, AfCFTA's Services Protocol — creating exactly the regulatory fragmentation that a multilateral framework was supposed to prevent. Each regional agreement has its own listing approach, its own rules on data flows, its own dispute settlement mechanism, and its own mutual recognition arrangements. The result is what Bhagwati (2008) called a "spaghetti bowl" of overlapping bilateral commitments — but for services, the strands are thicker and more tangled than for goods, because services regulation is inherently more complex (involving professional licensing, prudential standards, data governance, and institutional compatibility that goods tariffs do not require). For developing countries with limited trade negotiation capacity, the proliferation of bilateral frameworks increases compliance costs, fragments legal obligations, and reduces the benefits of services trade liberalization by requiring adaptation to multiple incompatible regulatory standards. The WTO's failure to provide a multilateral services framework is thus not merely an institutional disappointment — it is an active contributor to the regulatory fragmentation that the Splinternet analysis of Section 16.2 describes.

## Conclusion: What the Discipline Does Next

In sixteen chapters and seven applied labs, this book has documented how economic activity organizes itself in space — driven by the interaction of agglomeration forces, institutional frameworks, trade networks, and geographic fundamentals. The evidence ranges from Samsung's Hwaseong campus and TSMC's Hsinchu cluster, where semiconductor fabrication concentrates in specialized nodes that combine scale economies, tacit knowledge, and state-nurtured infrastructure (Chapter 6), to the Gaziantep textile agglomeration, where Syrian refugee labor and Turkish entrepreneurial networks created an unlikely cluster in a conflict-adjacent border zone (Chapter 12). It encompasses M-Pesa's Nairobi ecosystem, where mobile money leapfrogged the banking infrastructure that a century of colonial and post-colonial governance had failed to provide (Chapter 13), and the EU's Cohesion Fund experiment, where hundreds of billions of euros in transfers tested whether policy could override the centripetal forces that concentrate prosperity in core regions (Chapter 9). It includes the IRA's emerging Battery Belt, where green industrial policy is reshaping the manufacturing geography of the American South (Chapter 4), and the Gulf states' sovereign wealth funds, whose outward investments connect desert petroeconomies to the global financial network (Chapter 11). The common thread across all these cases is that institutions mediate everything. Market forces create spatial patterns — agglomeration, specialization, core-periphery hierarchies — but institutions determine whether those patterns produce shared prosperity or concentrated extraction, resilient growth or fragile dependence, inclusive cities or exclusionary enclaves.

The three forces examined in this chapter — telemigration, the Splinternet, and the fortress-versus-flexibility question — will determine the spatial economics of the next quarter-century. Telemigration may partially equalize service-sector wages across countries, but institutional frictions, time zones, and regulatory barriers will limit its reach. The Splinternet may fragment the digital economy into competing regulatory zones, but the economic logic of interconnection will push back against fragmentation. And the fortress-versus-flexibility question will be resolved not by economic logic alone but by the geopolitical decisions of great powers — decisions that will redistribute comparative advantage across regions in ways that no economic model can fully predict.

The optimistic scenario is worth stating clearly. The AfCFTA Services Protocol, if implemented with the political commitment that the goods protocol has so far struggled to achieve, would create the world's largest services market by population — and the one with the greatest potential for intra-regional services trade creation, precisely because it starts from the lowest base. Telemigration, for all its distributional tensions, enables skilled workers in Lagos, Dhaka, and Lima to access global wages without the wrenching dislocation of physical migration — a Pareto improvement if complementary policies (retraining, social insurance, regulatory adaptation) manage the adjustment costs in developed countries. Green industrial policy, if designed with the place-based sensitivity that Chapter 15 advocates, can generate new agglomeration economies in developing countries — renewable energy manufacturing clusters, critical mineral processing hubs, sustainable agriculture value chains — that reduce dependence on fossil fuel exports while building institutional capacity. And digital public infrastructure of the India Stack variety, if successfully adapted to other institutional contexts, can bypass the governance weaknesses that have constrained formal-sector development in much of the Global South — providing identity verification, payment systems, and data infrastructure as public goods rather than private monopolies.

The pessimistic scenario is equally worth confronting. The Splinternet fragments digital trade into three or four regulatory zones, raising compliance costs and reducing the efficiency gains of cross-border digital services. Friend-shoring excludes developing countries from the technology supply chains on which their industrial upgrading depends — a new form of exclusion that reproduces the core-periphery hierarchy through geopolitical rather than market mechanisms. CBAM, the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, penalizes the carbon-intensive manufacturing that is often the first rung on the industrialization ladder, closing the door that Europe, East Asia, and China each climbed through. Climate change strands the most vulnerable regions — the Sahel, the small island states, the river deltas of South and Southeast Asia — in a spiral of environmental degradation, conflict, and institutional failure that no amount of spatial economic analysis can reverse. And AI, the technology that promised to democratize access to high-skill services through translation, code generation, and content creation, may instead eliminate the telemigration jobs before developing-country workers can capture them — automating the very "missing middle" tasks that platforms were beginning to make tradable.

Between these scenarios lies the actual future, which — as always — will be shaped by choices that no model fully captures. But the discipline of spatial economics — armed with the satellite data, machine learning methods, quantitative spatial models, and causal inference tools surveyed in this chapter — has never been better equipped to study these questions. The data is richer than anything previous generations of spatial economists could access. The methods are more powerful. The questions are more urgent. Whether the answers the discipline produces will inform policy or merely document decline depends on choices that lie outside any model. The spatial economy is not a natural phenomenon to be observed. It is a human construction that can be reconstructed. That reconstruction — more equitable, more resilient, more aware of the institutional foundations on which prosperity depends — is the work to which this book hopes to contribute.
