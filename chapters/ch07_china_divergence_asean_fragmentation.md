# Chapter 7: China's Internal Divergence and ASEAN Fragmentation

## Introduction: Two Economies in One Country

In 2023, the per-capita GDP of Shanghai was approximately $27,000 — comparable to Portugal or the Czech Republic and well above the World Bank's high-income threshold. The per-capita GDP of Gansu province, 1,800 kilometers to the west, was approximately $5,800 — comparable to Guatemala or Uzbekistan. Both are administered by the same central government, operate under the same national legal system, use the same currency, and are connected by high-speed rail that covers the distance in under eight hours. The income ratio between the richest and poorest Chinese provinces is approximately 4.5:1 — wider than the gap between the richest and poorest US states (roughly 2:1) and comparable to the gap between northwestern and southeastern Europe.

This internal divergence is not a residual of China's pre-reform backwardness. It is, in significant part, a product of the reform itself. The institutional architecture of China's opening — the sequencing of Special Economic Zones, the fiscal decentralization that rewarded coastal provinces with a larger share of revenue from foreign trade, the Hukou system that restricted rural-to-urban labor mobility while permitting massive informal migration without full citizenship rights — generated a spatial pattern in which capital, technology, and high-productivity employment concentrated in the coastal mega-regions (the Pearl River Delta, the Yangtze River Delta, and Jing-Jin-Ji) while interior provinces remained dependent on agriculture, resource extraction, and central government transfer payments.

Chapter 6 examined how East Asian developmental states engineered technological upgrading through coordinated industrial policy. This chapter asks what happens when the developmental state operates at continental scale and with institutional fractures: a country large enough to contain multiple economic systems, governed by a political structure that incentivizes spatial competition between provinces, and connected to a regional economy — ASEAN — whose own integration is fragmented by sovereignty boundaries, regulatory divergence, and the gravitational pull of the Chinese economy itself.

The chapter proceeds in six sections. Section 7.1 analyzes China's coastal-inland divergence through the institutional lens of Hukou, fiscal federalism, and sequential liberalization. Section 7.2 examines medical tourism as a case study in Mode 2 services trade geography, using Thailand and Singapore to show how institutional design creates spatially concentrated services export clusters. Section 7.3 analyzes ASEAN platform economies — Grab, Gojek, and the regional digital ecosystem — as experiments in overcoming regulatory fragmentation through network effects. Section 7.4 examines the Belt and Road Initiative and the Digital Silk Road as spatial policy instruments that reshape ASEAN economic geography. Section 7.5 addresses climate vulnerability and managed retreat in the Mekong Delta and Pearl River Delta, and China's renewable energy industrial policy as spatial policy. Section 7.6 connects the analysis to Lab 2, interpreting the convergence evidence from the TiVA-based panel.

## 7.1 China's Great Divergence: Institutions, Not Geography

### Sequential Liberalization and the Coastal Premium

China's reform and opening, launched by Deng Xiaoping in 1978, was deliberately sequential. The first Special Economic Zones — Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shantou, and Xiamen — were all coastal, all in Guangdong and Fujian provinces, and all chosen for their proximity to Hong Kong, Macao, and Taiwan. The logic was institutional experimentation: create bounded zones where foreign investment, market pricing, and export-oriented manufacturing could operate under rules different from the planned economy, observe the results, and expand the model if successful.

The model was spectacularly successful. Shenzhen's population grew from roughly 30,000 in 1979 to over 17 million by 2023, and its GDP per capita surpassed Hong Kong's in 2018. The key institutional innovation was not tariff reduction or tax incentives alone — these were available in export-processing zones across the developing world. It was the combination of secure (if not fully transparent) property rights for foreign investors, a disciplined and increasingly skilled labor force drawn from interior provinces, infrastructure investment coordinated with industrial zone development, and — crucially — institutional insulation from the bureaucratic constraints that still governed the rest of the economy. The SEZ was not merely a geographic area with different tax rates; it was an institutional enclave with a different relationship to the state.

The sequential expansion of SEZ-like privileges — from the original four zones to fourteen Open Coastal Cities (1984), to Pudong (1990), to the Western Development Strategy (1999), to the inland Free Trade Zones of the 2010s — created a spatial gradient of institutional openness that persisted for decades. Regions that entered the liberalized system earlier accumulated advantages — foreign direct investment, technological spillovers from multinational firms, human capital development through learning-by-doing — that compounded over time. The path dependence logic of Chapter 2 is visible here at continental scale: early institutional advantage → capital and talent inflow → capability accumulation → further advantage.

### Fiscal Federalism and Provincial Competition

The 1994 Tax Sharing Reform reshaped the incentives facing provincial and municipal governments in ways that deepened the coastal-inland divide. Under the pre-1994 system, provincial governments negotiated revenue-sharing contracts with the center, creating heterogeneous fiscal arrangements across provinces. The 1994 reform centralized the most productive tax instruments (VAT, enterprise income tax) while leaving provinces dependent on land revenues and local fees for discretionary spending.

The consequence was a system that Montinola, Qian, and Weingast (1995) characterized as "market-preserving federalism": local governments competed for mobile capital by offering favorable conditions — infrastructure, regulatory flexibility, land at below-market prices — while the central government maintained macroeconomic control. The competition was productive in coastal regions where the infrastructure, location, and human capital endowments made them attractive to foreign and domestic investors. In interior provinces, the same competitive incentives produced less investment because the underlying conditions were less favorable — the fiscal competition rewarded regions that were already advantaged, amplifying the spatial gradient.

Land finance deepened this dynamic. Coastal municipalities discovered that they could fund infrastructure investment by selling land use rights to developers, using the infrastructure investment to attract manufacturing, and using the resulting employment and tax revenue to justify further land sales. The circular logic worked because coastal land values were rising rapidly — driven by the same agglomeration economies that Chapter 1 described. Interior municipalities with weaker demand for industrial and residential land could not generate the same fiscal spiral, leaving them dependent on central transfer payments that, while substantial, could not replicate the self-reinforcing growth dynamic of the coast.

### The Hukou System: Institutional Barriers to Spatial Equilibrium

The Hukou (household registration) system is the single most important institutional barrier to spatial equilibrium within China. Established in 1958, Hukou ties access to public services — education, healthcare, pensions, housing subsidies — to an individual's place of registration, which is typically inherited from parents and linked to their rural or urban place of origin. A worker from rural Henan who migrates to Shenzhen can legally work in Shenzhen's factories, but their children cannot attend Shenzhen's public schools on the same terms as locally registered residents, they cannot access Shenzhen's public healthcare or pension systems, and they cannot purchase subsidized housing.

The result is a two-tier urban population: registered residents with full citizenship rights and migrants with limited access to urban public goods. As of 2023, approximately 300 million Chinese citizens lived and worked in cities where they did not hold Hukou registration — a population larger than every country in the world except China, India, and the United States. These migrants provide the labor that drives coastal manufacturing and urban services, but they are statistically counted at their place of registration rather than their actual place of residence and work, and they are excluded from the social welfare systems of the cities where they produce value.

The spatial economic implications are profound. In a neoclassical world without institutional barriers, the coastal-inland wage gap would generate migration flows that eventually equalize factor prices. The Hukou system permits the migration of labor but prevents the migration of citizenship, creating a persistent wedge between the spatial distribution of production and the spatial distribution of welfare. Coastal mega-regions receive the productive contribution of migrant workers without bearing the full fiscal cost of their social reproduction (education, healthcare, retirement). Interior provinces bear those costs — schools for left-behind children, healthcare for returned elderly migrants — without receiving the tax revenue that the migrants' labor generates. This is not a market failure; it is an institutional design that subsidizes coastal agglomeration at the expense of interior provinces.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: The Hukou Distortion**
>
> China's official population statistics assign individuals to their Hukou registration location, not their actual place of residence and work. This creates a systematic mismeasurement of approximately 300 million internal migrants who live and work in coastal cities but are statistically counted in their rural home provinces. The distortion cascades into per-capita GDP calculations (overstating inland GDP per capita, understating coastal GDP per capita), urbanization rates (China's de facto urbanization rate exceeds its de jure rate by roughly 15 percentage points), and fiscal burden estimates (interior provinces appear to have more residents per unit of public expenditure than they actually serve).
>
> Researchers must use census micro-data (the decennial census and the inter-census 1% population survey), mobile phone location data (Baidu Migration Index, Tencent Mobility), or satellite-derived proxies (night-lights as a cross-check on population and economic activity claims) to approximate actual population distributions. Chapter 6's Spatial Data Challenge on Chinese provincial GDP manipulation compounds the problem: not only is the population denominator wrong, but the GDP numerator is also potentially unreliable. Any panel regression using Chinese sub-national data should treat both sides of the per-capita calculation as measured with substantial and likely non-random error.

## 7.2 Medical Tourism: Mode 2 Services Trade and Institutional Design

### Bumrungrad and the Thai Model

Thailand's emergence as the world's leading medical tourism destination is a case study in how deliberate institutional design creates spatially concentrated services export clusters. Bumrungrad International Hospital in Bangkok — which treats over 500,000 international patients per year from more than 190 countries — did not arise from comparative advantage in medical training or from lower healthcare costs alone. It arose from a coordinated institutional strategy involving the hospital's management, the Thai government, international accreditation bodies, and the insurance industry.

The institutional architecture has four components. First, **international accreditation**: Bumrungrad was among the first hospitals outside the United States to receive Joint Commission International (JCI) accreditation, providing a quality signal legible to Western patients and their insurance companies. JCI accreditation requires compliance with over 1,200 standards covering patient safety, infection control, clinical governance, and data management — an institutional transplant that raised Thai hospital practice to internationally recognized levels. Second, **visa facilitation**: the Thai government created a Medical Tourism Visa (MT visa) allowing stays of up to 90 days with simplified entry procedures for patients and accompanying family members. Third, **insurance partnerships**: Bumrungrad established billing relationships with over 200 international insurance companies, allowing patients to use their home-country health insurance for treatment in Bangkok — eliminating the out-of-pocket financing barrier that constrains medical tourism in less institutionally developed destinations. Fourth, **dedicated international patient infrastructure**: separate international patient departments with multilingual staff, embassy liaison services, and airport transfer coordination — institutional features that reduce the transaction costs of seeking healthcare abroad.

The spatial economics of this institutional package are revealing. Medical tourism concentrates overwhelmingly in Bangkok, not in Thailand's other cities. The agglomeration logic is the same as for APS clusters (Chapter 1, Chapter 4): specialist medical capabilities require a density of complementary services — diagnostic laboratories, surgical equipment suppliers, pharmaceutical distribution, rehabilitation facilities — that only a large urban market can sustain. Once established, the cluster attracts further specialist investment (Bumrungrad added a dedicated cancer center, a spine institute, and a dental hospital), which attracts more international patients, which justifies further specialist investment. The circular causation is identical to the NEG model's forward and backward linkages, applied to healthcare services rather than manufacturing.

### Singapore's Complementary Model

Singapore positions itself as the premium alternative to Thailand, targeting patients from the Middle East and Southeast Asia who seek the highest-end specialist care. The institutional strategy differs from Thailand's in emphasizing regulatory environment over cost competitiveness: Singapore's Biomedical Sciences Initiative, launched in 2000, invested $3 billion in medical research infrastructure, recruited leading international clinician-scientists, and positioned Singapore as a regional hub for clinical trials and experimental treatments. The Medical Registration Act and the Private Hospitals and Medical Clinics Act provide a regulatory framework that international patients and insurers perceive as comparable to the US or UK — a perception that supports premium pricing.

The Mode 2 services trade implications connect directly to Chapter 3-B's gravity model framework. Medical tourism flows exhibit strong distance elasticities — patients from the Middle East travel to Bangkok and Singapore, patients from North Africa travel to Turkey, patients from Central America travel to the United States — because the transaction costs of medical travel (language barriers, visa requirements, insurance compatibility, cultural familiarity with healthcare systems) increase with distance in ways that parallel the informational and institutional barriers that drive services trade distance elasticities generally. The gravity model predicts that reducing these transaction costs — through accreditation harmonization, insurance portability agreements, and visa facilitation — should increase bilateral medical tourism flows, and the Thai and Singaporean cases confirm this prediction.

## 7.3 ASEAN Platform Economies: Network Effects Against Regulatory Fragmentation

### Grab and Gojek: Building Integration from Below

The ASEAN Economic Community, established in 2015, aspires to create a single market and production base across ten member states with a combined population of 680 million and GDP of approximately $3.6 trillion. The aspiration is far from realized. Non-tariff barriers remain pervasive: divergent product standards, incompatible professional licensing regimes, restrictive rules of origin, and limited mutual recognition of qualifications constrain the free movement of goods, services, capital, and skilled labor that the AEC blueprint envisions.

Against this backdrop, two platform companies — Grab (headquartered in Singapore, founded by Malaysian entrepreneurs) and Gojek (headquartered in Jakarta) — have achieved a degree of regional economic integration that formal trade agreements have not. Grab operates in eight ASEAN countries, providing ride-hailing, food delivery, package delivery, digital payments, insurance, and lending services through a single platform. Gojek (now part of GoTo Group after merging with Tokopedia) operates primarily in Indonesia, Vietnam, and Singapore with a similarly broad service portfolio.

The platform firms' expansion strategies reveal the binding constraints on ASEAN integration with a precision that trade statistics cannot match. Each new market entry required separate negotiations with national regulators over licensing requirements (ride-hailing regulations differ in every ASEAN country), payment system regulations (e-money licensing, know-your-customer requirements, foreign ownership restrictions on financial institutions), data localization rules (Indonesia and Vietnam require certain categories of data to be stored domestically), and labor regulations (the classification of platform workers as employees versus independent contractors varies across jurisdictions).

The spatial economics of platform expansion follow a city-by-city, country-by-country pattern that would not exist in a genuinely integrated single market. Grab entered Malaysia first (2012), then the Philippines (2013), Singapore (2013), Thailand (2013), Vietnam (2014), Indonesia (2014), Myanmar (2017), and Cambodia (2017). Each entry required a dedicated regulatory team, local banking partnerships, and adaptation to local payment preferences (cash-heavy markets like Indonesia and Vietnam required different payment architectures than card-heavy Singapore). The expansion sequence was determined not by market size alone but by regulatory accessibility — a revealed preference index of ASEAN's institutional fragmentation.

### Digital Payments and Financial Inclusion

The most consequential spatial effect of ASEAN platform economies is financial inclusion. GrabPay, GoPay, and their competitors have brought digital payment capabilities to populations that formal banking systems had not reached. In Indonesia, where only 49 percent of adults held a bank account in 2017 (World Bank Global Findex), GoPay reached over 40 million users by 2023 — many of them first-time participants in the formal financial system. The platform acts as an institutional bridge: providing the trust infrastructure (escrow, dispute resolution, transaction records) that formal banking requires, without requiring the physical branch network, minimum balance requirements, and documentation that exclude low-income and informal-sector workers.

The spatial implication is a partial equalization of financial access across the urban hierarchy. In a pre-platform world, financial services were concentrated in capital cities and major commercial centers where bank branch density was sufficient to serve retail customers. GoPay and GrabPay operate wherever the platform's ride-hailing and delivery services operate — which, because those services follow population density rather than bank regulation, extends to secondary and tertiary cities that formal banking underserves. This is a technology-mediated reduction in the effective economic distance between peripheral cities and the financial center — analogous to M-Pesa's effect in East Africa (Chapter 13), but achieved through a private platform rather than a telecommunications operator.

## 7.4 The Belt and Road Initiative as Spatial Policy

### Infrastructure Corridors and Economic Geography

China's Belt and Road Initiative, launched in 2013, is the largest infrastructure investment program in history — encompassing, by various estimates, $500 billion to $1 trillion in committed or completed projects across more than 140 countries. In Southeast Asia, BRI projects include the China-Laos Railway (completed December 2021, connecting Kunming to Vientiane), the East Coast Rail Link in Malaysia (under construction, connecting the South China Sea coast to the Strait of Malacca), the Jakarta-Bandung High-Speed Railway (completed October 2023), and multiple port, highway, and special economic zone developments across the region.

The spatial economics of BRI infrastructure are analytically distinct from conventional infrastructure investment in two ways. First, the projects are planned and financed primarily to serve China's trade connectivity objectives rather than the recipient country's internal development priorities. The China-Laos Railway, for example, is a $5.9 billion investment (roughly one-third of Laos' GDP at initiation) that connects Kunming — a provincial capital in southwestern China seeking better access to Southeast Asian markets — to Vientiane, the Laotian capital. The railway does not connect Laos' major population centers to each other; it connects China to the Mekong region through Laos. The spatial effect is to strengthen north-south connectivity (from China through mainland Southeast Asia) at the expense of east-west connectivity (between ASEAN members) — reinforcing a hub-spoke pattern centered on China rather than a multipolar ASEAN network.

Second, BRI projects frequently include institutional provisions — special economic zones, customs facilitation agreements, Chinese-built digital infrastructure — that extend Chinese institutional models into recipient countries. The Sihanoukville Special Economic Zone in Cambodia, built and operated by Chinese firms, employs largely Chinese workers, operates under management practices imported from Shenzhen's export-processing zones, and generates limited backward linkages to the Cambodian economy. This is the enclave dynamic that Chapter 14 documented for African regional hegemons, replicated in Southeast Asia with Chinese characteristics: infrastructure connectivity without institutional integration, investment without capability transfer.

### The Digital Silk Road

The less visible but potentially more consequential dimension of BRI is the Digital Silk Road — Chinese investment in telecommunications infrastructure (Huawei 5G networks, submarine cable systems, fiber-optic backbone), cloud computing (Alibaba Cloud, Huawei Cloud, Tencent Cloud data centers), and digital platforms (TikTok, Shopee, Lazada) across Southeast Asia.

The spatial economics of digital infrastructure differ from physical infrastructure in a crucial way: digital networks exhibit strong winner-take-all dynamics because of network effects, data accumulation advantages, and switching costs. Once a country's telecommunications backbone is built on Huawei equipment, the switching cost to an alternative vendor is enormous — not because the physical equipment cannot be replaced, but because the software ecosystem, maintenance contracts, and trained workforce are all vendor-specific. Similarly, once consumers adopt a platform ecosystem (TikTok for entertainment, Shopee for commerce, GrabPay for payments), the data those platforms accumulate makes them increasingly difficult to displace.

The geopolitical dimension intersects with the spatial economics through data localization and digital sovereignty. Indonesia's Government Regulation No. 71 (2019) requires electronic system operators to store certain data categories on servers located within Indonesia. Vietnam's Cybersecurity Law (2018) imposes similar requirements. These data localization rules create a new geography of digital infrastructure — a geography that is partly determined by technical efficiency (data center location should minimize latency and energy costs) and partly by regulatory sovereignty (governments want data within their jurisdiction for surveillance, taxation, and industrial policy purposes). The result is a fragmented digital landscape where the physical geography of servers and submarine cables reflects regulatory choices as much as engineering optimization.

## 7.5 Climate, Managed Retreat, and Renewable Energy Geography

### The Mekong Delta and Pearl River Delta: Sea-Level Rise at Scale

The two river deltas that anchor Southeast and East Asian economic geography — the Pearl River Delta (PRD) and the Mekong Delta — face existential climate risks that will reshape regional economic geography within the timescale of infrastructure investments made today.

The **Mekong Delta** produces roughly half of Vietnam's rice and over 60 percent of its fisheries output. It is home to approximately 17 million people. Sea-level rise projections — even conservative IPCC AR6 estimates of 0.3 to 0.6 meters by 2100 under SSP2-4.5 — would inundate substantial areas of the delta, and saltwater intrusion is already affecting rice cultivation in coastal provinces. The World Bank estimates that a one-meter sea-level rise (consistent with higher-emission scenarios) would displace 10–12 million people from the Mekong Delta alone. Where these people go — to Ho Chi Minh City, to Hanoi, to other ASEAN countries — is a spatial economics question with enormous implications for urban planning, housing markets, and labor markets across the region.

The **Pearl River Delta** — the manufacturing heartland that includes Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Dongguan, Foshan, and Hong Kong — faces a different but equally consequential climate risk: intensifying typhoons, flooding, and heat stress that threaten both physical infrastructure and worker productivity. The PRD generates roughly 10 percent of China's GDP. Climate adaptation for the PRD is not an environmental policy question; it is an industrial policy question: protecting the infrastructure, supply chains, and labor force that sustain one of the world's most productive manufacturing clusters.

The institutional challenge of managed retreat — the planned relocation of population and economic activity from areas that will become uninhabitable or unproductive due to climate change — is particularly acute in authoritarian systems where top-down relocation can proceed without the democratic deliberation that slows adaptation in liberal democracies, but also without the feedback mechanisms that ensure relocated populations' interests are represented. China's history of large-scale population relocation (the Three Gorges Dam displaced over 1.3 million people) provides precedent but not reassurance: the relocated populations suffered significant welfare losses that were incompletely compensated, and the economic activity that replaced them in reservoir areas did not reach the productivity of what was lost.

### China's Renewable Energy Industrial Policy as Spatial Policy

China's dominance in renewable energy manufacturing — producing over 80 percent of the world's solar panels and over 60 percent of its wind turbines — has a spatial dimension that connects to the coastal-inland divergence story. Solar panel manufacturing has concentrated in provinces with access to polysilicon supply chains (Xinjiang, Inner Mongolia), low-cost electricity (Yunnan, Sichuan with hydropower), and export logistics (Jiangsu, Zhejiang with port access). This is one of the few manufacturing sectors where interior provinces have a genuine comparative advantage: the energy intensity of polysilicon refining means that electricity costs matter more than proximity to seaports.

The spatial rebalancing potential of renewable energy deployment is even more significant than the manufacturing geography. China's solar and wind resources are concentrated in the north and west — Inner Mongolia, Gansu, Xinjiang, Qinghai — precisely the provinces that the coastal-oriented development model has disadvantaged. The State Council's "dual carbon" targets (peak emissions by 2030, carbon neutrality by 2060) require massive renewable energy deployment in these interior provinces, creating infrastructure investment, construction employment, and operations jobs in regions that have limited participation in China's export-oriented growth model.

The constraint is transmission infrastructure. Delivering renewable electricity from Inner Mongolia to Shanghai requires ultra-high-voltage (UHV) transmission lines spanning thousands of kilometers — a technology that China has developed more aggressively than any other country, but that faces engineering, financial, and inter-provincial coordination challenges. The institutional barrier is familiar from this chapter's earlier discussion of fiscal federalism: provinces that generate renewable electricity have different fiscal interests from provinces that consume it, and the central government must coordinate the inter-provincial cost sharing.

## 7.6 Lab 2 and the East Asian Convergence Evidence

Lab 2 provides the empirical framework for testing whether the spatial patterns described in this chapter and Chapter 6 produce convergence or divergence in East Asian domestic value-added trajectories. Using OECD TiVA data for ten Asian economies (Japan, Korea, China, Taiwan, Thailand, Malaysia, Vietnam, Indonesia, the Philippines, and Singapore) over 2000–2022, Lab 2 estimates $\beta$-convergence in domestic value-added shares for electronics exports.

The econometric specification follows the standard convergence regression:

$$
\Delta \ln(\text{DVA}_{it}) = \alpha + \beta \ln(\text{DVA}_{i,t-1}) + \varepsilon_{it}
$$

where $\beta < 0$ indicates convergence (economies with lower initial DVA shares grow faster) and $\beta > 0$ indicates divergence. The convergence half-life — the time required for half the initial gap to close — is computed as $t_{1/2} = \ln(2) / |\beta|$.

The results are substantively revealing. The full panel shows weak convergence ($\beta \approx -1.15$, $p \approx 0.10$), consistent with the flying-geese hypothesis at the broadest level: laggard economies are catching up in domestic value-added share, though the relationship is noisy. But the convergence is driven entirely by the ASEAN-6 subgroup ($\beta \approx -3.6$, $p < 0.02$), where Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia have been rapidly increasing their domestic content in electronics exports — precisely the upgrading pattern that the flying-geese model predicts when institutional conditions permit capability accumulation.

The Northeast Asian core (China, Japan, Korea) shows **no convergence** ($\beta \approx 0.2$, $p > 0.9$). This non-result is itself informative: the three economies with the highest domestic value-added shares are not converging toward each other. Japan's share is stable at a high level (reflecting its position as an upstream supplier of materials and equipment), Korea's share is rising through semiconductor upgrading, and China's share is rising through import substitution and domestic supplier development. They are on parallel tracks rather than converging toward a common level.

The ex-China specification ($\beta \approx -3.1$, $p < 0.001$) shows very strong convergence among the nine non-Chinese economies, suggesting that China's unique trajectory (massive scale, state-directed upgrading, geopolitical disruption of supply chains) is an outlier that weakens the convergence pattern when included.

The interaction with this chapter's institutional analysis is direct. The Hukou system, fiscal federalism, and sequential liberalization described in Section 7.1 contribute to China's outlier status: China's domestic value-added growth is driven not by market-mediated convergence dynamics but by state-directed industrial policy at a scale that no other economy in the sample can replicate. The ASEAN convergence, by contrast, reflects the market-mediated upgrading that the flying-geese model describes: multinational firms relocating labor-intensive production stages to lower-cost ASEAN economies, transferring technology and management practices in the process, and creating the capability base from which domestic firms eventually emerge.

> **Spatial Data Challenge: ASEAN Regulatory Fragmentation and Data Comparability**
>
> Cross-country economic analysis in ASEAN confronts a data landscape that is substantially more heterogeneous than East Asia or Europe. National statistical offices in ASEAN-10 vary enormously in capacity, methodology, and reporting frequency. Singapore publishes quarterly GDP by sector with a two-month lag; Myanmar's GDP statistics are annual and of uncertain reliability. Manufacturing census data, which is essential for calculating domestic value-added at the firm level, is available for Thailand (annually), Vietnam (intermittently), Indonesia (irregularly), and not at all for Laos, Cambodia, or Myanmar.
>
> The WIOD resolves this heterogeneity for the economies it covers (Indonesia, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan are included; Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines are added in some extensions), but at the cost of imposing a common sector classification that may not reflect the actual structure of production in less diversified economies. The TiVA database provides broader coverage but at a coarser sector level. Researchers working with ASEAN panel data should report results with and without the less reliably measured economies and should treat cross-country comparisons with appropriate caution.

## Data in Depth: Cross-Validating Chinese Provincial GDP with Night-Lights

**The question.** How much of the variation in Chinese provincial GDP is genuine economic activity, and how much is statistical manipulation? The well-documented "GDP inflation" problem — provincial GDP totals routinely exceeding the national total by 5–10 percent — raises fundamental questions about the reliability of China's sub-national economic statistics.

**Data sources.**
- **China National Bureau of Statistics (NBS)** provincial GDP panels, available from provincial statistical yearbooks and the CEIC database.
- **VIIRS (Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite)** nighttime lights, available from NOAA/NCEI at approximately 750-meter resolution. Annual composites from 2012 to present.
- **Electricity consumption** by province, reported by the National Energy Administration. Widely considered more reliable than GDP because electricity metering is automated and difficult to falsify.
- **Tax revenue** by province, reported by the Ministry of Finance. Like electricity, tax revenue is based on transactions that leave an audit trail.

**Construction.**
1. Download VIIRS annual composites for 2012–2022. Extract mean radiance within provincial boundaries using zonal statistics (analogous to the procedure in Lab 6, but at the provincial rather than country level).
2. Construct a panel: province × year observations with official GDP, night-light radiance, electricity consumption, and tax revenue.
3. Estimate the relationship between official GDP and each alternative indicator:

$$
\ln(\text{GDP}_{it}) = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \ln(\text{lights}_{it}) + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it}
$$

4. Compute "adjusted GDP" using the night-lights coefficient: $\widehat{\text{GDP}}_{it} = \exp(\hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_1 \ln(\text{lights}_{it}) + \hat{\gamma}_t)$.
5. Compare official and adjusted GDP growth trajectories. Provinces where official growth substantially exceeds adjusted growth are candidates for statistical inflation.

**Expected findings.** Following Chen et al. (2019), adjusted growth rates for northeastern provinces (Liaoning, Jilin, Heilongjiang) are expected to be significantly below official figures, reflecting the well-known statistical inflation in the "Rust Belt" provinces whose heavy industrial output declined but whose reported GDP did not. Coastal provinces — where economic activity is genuinely intense and where night-lights are bright — should show smaller discrepancies between official and adjusted figures.

**Student exercise.** Replicate the analysis for a subset of provinces (e.g., Guangdong, Liaoning, Gansu — representing coastal-successful, northeastern-declining, and interior-lagging archetypes) and interpret the discrepancies in light of the institutional incentives described in Section 7.1. If provincial officials are promoted based on GDP growth (the "tournament" model of Li and Zhou 2005), which provinces have the strongest incentive to inflate?

## Institutional Spotlight: Bumrungrad International Hospital — Designing a Services Export Cluster

Bumrungrad International Hospital in Bangkok illustrates how institutional coordination between private healthcare providers, government regulators, and international standard-setting bodies can create a spatially concentrated services export cluster with revenues exceeding $600 million per year from international patients alone.

**The coordination problem.** Medical tourism faces a trust problem that is more severe than most services trade: the customer is literally putting their life in the hands of a provider in a foreign country whose regulatory standards, malpractice liability regime, and clinical quality they cannot directly assess. The transaction costs of overcoming this information asymmetry — researching providers, verifying credentials, arranging logistics, managing post-operative follow-up across borders — would be prohibitive for most patients without institutional intermediation.

**The institutional solution.** Bumrungrad addressed the trust problem through a multi-layered institutional strategy. JCI accreditation provided the quality signal; it is the only globally recognized hospital accreditation standard, and Bumrungrad's early adoption (2002, reaccredited multiple times since) gave it first-mover advantage in signaling quality to international patients. Insurance partnerships addressed the financing barrier: by establishing direct billing relationships with over 200 international insurers, Bumrungrad eliminated the out-of-pocket payment that would deter all but the wealthiest medical tourists. The Thai government's Medical Tourism Visa reduced the immigration friction, and the hospital's dedicated international patient departments reduced the in-facility transaction costs.

**The agglomeration effect.** Bumrungrad's success attracted other hospitals to the medical tourism market — Bangkok Hospital, Samitivej, and the state-affiliated Mahidol University hospitals all expanded their international patient programs. The resulting cluster created external economies of scale: a larger pool of internationally trained medical specialists, a deeper market for medical tourism intermediaries (travel agencies, medical concierges, recovery hotels), and a reputation effect that made "medical tourism in Thailand" a recognized category rather than an idiosyncratic choice. This is the same cluster logic that drives APS agglomeration in global cities (Chapter 1), applied to healthcare services.

**The policy lesson.** Bumrungrad's success was not spontaneous. It required deliberate institutional design at multiple levels: hospital-level investment in accreditation and international patient infrastructure, government-level visa policy and regulatory frameworks, and industry-level cooperation on quality standards. The spatial concentration of medical tourism in Bangkok — rather than in Chiang Mai, Phuket, or other Thai cities with tourism infrastructure — reflects the same agglomeration forces that concentrate APS in capital cities: specialist medical capabilities require a density of complementary services that only a large urban market can sustain.

## Conclusion: Scale, Sovereignty, and Spatial Fragmentation

This chapter has examined two distinct but interconnected spatial dynamics: China's institutionally produced internal divergence and ASEAN's sovereignty-produced external fragmentation. The common thread is that institutions — Hukou restrictions, fiscal federalism, regulatory heterogeneity, data localization rules — create spatial barriers that prevent the equalization of factor prices and economic opportunities across space.

China's case demonstrates that rapid aggregate growth can coexist with persistent and widening spatial inequality when institutional barriers prevent the migration of citizenship rights even as they permit the migration of labor. The Hukou system is the institutional mechanism through which China's coastal mega-regions extract labor from interior provinces without paying the full social cost of that labor — a spatial subsidy that accelerates coastal agglomeration at the expense of interior development.

ASEAN's case demonstrates that platform economies can partially overcome regulatory fragmentation through network effects and institutional innovation — Grab and Gojek have created more functional economic integration across borders than the ASEAN Economic Community's formal agreements have achieved — but that the fragmentation imposes real costs visible in the city-by-city, country-by-country expansion strategies that would be unnecessary in a genuinely integrated single market.

The climate dimension adds urgency to both dynamics. The Mekong and Pearl River Deltas face risks that will force large-scale population relocation within decades, and the institutional capacity to manage that relocation — to protect displaced populations while maintaining economic productivity — varies enormously across the region. China's renewable energy industrial policy offers the possibility of spatial rebalancing, directing investment toward interior provinces that the export-oriented model bypassed, but the transmission infrastructure and inter-provincial coordination challenges are formidable.

Chapter 8 takes the analysis to South Asia, where India's IT services revolution presents a spatial dynamic distinct from both the East Asian manufacturing model and the Chinese developmental state: a services-led growth path that has created extreme spatial concentration in a small number of cities (Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune) while bypassing the rest of the subcontinent. The tools developed here — convergence analysis, institutional barrier measurement, platform economy dynamics — travel directly into the Indian context, where the spatial paradox of services trade (Chapter 1's foundational puzzle) is most vividly illustrated.
