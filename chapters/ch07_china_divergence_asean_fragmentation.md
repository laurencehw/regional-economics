# Chapter 7: China's Internal Divergence and ASEAN Fragmentation

## Introduction: Two Economies in One Country

In 2023, the per-capita GDP of Shanghai was approximately $27,000 — comparable to Portugal or the Czech Republic and well above the World Bank's high-income threshold. The per-capita GDP of Gansu province, 1,800 kilometers to the west, was approximately $5,800 — comparable to Guatemala or Uzbekistan. Both are administered by the same central government, operate under the same national legal system, use the same currency, and are connected by high-speed rail that covers the distance in under eight hours. The income ratio between the richest and poorest Chinese provinces is approximately 4.5:1 — wider than the gap between the richest and poorest US states (roughly 2:1) and comparable to the gap between the EU's highest- and lowest-income member states in purchasing power terms.

This internal divergence is not a residual of China's pre-reform backwardness. It is, in significant part, a product of the reform itself. The institutional architecture of China's opening — the sequencing of Special Economic Zones, the fiscal decentralization that rewarded coastal provinces with a larger share of revenue from foreign trade, the Hukou system that restricted rural-to-urban labor mobility while permitting massive informal migration without full citizenship rights — generated a spatial pattern in which capital, technology, and high-productivity employment concentrated in the coastal mega-regions (the Pearl River Delta, the Yangtze River Delta, and Jing-Jin-Ji) while interior provinces remained dependent on agriculture, resource extraction, and central government transfer payments.

Chapter 6 showed that East Asia's technology leaders engineered their upgrading through developmental state coordination — ITRI's tenant-selection discipline in Hsinchu, KDB's directed credit conditionality for Korean chaebol, Japan's ministry-guided specialty materials ecosystem. These were institutional achievements, not market outcomes. This chapter asks the harder question: what happens inside the developmental state when it operates at continental scale? China's government deploys the same tools — directed credit, technology acquisition targets, strategic investment in infrastructure — but it does so across a territory containing 1.4 billion people, 31 provincial-level jurisdictions with competing fiscal interests, and an income gradient wider than the gap between the EU's richest and poorest member states in purchasing power terms. The coordination problems are qualitatively different from those facing a city-state (Singapore), a mid-sized island economy (Taiwan), or even a 50-million-person chaebol-dominated system (Korea). And the external consequences are proportionately larger: China's industrial policy choices reshape the economic geography not only of its own interior but of the entire ASEAN periphery.

The chapter thus examines two connected spatial dynamics: China's institutionally produced internal divergence and ASEAN's sovereignty-produced external fragmentation. The common analytical thread is that institutions — Hukou restrictions, fiscal federalism, regulatory heterogeneity, data localization rules — create spatial barriers that prevent the equalization of factor prices and economic opportunities across space, even when the underlying economic forces (agglomeration economies, comparative advantage, labor mobility) push toward convergence.

The chapter proceeds in six sections. Section 7.1 analyzes China's coastal-inland divergence through the institutional lens of Hukou, fiscal federalism, and sequential liberalization. Section 7.2 examines medical tourism as a case study in Mode 2 services trade geography, using Thailand and Singapore to show how institutional design creates spatially concentrated services export clusters. Section 7.3 analyzes ASEAN platform economies — Grab, Gojek, and the regional digital ecosystem — as experiments in overcoming regulatory fragmentation through network effects. Section 7.4 examines the Belt and Road Initiative and the Digital Silk Road as spatial policy instruments that reshape ASEAN economic geography. Section 7.5 addresses climate vulnerability and managed retreat in the Mekong Delta and Pearl River Delta, and China's renewable energy industrial policy as spatial policy. Section 7.6 connects the analysis to Lab 2, interpreting the convergence evidence from the TiVA-based panel.

## 7.1 China's Great Divergence: Institutions, Not Geography

### Sequential Liberalization and the Coastal Premium

China's reform and opening, launched by Deng Xiaoping in 1978, was deliberately sequential. The first Special Economic Zones — Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shantou, and Xiamen — were all coastal, all in Guangdong and Fujian provinces, and all chosen for their proximity to Hong Kong, Macao, and Taiwan. The logic was institutional experimentation: create bounded zones where foreign investment, market pricing, and export-oriented manufacturing could operate under rules different from the planned economy, observe the results, and expand the model if successful.

The model was spectacularly successful. Shenzhen's population grew from roughly 30,000 in 1979 to over 17 million by 2023, and its GDP per capita surpassed Hong Kong's in 2018. The key institutional innovation was not tariff reduction or tax incentives alone — these were available in export-processing zones across the developing world. It was the combination of secure (if not fully transparent) property rights for foreign investors, a disciplined and increasingly skilled labor force drawn from interior provinces, infrastructure investment coordinated with industrial zone development, and — crucially — institutional insulation from the bureaucratic constraints that still governed the rest of the economy. The SEZ was not merely a geographic area with different tax rates; it was an institutional enclave with a different relationship to the state.

The sequential expansion of SEZ-like privileges — from the original four zones to fourteen Open Coastal Cities (1984), to Pudong (1990), to the Western Development Strategy (1999), to the inland Free Trade Zones of the 2010s — created a spatial gradient of institutional openness that persisted for decades. Regions that entered the liberalized system earlier accumulated advantages — foreign direct investment, technological spillovers from multinational firms, human capital development through learning-by-doing — that compounded over time. The path dependence logic of Chapter 2 is visible here at continental scale: early institutional advantage → capital and talent inflow → capability accumulation → further advantage.

### Fiscal Federalism and Provincial Competition

The 1994 Tax Sharing Reform reshaped the incentives facing provincial and municipal governments in ways that deepened the coastal-inland divide. Under the pre-1994 system, provincial governments negotiated revenue-sharing contracts with the center, creating heterogeneous fiscal arrangements across provinces. The 1994 reform centralized the most productive tax instruments (VAT, enterprise income tax) while leaving provinces dependent on land revenues and local fees for discretionary spending.

The consequence was a system that Montinola, Qian, and Weingast (1995) characterized as "market-preserving federalism": local governments competed for mobile capital by offering favorable conditions — infrastructure, regulatory flexibility, land at below-market prices — while the central government maintained macroeconomic control. The label captured the 1990s dynamic reasonably well, but subsequent developments — particularly the post-2003 explosion of land finance and local government debt — revealed that the system preserved markets for capital while distorting markets for land, housing, and credit in ways that the original framework did not anticipate (Hsing 2010). The competition was productive in coastal regions where the infrastructure, location, and human capital endowments made them attractive to foreign and domestic investors. In interior provinces, the same competitive incentives produced less investment because the underlying conditions were less favorable — the fiscal competition rewarded regions that were already advantaged, amplifying the spatial gradient.

The quantitative dimensions of fiscal decentralization clarify why this competitive system deepened the spatial gradient. After the 1994 reform, the central government captured approximately 55 percent of total tax revenue — primarily through the VAT and enterprise income tax — but bore only about 30 percent of total government expenditure. The remaining 70 percent of expenditure responsibilities fell on provincial and sub-provincial governments: education, healthcare, social security, local infrastructure, and environmental protection. This vertical fiscal imbalance — where the center collects most of the revenue but local governments bear most of the spending obligations — created a structural fiscal gap that local governments had to close through their own revenue generation. For coastal municipalities with booming economies and rising land values, this was manageable; for interior provinces with stagnant economies and limited tax bases, it was a chronic fiscal crisis partially offset by central transfer payments that carried their own political conditions and administrative inefficiencies.

Land finance deepened this dynamic into what became, in many coastal cities, a fiscal addiction. Coastal municipalities discovered that they could fund infrastructure investment by selling land use rights to developers, using the infrastructure investment to attract manufacturing, and using the resulting employment and tax revenue to justify further land sales. By the mid-2010s, land conveyance fees constituted 40 to 80 percent of discretionary municipal revenue in many coastal and tier-2 cities — a dependence that the IMF and Chinese economists alike identified as a systemic risk. The circular logic worked because coastal land values were rising rapidly — driven by the same agglomeration economies that Chapter 1 described — but it created perverse incentives. Municipal officials had stronger fiscal incentives to promote real estate development (which generated immediate land sale revenue) than to support manufacturing upgrading (which generated slower, smaller tax flows). The result was overinvestment in residential and commercial real estate, speculative price appreciation, and the eventual property market crisis that began unfolding with the Evergrande default in 2021 — a fiscal-spatial feedback loop with macroeconomic consequences. Interior municipalities with weaker demand for industrial and residential land could not generate the same fiscal spiral, leaving them dependent on central transfer payments that, while substantial, could not replicate the self-reinforcing growth dynamic of the coast.

### SOE Reform and the Spatial Division of State Capital

The coastal-inland divergence was sharpened dramatically by the SOE restructuring of the late 1990s. Under Zhu Rongji's "grasp the large, release the small" (抓大放小) strategy, the central government consolidated surviving state-owned enterprises into national champions in heavy industry, energy, finance, and telecommunications while allowing smaller SOEs to be privatized, merged, or simply closed. The spatial consequences were devastating for the industrial northeast. Liaoning, Jilin, and Heilongjiang — the "Rust Belt" provinces that had formed the core of Mao-era heavy industry — lost tens of millions of SOE workers between 1995 and 2005. Nationally, SOE employment fell from approximately 113 million in 1995 to 64 million by 2005, with the northeast bearing a disproportionate share of the layoffs because of its concentration in precisely the sectors — steel, heavy machinery, mining, textiles — targeted for rationalization.

The surviving national champions relocated their headquarters and strategic functions to Beijing and Shanghai, where proximity to central government ministries, capital markets, and international business services provided advantages that no northeastern city could match. The northeast's share of national industrial output fell from roughly 15 percent in 1990 to approximately 6 percent by 2020 — a collapse comparable in relative terms to the deindustrialization of the American Midwest, but compressed into a shorter time frame and occurring without the social safety net that cushioned (imperfectly) the adjustment in Western economies. The xiagang (下岗) — "laid-off" workers who received nominal severance and re-employment assistance but often found no comparable employment — became the human face of a spatial reallocation of state capital from the interior to the coast.

The connection to fiscal federalism is direct: interior provinces bore the fiscal burden of social spending for displaced SOE workers — unemployment benefits, pension obligations, retraining programs — while coastal provinces captured the dynamic private sector that emerged in the space vacated by downsized SOEs. Guangdong's private sector grew from roughly 20 percent of provincial GDP in 1995 to over 55 percent by 2010; Heilongjiang's private sector remained below 40 percent, constrained by the absence of the entrepreneurial ecosystem, supply chain networks, and venture capital that private firms require. The SOE reform was thus a massive, state-directed spatial reallocation of productive capital that deepened the coastal premium the SEZ system had created.

### The Hukou System: Institutional Barriers to Spatial Equilibrium

The Hukou (household registration) system is the single most important institutional barrier to spatial equilibrium within China. Established in 1958, Hukou ties access to public services — education, healthcare, pensions, housing subsidies — to an individual's place of registration, which is typically inherited from parents and linked to their rural or urban place of origin. A worker from rural Henan who migrates to Shenzhen can legally work in Shenzhen's factories, but their children cannot attend Shenzhen's public schools on the same terms as locally registered residents, they cannot access Shenzhen's public healthcare or pension systems, and they cannot purchase subsidized housing.

The result is a two-tier urban population: registered residents with full citizenship rights and migrants with limited access to urban public goods. As of 2023, approximately 300 million Chinese citizens lived and worked in cities where they did not hold Hukou registration — a population larger than every country in the world except China, India, and the United States. These migrants provide the labor that drives coastal manufacturing and urban services, but they are statistically counted at their place of registration rather than their actual place of residence and work, and they are excluded from the social welfare systems of the cities where they produce value.

The spatial economic implications are profound. In a neoclassical world without institutional barriers, the coastal-inland wage gap would generate migration flows that eventually equalize factor prices. The Hukou system permits the migration of labor but prevents the migration of citizenship, creating a persistent wedge between the spatial distribution of production and the spatial distribution of welfare. Coastal mega-regions receive the productive contribution of migrant workers without bearing the full fiscal cost of their social reproduction (education, healthcare, retirement). Interior provinces bear those costs — schools for left-behind children, healthcare for returned elderly migrants — without receiving the tax revenue that the migrants' labor generates. This is not a market failure; it is an institutional design that subsidizes coastal agglomeration at the expense of interior provinces.

The empirical magnitude of this institutional distortion is substantial. Estimates of the Hukou wage penalty — the gap between what migrant workers earn and what observationally equivalent local residents earn in the same city — range from approximately 15 to 40 percent depending on methodology and city tier (Au and Henderson 2006, Combes et al. 2020). The penalty is not simply a return to unmeasured skill differences: it reflects the institutional exclusion from urban labor markets where Hukou registration serves as a screening device, particularly in public sector employment, licensed professions, and formal-sector jobs that offer social insurance. Migrant workers are systematically sorted into informal, temporary, and low-benefit employment — precisely the jobs that do not build the human capital accumulation that would close the income gap over time.

The 2014 New Urbanization Plan acknowledged the Hukou system's distortions and introduced a tiered reform approach: abolish Hukou restrictions entirely in small cities (under 500,000 population), relax requirements in medium cities (500,000–3 million), and maintain strict controls in mega-cities (over 5 million). The logic was to redirect migration away from the overcrowded tier-1 cities toward smaller cities that had infrastructure capacity to absorb newcomers. The results have been mixed at best. Small cities relaxed Hukou restrictions, but migrants did not come — because the economic opportunities that drove migration in the first place are concentrated in precisely the tier-1 and tier-2 cities where restrictions remain tight. The reform revealed a fundamental tension: the places that have labor demand are the places that resist Hukou liberalization, and the places that welcome new residents are the places that lack the jobs to attract them.

The comparative institutional parallel with Vietnam is instructive. Vietnam's hộ khẩu (household registration) system was modeled on China's Hukou and served a similar function of controlling rural-to-urban migration. But Vietnam liberalized its system more aggressively: a 2006 reform allowed registration transfers after three years of residence in a new location, and subsequent reforms further reduced barriers. The result has been more rapid urbanization (Vietnam's urban share rose from 24 percent in 2000 to 39 percent in 2023) and stronger convergence in regional incomes, though at the cost of congestion and strain on urban infrastructure in Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi. The contrast suggests that Hukou reform is not merely an equity measure — it is a spatial efficiency intervention whose absence imposes measurable costs on aggregate productivity by preventing labor from reaching its highest-value use.

## 7.2 Medical Tourism: Mode 2 Services Trade and Institutional Design

### Scale, Scope, and Spatial Concentration

Before examining the institutional architecture of medical tourism, it is worth establishing the quantitative scale and spatial concentration of the phenomenon. Thailand received approximately 3.5 million medical tourists in 2019 (pre-COVID), generating an estimated $5 billion in direct healthcare revenue and substantially more in ancillary spending on accommodation, travel, and recovery tourism. Singapore received roughly 500,000 medical tourists at significantly higher per-patient value, reflecting its positioning in complex, high-cost procedures. Malaysia attracted approximately 1.2 million, concentrated in dental and cardiac care. Together, these three ASEAN economies constituted one of the world's largest medical tourism clusters, competing with (and in some segments outperforming) established destinations like India, Turkey, and Mexico.

The spatial concentration within each country is extreme. Bangkok alone accounts for roughly 80 percent of Thailand's medical tourism revenue — a degree of primacy that exceeds even the capital-city dominance typical of developing-country service industries. Singapore, as a city-state, is spatially concentrated by definition. In Malaysia, the Kuala Lumpur corridor (including Petaling Jaya and the Putrajaya medical hub) captures the bulk of international patients. This concentration is not accidental; it reflects the agglomeration logic that Chapter 1 established for APS clusters, applied to healthcare services. Specialist medical capabilities — interventional cardiology, oncology, orthopedic surgery, organ transplantation — require a density of complementary resources (diagnostic labs, surgical supply chains, rehabilitation facilities, specialist nursing pools) that only large urban markets can sustain.

The COVID-19 pandemic revealed a structural vulnerability in this spatial model. Mode 2 services trade — consumption abroad, where the consumer crosses the border — is inherently exposed to border closures in a way that Mode 1 (cross-border supply, including telemedicine) and Mode 3 (commercial presence through FDI) are not. Medical tourism revenues in Thailand collapsed by over 80 percent in 2020–2021 as border restrictions eliminated international patient flows. The recovery was slow, not reaching pre-pandemic levels until 2024 in most estimates. The episode demonstrated that spatial concentration in Mode 2 services creates a portfolio risk: the same agglomeration economies that make Bangkok's medical cluster productive also make it fragile to border shocks. The post-pandemic response has included investment in telemedicine platforms (Mode 1 substitution) and outward FDI by Thai hospital groups into Middle Eastern and South Asian markets (Mode 3 diversification) — institutional adaptations to a spatial vulnerability that the gravity model framework of Chapter 3-B would predict.

### Bumrungrad and the Thai Model

Thailand's emergence as the world's leading medical tourism destination is a case study in how deliberate institutional design creates spatially concentrated services export clusters. Bumrungrad International Hospital in Bangkok — which treats over 500,000 international patients per year from more than 190 countries — did not arise from comparative advantage in medical training or from lower healthcare costs alone. It arose from a coordinated institutional strategy involving the hospital's management, the Thai government, international accreditation bodies, and the insurance industry.

The institutional architecture has four components. First, **international accreditation**: Bumrungrad was among the first hospitals outside the United States to receive Joint Commission International (JCI) accreditation, providing a quality signal legible to Western patients and their insurance companies. JCI accreditation requires compliance with over 1,200 standards covering patient safety, infection control, clinical governance, and data management — an institutional transplant that raised Thai hospital practice to internationally recognized levels. Second, **visa facilitation**: the Thai government created a Medical Tourism Visa (MT visa) allowing stays of up to 90 days with simplified entry procedures for patients and accompanying family members. Third, **insurance partnerships**: Bumrungrad established billing relationships with over 200 international insurance companies, allowing patients to use their home-country health insurance for treatment in Bangkok — eliminating the out-of-pocket financing barrier that constrains medical tourism in less institutionally developed destinations. Fourth, **dedicated international patient infrastructure**: separate international patient departments with multilingual staff, embassy liaison services, and airport transfer coordination — institutional features that reduce the transaction costs of seeking healthcare abroad.

The spatial economics of this institutional package are revealing. Medical tourism concentrates overwhelmingly in Bangkok, not in Thailand's other cities. The agglomeration logic is the same as for APS clusters (Chapter 1, Chapter 4): specialist medical capabilities require a density of complementary services — diagnostic laboratories, surgical equipment suppliers, pharmaceutical distribution, rehabilitation facilities — that only a large urban market can sustain. Once established, the cluster attracts further specialist investment (Bumrungrad added a dedicated cancer center, a spine institute, and a dental hospital), which attracts more international patients, which justifies further specialist investment. The circular causation is identical to the NEG model's forward and backward linkages, applied to healthcare services rather than manufacturing.

### Singapore's Complementary Model

Singapore positions itself as the premium alternative to Thailand, targeting patients from the Middle East and Southeast Asia who seek the highest-end specialist care. The institutional strategy differs from Thailand's in emphasizing regulatory environment over cost competitiveness: Singapore's Biomedical Sciences Initiative, launched in 2000, invested $3 billion in medical research infrastructure, recruited leading international clinician-scientists, and positioned Singapore as a regional hub for clinical trials and experimental treatments. The Medical Registration Act and the Private Hospitals and Medical Clinics Act provide a regulatory framework that international patients and insurers perceive as comparable to the US or UK — a perception that supports premium pricing.

The differentiation between Singapore and Thailand is not simply a matter of price but of institutional trust and procedural specialization. Singapore targets complex procedures — organ transplantation, advanced oncology, neurosurgery — where regulatory confidence matters more than cost savings, and where the clinical trial infrastructure (anchored by Duke-NUS Medical School, a deliberate institutional transplant from the Duke University system) provides access to experimental treatments unavailable in the patient's home country. Thailand targets high-volume, protocol-standardized procedures — cosmetic surgery, orthopedic replacement, dental work, wellness and preventive medicine — where cost savings of 50–80 percent relative to US or European prices are the primary driver. This spatial sorting of medical specialization across the two ASEAN economies mirrors the APS tier differentiation from Chapter 1: Singapore occupies the "command and control" tier of medical tourism (complex cases requiring the highest regulatory and clinical infrastructure), while Bangkok occupies the "volume processing" tier (standardized procedures at competitive prices with high throughput). Both positions are institutionally constructed and self-reinforcing through the same agglomeration dynamics that sustain the APS hierarchy in financial services.

The Mode 2 services trade implications connect directly to Chapter 3-B's gravity model framework. Medical tourism flows exhibit strong distance elasticities — patients from the Middle East travel to Bangkok and Singapore, patients from North Africa travel to Turkey, patients from Central America travel to the United States — because the transaction costs of medical travel (language barriers, visa requirements, insurance compatibility, cultural familiarity with healthcare systems) increase with distance in ways that parallel the informational and institutional barriers that drive services trade distance elasticities generally. The gravity model predicts that reducing these transaction costs — through accreditation harmonization, insurance portability agreements, and visa facilitation — should increase bilateral medical tourism flows, and the Thai and Singaporean cases confirm this prediction.

The medical tourism evidence sharpens a point that Chapter 3-B introduced theoretically: for services trade, institutional distance dominates geographic distance as the binding constraint. A patient from Oman will travel 7,000 kilometers to Bangkok — but not 200 kilometers to a hospital in a neighboring country without JCI accreditation, insurance compatibility, or visa facilitation. The "distance" that the gravity model measures in medical tourism flows is overwhelmingly institutional: regulatory compatibility (accreditation recognition), financial infrastructure (insurance portability and direct billing), and administrative facilitation (visa regimes). Geographic distance enters mainly through its correlation with cultural and linguistic familiarity, which reduces the psychic costs of seeking healthcare in an unfamiliar environment. This institutional primacy is consistent with the broader argument of this book: that spatial economic patterns are shaped more by the institutional architecture of market access than by the physical geography of location.

## 7.3 ASEAN Platform Economies: Network Effects Against Regulatory Fragmentation

### Grab and Gojek: Building Integration from Below

The ASEAN Economic Community, established in 2015, aspires to create a single market and production base across ten member states with a combined population of 680 million and GDP of approximately $3.6 trillion. The aspiration is far from realized. Non-tariff barriers remain pervasive: divergent product standards, incompatible professional licensing regimes, restrictive rules of origin, and limited mutual recognition of qualifications constrain the free movement of goods, services, capital, and skilled labor that the AEC blueprint envisions.

Against this backdrop, two platform companies — Grab (headquartered in Singapore, founded by Malaysian entrepreneurs) and Gojek (headquartered in Jakarta) — have achieved a degree of regional economic integration that formal trade agreements have not. Grab operates in eight ASEAN countries, providing ride-hailing, food delivery, package delivery, digital payments, insurance, and lending services through a single platform. Gojek (now part of GoTo Group after merging with Tokopedia) operates primarily in Indonesia, Vietnam, and Singapore with a similarly broad service portfolio.

The platform firms' expansion strategies reveal the binding constraints on ASEAN integration with a precision that trade statistics cannot match. Each new market entry required separate negotiations with national regulators over licensing requirements (ride-hailing regulations differ in every ASEAN country), payment system regulations (e-money licensing, know-your-customer requirements, foreign ownership restrictions on financial institutions), data localization rules (Indonesia and Vietnam require certain categories of data to be stored domestically), and labor regulations (the classification of platform workers as employees versus independent contractors varies across jurisdictions).

The spatial economics of platform expansion follow a city-by-city, country-by-country pattern that would not exist in a genuinely integrated single market. Grab entered Malaysia first (2012), then the Philippines (2013), Singapore (2013), Thailand (2013), Vietnam (2014), Indonesia (2014), Myanmar (2017), and Cambodia (2017). Each entry required a dedicated regulatory team, local banking partnerships, and adaptation to local payment preferences (cash-heavy markets like Indonesia and Vietnam required different payment architectures than card-heavy Singapore). The expansion sequence was determined not by market size alone but by regulatory accessibility — a revealed preference index of ASEAN's institutional fragmentation.

### Digital Payments and Financial Inclusion

The most consequential spatial effect of ASEAN platform economies is financial inclusion. GrabPay, GoPay, and their competitors have brought digital payment capabilities to populations that formal banking systems had not reached. In Indonesia, where only 49 percent of adults held a bank account in 2017 (World Bank Global Findex), GoPay reached over 40 million users by 2023 — many of them first-time participants in the formal financial system. The platform acts as an institutional bridge: providing the trust infrastructure (escrow, dispute resolution, transaction records) that formal banking requires, without requiring the physical branch network, minimum balance requirements, and documentation that exclude low-income and informal-sector workers.

The spatial implication is a partial equalization of financial access across the urban hierarchy. In a pre-platform world, financial services were concentrated in capital cities and major commercial centers where bank branch density was sufficient to serve retail customers. GoPay and GrabPay operate wherever the platform's ride-hailing and delivery services operate — which, because those services follow population density rather than bank regulation, extends to secondary and tertiary cities that formal banking underserves. This is a technology-mediated reduction in the effective economic distance between peripheral cities and the financial center — analogous to M-Pesa's effect in East Africa (Chapter 13), but achieved through a private platform rather than a telecommunications operator.

The contrast between Indonesia's fragmented e-money landscape and Singapore's unified system illustrates how regulatory architecture shapes platform payment geography. Indonesia hosts multiple competing e-wallets — GoPay, OVO, Dana, ShopeePay — each with its own merchant network, user base, and interoperability limitations. Bank Indonesia's QRIS (Quick Response Code Indonesian Standard), launched in 2020, imposed a common QR code standard that allows any e-wallet to pay at any QRIS-registered merchant, but interoperability between wallets for peer-to-peer transfers and cross-platform balance management remains incomplete. Singapore's PayNow, by contrast, provides a unified real-time payment infrastructure linked to national identity numbers and phone numbers, enabling instant transfers between any bank account or e-wallet. The result is that financial inclusion in Indonesia is platform-dependent (your access to digital payments depends on which platform you use, which depends on where you live and what services are available), while in Singapore it is infrastructure-based (anyone with a bank account or phone number can participate regardless of platform choice).

At the regional level, cross-border payment linkages are beginning to create a new institutional geography of financial integration. The Singapore-Thailand QR payment interoperability agreement (launched 2021) allows Singaporean consumers to pay Thai merchants using PayNow, and Thai consumers to pay Singaporean merchants using PromptPay — a bilateral integration achieved through central bank coordination rather than trade negotiations. Similar linkages are being developed between Singapore and Malaysia, Singapore and India, and Thailand and Malaysia. These bilateral payment corridors are building an ASEAN payment network incrementally, without the comprehensive multilateral agreement that the AEC blueprint envisions but cannot deliver. The comparison to M-Pesa in East Africa (Chapter 13) is instructive: M-Pesa succeeded in a regulatory environment that was permissive by default (Kenya's central bank allowed Safaricom to operate as a quasi-bank before mobile money regulations existed), while ASEAN platform payments navigate a regulatory environment that is restrictive by default and requires active coordination between national regulators to enable cross-border flows.

### The Super App Model and Regulatory Arbitrage

Grab and Gojek did not remain ride-hailing companies. They evolved into "super apps" — integrated platforms offering ride-hailing, food delivery, package logistics, digital payments, micro-lending, insurance, investment products, and healthcare booking through a single application. Grab's financial services arm (GrabFin) and Gojek's (GoPay, later GoFinancials) each serve tens of millions of users across multiple countries. The super app model is not simply a diversification strategy; it is a direct response to, and exploitation of, ASEAN's regulatory fragmentation.

The logic is institutional arbitrage. In a regulatory environment where each new market and each new service category requires separate licensing, compliance infrastructure, and government relationships, the fixed costs of market entry are high. Once a firm has navigated the licensing requirements for ride-hailing in a given country — securing transport authority approval, establishing driver background check processes, negotiating insurance frameworks, building relationships with local regulators — the marginal cost of adding food delivery, package logistics, or payment services on the same platform is relatively low because much of the regulatory infrastructure (entity registration, compliance teams, government relations, data management systems) can be shared. The regulatory complexity that prevents new single-service entrants from competing becomes a moat for incumbents who have already incurred the fixed costs. ASEAN's fragmentation, which impedes integration for most firms, becomes a competitive advantage for the few platforms large enough to absorb the entry costs across multiple jurisdictions.

This dynamic explains why ASEAN has produced regional super apps while the United States and Europe — with their more unified regulatory environments — have not. In a single market, a ride-hailing company faces low barriers to entry from specialized competitors in adjacent services: a food delivery startup, a payment fintech, an insurance app. In ASEAN, each of those specialized competitors would need to navigate the same country-by-country regulatory gauntlet that the super app has already completed. The regulatory fragmentation functions as an economy of scope for multi-service platforms — the opposite of its effect on single-service firms, for which it functions as a barrier to entry.

### Platform Labor and Regulatory Divergence

The gig economy debate plays out across ASEAN with a spatial heterogeneity that reveals the depth of regulatory fragmentation within the region. Indonesia imposed ride-hailing price floors in 2019, setting minimum fares that constrained the predatory pricing strategies platforms had used to gain market share — a regulation that protected driver incomes but reduced consumer surplus and slowed platform expansion in price-sensitive secondary cities. Singapore is developing a comprehensive platform worker protection framework, including mandatory insurance contributions and retirement savings for gig workers — an approach consistent with Singapore's broader social compact model but one that raises labor costs relative to less regulated neighboring markets. Vietnam classifies platform drivers as self-employed contractors, imposing minimal obligations on platforms but leaving workers without social protection.

These regulatory divergences mean that the same platform firm operates under fundamentally different labor regimes across its markets. A Grab driver in Singapore receives employer insurance contributions; a Grab driver in Jakarta operates under regulated minimum fares; a Grab driver in Ho Chi Minh City is an independent contractor with neither floor price protection nor employer-provided benefits. The spatial variation in labor costs and worker welfare within a single corporate network creates both arbitrage opportunities (platforms can shift investment toward less regulated markets) and political risks (regulatory tightening in one market can cascade through the network as platforms adjust pricing and availability). For the regional economist, the platform labor question illustrates a broader principle: in the absence of harmonized labor standards, regulatory competition between jurisdictions can produce a race to the bottom in worker protections — or, if the Singaporean model proves that regulated platforms are more sustainable, a demonstration effect that gradually raises standards across the region. The structural parallel with Section 7.1's Hukou analysis is worth noting: both the Hukou system and ASEAN's platform labor regimes involve a spatial extraction of labor value without full social reproduction costs. Chinese coastal firms benefit from migrant workers whose education, healthcare, and pensions are borne by interior provinces; ASEAN platform firms benefit from gig workers whose social protection is borne by no one. The institutional mechanism differs — administrative restriction in China, regulatory omission in ASEAN — but the spatial economic logic is the same: the places where value is produced externalize the costs of the labor that produces it.

## 7.4 The Belt and Road Initiative as Spatial Policy

### Infrastructure Corridors and Economic Geography

China's Belt and Road Initiative, launched in 2013, is the largest infrastructure investment program in history — encompassing, by various estimates, $500 billion to $1 trillion in committed or completed projects across more than 140 countries. In Southeast Asia, BRI projects include the China-Laos Railway (completed December 2021, connecting Kunming to Vientiane), the East Coast Rail Link in Malaysia (under construction, connecting the South China Sea coast to the Strait of Malacca), the Jakarta-Bandung High-Speed Railway (completed October 2023), and multiple port, highway, and special economic zone developments across the region.

The spatial economics of BRI infrastructure are analytically distinct from conventional infrastructure investment in two ways. First, the projects are planned and financed primarily to serve China's trade connectivity objectives rather than the recipient country's internal development priorities. The China-Laos Railway illustrates this logic concretely. The $5.9 billion project — equivalent to roughly one-third of Laos's GDP at initiation — connects Kunming, a provincial capital in southwestern China seeking better access to Southeast Asian markets, to Vientiane, the Laotian capital. The railway does not connect Laos's major population centers to each other; it connects China to the Mekong region through Laos. The spatial effect is to strengthen north-south connectivity (from China through mainland Southeast Asia) at the expense of east-west connectivity (between ASEAN members) — reinforcing a hub-spoke pattern centered on China rather than a multipolar ASEAN network.

The financing structure reveals the asymmetry. Laos holds a 30 percent stake in the joint venture, funded primarily by a $1.5 billion loan from the Export-Import Bank of China at concessional rates — meaning Laos borrowed from China to pay China for construction that serves China's connectivity objectives. Operational since December 2021, early performance data tells a mixed story. Passenger traffic has exceeded initial projections, with over 11 million passengers in the first full year of operation — driven substantially by Chinese tourists traveling to Vientiane and Luang Prabang. Freight traffic, however, has remained below expectations, partly because the customs facilitation and logistics infrastructure needed to move goods efficiently across the border has lagged behind the railway construction itself.

The spatial effects are already becoming visible along the railway corridor. Land prices in Vang Vieng — a midpoint stop previously known as a backpacker destination — have risen sharply, and the town is being repositioned as a Chinese-tourist and logistics hub. New hotel construction, Chinese-language signage, and a planned special economic zone around the station reflect a reorientation of Vang Vieng's economic geography from its organic backpacker economy toward a Chinese-capital-driven development model. The question is whether this constitutes genuine spatial economic development — diversifying Laos's economic base and creating sustainable employment — or asset inflation driven by speculative capital following the railway, similar to the real estate dynamics that have periodically distorted Chinese municipal economies (Section 7.1).

Second, BRI projects frequently include institutional provisions — special economic zones, customs facilitation agreements, Chinese-built digital infrastructure — that extend Chinese institutional models into recipient countries. The Sihanoukville Special Economic Zone in Cambodia, built and operated by Chinese firms, employs largely Chinese workers, operates under management practices imported from Shenzhen's export-processing zones, and generates limited backward linkages to the Cambodian economy. This is the enclave dynamic that Chapter 14 documented for African regional hegemons, replicated in Southeast Asia with Chinese characteristics: infrastructure connectivity without institutional integration, investment without capability transfer.

### The Debt Sustainability Debate

The Hambantota Port narrative — in which Sri Lanka "ceded" port control to China Merchants Port Holdings after failing to service Chinese loans — has been widely cited as evidence of "debt trap diplomacy," a deliberate Chinese strategy of lending to countries that cannot repay in order to seize strategic assets. The evidence, however, is more nuanced than the headline version suggests. Hambantota's 99-year lease was driven primarily by Sri Lanka's broader fiscal crisis (rooted in domestically financed infrastructure spending and tax cuts, not only Chinese loans), and the port's economic rationale was questionable from the outset — a decision driven more by the political ambitions of the Rajapaksa government than by Chinese strategic design (Brautigam 2020).

Nevertheless, the BRI financing model does create genuine debt sustainability risks for small economies, even if the mechanism is not a deliberate "trap." Chinese state bank loans are typically extended at commercial or near-commercial rates (LIBOR plus 2–3 percent is common), with shorter maturities than traditional development finance from the World Bank or Asian Development Bank. The projects they finance — railways, ports, power plants — are long-lived infrastructure assets that may take decades to generate sufficient returns to service the debt. The maturity mismatch between short-term debt and long-lived assets creates liquidity risk even when the project is fundamentally viable.

Laos's situation crystallizes the problem. Public debt exceeded 65 percent of GDP by 2023, with roughly half owed to Chinese entities — including not only the railway loan but accumulated borrowing for hydroelectric dams, highway construction, and SEZ development. Debt service costs consume a growing share of government revenue, constraining spending on education, healthcare, and the institutional development that the flying-geese framework (Chapter 6) identifies as essential for capability accumulation. The DSSI (Debt Service Suspension Initiative) during COVID-19, and the subsequent bilateral restructuring negotiations between Laos and its Chinese creditors, revealed a governance gap: there is no multilateral framework for restructuring BRI loans comparable to the Paris Club process for traditional sovereign debt. Each restructuring is bilateral, opaque, and conducted under asymmetric bargaining power. For the spatial economist, the debt sustainability question is inseparable from the spatial development question: if infrastructure projects fail to generate the economic activity needed to service their financing, the fiscal burden falls on precisely the public services — education, health, institutional capacity — that determine whether infrastructure connectivity translates into genuine development.

### The Digital Silk Road

The less visible but potentially more consequential dimension of BRI is the Digital Silk Road — Chinese investment in telecommunications infrastructure (Huawei 5G networks, submarine cable systems, fiber-optic backbone), cloud computing (Alibaba Cloud, Huawei Cloud, Tencent Cloud data centers), and digital platforms (TikTok, Shopee, Lazada) across Southeast Asia.

The spatial economics of digital infrastructure differ from physical infrastructure in a crucial way: digital networks exhibit strong winner-take-all dynamics because of network effects, data accumulation advantages, and switching costs. Once a country's telecommunications backbone is built on Huawei equipment, the switching cost to an alternative vendor is enormous — not because the physical equipment cannot be replaced, but because the software ecosystem, maintenance contracts, and trained workforce are all vendor-specific. Similarly, once consumers adopt a platform ecosystem (TikTok for entertainment, Shopee for commerce, GrabPay for payments), the data those platforms accumulate makes them increasingly difficult to displace.

The smart city dimension adds a layer of spatial governance to the Digital Silk Road. Huawei's "Safe City" platform — an integrated suite of surveillance cameras, facial recognition software, command-and-control centers, and data analytics — has been deployed in over 100 cities across more than 40 countries, including several ASEAN markets (Malaysia, Laos, Cambodia). These systems generate granular real-time data on population movement, commercial activity, traffic flows, and social behavior. For urban planners, this data enables evidence-based decisions about transit routing, infrastructure investment, and emergency response. For governance, it creates surveillance capabilities that blur the line between urban management and political control. The spatial economics implications are double-edged: cities with Huawei Safe City infrastructure may benefit from more efficient urban management, but they also become nodes in a data network whose ultimate architecture is determined by a foreign technology provider — a dependency that parallels the hardware vendor lock-in of telecommunications networks but operates at the level of urban governance rather than communications infrastructure.

The geopolitical dimension intersects with the spatial economics through data localization and digital sovereignty. Indonesia's Government Regulation No. 71 (2019) requires electronic system operators to store certain data categories on servers located within Indonesia. Vietnam's Cybersecurity Law (2018) imposes similar requirements. These data localization rules create a new geography of digital infrastructure — a geography that is partly determined by technical efficiency (data center location should minimize latency and energy costs) and partly by regulatory sovereignty (governments want data within their jurisdiction for surveillance, taxation, and industrial policy purposes). The result is a fragmented digital landscape where the physical geography of servers and submarine cables reflects regulatory choices as much as engineering optimization.

## 7.5 Climate, Managed Retreat, and Renewable Energy Geography

### The Mekong Delta and Pearl River Delta: Sea-Level Rise at Scale

The two river deltas that anchor Southeast and East Asian economic geography — the Pearl River Delta (PRD) and the Mekong Delta — face existential climate risks that will reshape regional economic geography within the timescale of infrastructure investments made today.

The **Mekong Delta** produces roughly half of Vietnam's rice and over 60 percent of its fisheries output. It is home to approximately 17 million people. Sea-level rise projections — even conservative IPCC AR6 estimates of 0.3 to 0.6 meters by 2100 under SSP2-4.5 — would inundate substantial areas of the delta, and saltwater intrusion is already affecting rice cultivation in coastal provinces. The World Bank estimates that a one-meter sea-level rise (consistent with higher-emission scenarios) would displace 10–12 million people from the Mekong Delta alone. Where these people go — to Ho Chi Minh City, to Hanoi, to other ASEAN countries — is a spatial economics question with enormous implications for urban planning, housing markets, and labor markets across the region.

The **Pearl River Delta** — the manufacturing heartland that includes Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Dongguan, Foshan, and Hong Kong — faces a different but equally consequential climate risk: intensifying typhoons, flooding, and heat stress that threaten both physical infrastructure and worker productivity. The PRD generates roughly 10 percent of China's GDP. Climate adaptation for the PRD is not an environmental policy question; it is an industrial policy question: protecting the infrastructure, supply chains, and labor force that sustain one of the world's most productive manufacturing clusters.

The institutional challenge of managed retreat — the planned relocation of population and economic activity from areas that will become uninhabitable or unproductive due to climate change — is particularly acute in authoritarian systems where top-down relocation can proceed without the democratic deliberation that slows adaptation in liberal democracies, but also without the feedback mechanisms that ensure relocated populations' interests are represented. China's history of large-scale population relocation provides precedent but not reassurance. The Three Gorges Dam displaced over 1.3 million people from the Yangtze River valley between the mid-1990s and 2006. Displaced residents were promised compensation for lost assets, replacement housing, and livelihood restoration through agricultural resettlement or urban employment programs. Follow-up studies, however, paint a more sobering picture. Wilmsen (2016) found that resettled communities experienced persistent income gaps relative to comparable non-displaced populations, particularly among agricultural households who lost irrigated farmland in the river valley and received non-irrigated hillside plots that could not sustain equivalent yields. The compensation framework — based on the assessed value of physical assets (land, buildings, crops) — systematically underestimated welfare losses from disrupted social networks, lost place-specific agricultural knowledge, and destroyed informal economic relationships (credit networks, labor-sharing arrangements, market connections) that had no assessed value but substantial productive importance. Urban resettlers fared somewhat better, but faced discrimination in labor markets where their rural origins and limited urban social networks placed them at a disadvantage — a dynamic not unlike the Hukou penalty described in Section 7.1.

The Three Gorges lesson for the managed retreat that climate change will require in the Mekong and Pearl River Deltas is that compensation frameworks must account for relational and institutional capital, not merely physical assets. A fishing household in the Mekong Delta that is relocated to an inland settlement loses not just its boat and waterfront property but its knowledge of local waterways, its established relationships with buyers and processors, its informal insurance networks with neighboring households, and its children's access to schools and social networks adapted to their specific context. These losses are real, substantial, and systematically invisible to asset-based compensation schemes.

### Heat Stress and Manufacturing Productivity

The Pearl River Delta faces not only flood and typhoon risk but a less dramatic, more pervasive climate channel: rising heat stress that directly affects worker productivity in manufacturing and construction. Somanathan et al. (2021) estimate that each additional day above 35°C reduces Indian manufacturing output by 2–3 percent, with the effect concentrated in labor-intensive sectors where workers are directly exposed to ambient temperatures. The PRD, with its dense concentration of light manufacturing (electronics assembly, textiles, plastics) and construction, faces similar vulnerabilities — particularly in the workshops and smaller factories that lack the climate-controlled environments of the largest Foxconn-scale operations.

This is a spatial economics channel with potentially transformative implications. If heat stress progressively reduces the productivity advantage of southern coastal manufacturing, it could accelerate the relocation of production to cooler inland or northern locations — a climate-driven reversal of the coastal premium that has defined China's economic geography for four decades. The connection to Baumol's cost disease from Chapter 6 is instructive: if labor productivity in heat-exposed manufacturing falls while service-sector productivity (in air-conditioned offices and data centers) does not, the Baumol dynamic accelerates — the relative cost of manufacturing in heat-exposed locations rises, pushing economic activity further toward climate-controlled services and further toward locations where ambient temperatures do not impose a productivity penalty. China's own internal geography offers a natural experiment: comparing manufacturing productivity trends in the subtropical PRD with those in the temperate Yangtze Delta and the cool-summer Bohai Rim could reveal whether heat stress is already a measurable spatial reallocation force.

### China's Renewable Energy Industrial Policy as Spatial Policy

China's dominance in renewable energy manufacturing — producing over 80 percent of the world's solar panels and over 60 percent of its wind turbines — has a spatial dimension that connects to the coastal-inland divergence story. Solar panel manufacturing has concentrated in provinces with access to polysilicon supply chains (Xinjiang, Inner Mongolia), low-cost electricity (Yunnan, Sichuan with hydropower), and export logistics (Jiangsu, Zhejiang with port access). This is one of the few manufacturing sectors where interior provinces have a genuine comparative advantage: the energy intensity of polysilicon refining means that electricity costs matter more than proximity to seaports.

The spatial rebalancing potential of renewable energy deployment is even more significant than the manufacturing geography. China's solar and wind resources are concentrated in the north and west — Inner Mongolia, Gansu, Xinjiang, Qinghai — precisely the provinces that the coastal-oriented development model has disadvantaged. The State Council's "dual carbon" targets (peak emissions by 2030, carbon neutrality by 2060) require massive renewable energy deployment in these interior provinces, creating infrastructure investment, construction employment, and operations jobs in regions that have limited participation in China's export-oriented growth model.

The constraint is transmission infrastructure. Delivering renewable electricity from Inner Mongolia to Shanghai requires ultra-high-voltage (UHV) transmission lines spanning thousands of kilometers — a technology that China has developed more aggressively than any other country. The flagship project is the Changji-Guquan ±1100kV line, connecting Xinjiang's wind and solar resources to Anhui province in eastern China — at 1,100 kilovolts, the highest-voltage transmission line in the world, capable of transmitting 12 gigawatts over 3,300 kilometers with losses under 5 percent. Additional UHV corridors connect Inner Mongolia, Gansu, and Qinghai to the coastal load centers. These lines represent infrastructure investment at a scale that only a centrally coordinated system can mobilize — each corridor costs $3–5 billion and requires right-of-way coordination across multiple provincial jurisdictions.

But even with UHV technology, curtailment rates remain a persistent problem: an estimated 5–10 percent of renewable generation is wasted because of transmission bottlenecks, grid inflexibility, and insufficient energy storage capacity. The institutional barrier is familiar from this chapter's earlier discussion of fiscal federalism. Provinces that generate renewable electricity want to maximize revenue from power sales; provinces that consume electricity want the lowest possible prices; and the grid companies (State Grid Corporation and China Southern Power Grid) that operate the transmission network between them extract rents from both sides while managing the technical challenges of integrating intermittent renewable generation into a system designed for dispatchable coal and hydro plants. The central government must coordinate inter-provincial cost sharing, generation dispatch priority, and transmission pricing — a governance challenge that maps directly onto the fiscal federalism dynamics described in Section 7.1, applied to energy rather than tax revenue.

China's renewable energy dominance also has an ASEAN dimension that connects the energy story to the BRI analysis of Section 7.4. Chinese firms — led by State Power Investment Corporation, China Southern Power Grid, and a constellation of provincial energy companies — are the largest investors in ASEAN power generation, particularly in hydroelectric dams on the Mekong (Laos, Myanmar, Cambodia) and in solar and wind projects across Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines. The long-discussed ASEAN Power Grid — an interconnected regional electricity network that would allow cross-border power trade — has been advanced primarily through bilateral interconnections that Chinese engineering firms have designed and built. The pattern mirrors BRI infrastructure more broadly: China exports not just equipment but the institutional framework of power sector governance (generation-transmission-distribution models, tariff structures, grid management protocols), creating a regional energy architecture in which Chinese firms and Chinese technical standards occupy structurally central positions. Whether this constitutes a beneficial transfer of energy infrastructure or a new dimension of the dependency dynamics discussed in Section 7.4 depends on whether ASEAN states develop the institutional capacity to regulate, maintain, and eventually localize the technology — the same capability accumulation question that animates the flying-geese framework throughout this book.

## 7.6 Lab 2 and the East Asian Convergence Evidence

Lab 2 provides the empirical framework for testing whether the spatial patterns described in this chapter and Chapter 6 produce convergence or divergence in East Asian domestic value-added trajectories. Using OECD TiVA data for ten Asian economies (Japan, Korea, China, Taiwan, Thailand, Malaysia, Vietnam, Indonesia, the Philippines, and Singapore) over 2000–2022, Lab 2 estimates $\beta$-convergence in domestic value-added shares for electronics exports.

The econometric specification follows the standard convergence regression:

$$
\Delta \ln(\text{DVA}_{it}) = \alpha + \beta \ln(\text{DVA}_{i,t-1}) + \varepsilon_{it}
$$

where $\beta < 0$ indicates convergence (economies with lower initial DVA shares grow faster) and $\beta > 0$ indicates divergence. The convergence half-life — the time required for half the initial gap to close — is computed as $t_{1/2} = \ln(2) / |\beta|$.

The results are substantively revealing. The full panel shows weak convergence ($\beta \approx -1.15$, $p \approx 0.10$), consistent with the flying-geese hypothesis at the broadest level: laggard economies are catching up in domestic value-added share, though the relationship is noisy. But the convergence is driven entirely by the ASEAN-6 subgroup ($\beta \approx -3.6$, $p < 0.02$), where Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia have been rapidly increasing their domestic content in electronics exports — precisely the upgrading pattern that the flying-geese model predicts when institutional conditions permit capability accumulation.

The Northeast Asian core (China, Japan, Korea) shows **no convergence** ($\beta \approx 0.2$, $p > 0.9$). This non-result is itself informative: the three economies with the highest domestic value-added shares are not converging toward each other. Japan's share is stable at a high level (reflecting its position as an upstream supplier of materials and equipment), Korea's share is rising through semiconductor upgrading, and China's share is rising through import substitution and domestic supplier development. They are on parallel tracks rather than converging toward a common level.

The ex-China specification ($\beta \approx -3.1$, $p < 0.001$) shows very strong convergence among the nine non-Chinese economies, suggesting that China's unique trajectory (massive scale, state-directed upgrading, geopolitical disruption of supply chains) is an outlier that weakens the convergence pattern when included.

The institutional interpretation of the Northeast Asian non-convergence is revealing. Japan's stable high DVA share reflects its position as an upstream supplier of specialty materials and capital equipment — photoresists, silicon wafers, EUV pellicles, precision machine tools — a position sustained by decades of accumulated tacit knowledge and supplier relationships rather than by labor cost dynamics. Korea's rising DVA share reflects active state-directed upgrading through the institutional mechanisms Chapter 6 described: KDB-channeled investment, chaebol-scale R&D commitments, and government-coordinated technology acquisition. China's rising DVA share reflects a different mechanism entirely: import substitution at massive scale, driven by "Made in China 2025" targets for domestic content and accelerated by US export controls that forced Chinese firms to develop domestic alternatives to American semiconductor equipment and design software. These are three distinct institutional mechanisms producing three distinct trajectories — convergence would require that the three economies face similar competitive pressures and operate under similar institutional constraints, which the weaponized interdependence regime documented in Chapter 6 has ensured they do not.

The ASEAN convergence, by contrast, reflects the market-mediated upgrading that the flying-geese model predicts when institutional conditions permit capability accumulation. Multinational firms relocating production stages to lower-cost ASEAN economies transfer process technology (though not necessarily design capability), train local workforces, and create the supplier networks from which domestic firms eventually emerge. Thailand's automotive parts industry, Vietnam's electronics assembly clusters, and Indonesia's nickel processing chain all exhibit this pattern: foreign firms establish operations, domestic suppliers develop around them, and the domestic value-added share rises as local content increases. But the convergence may be bounded. ASEAN economies appear to be converging toward a "middle tier" of DVA shares — roughly 40–55 percent of export value — rather than toward the Northeast Asian frontier of 60–70 percent. This ceiling reflects the institutional constraints documented throughout this chapter: regulatory fragmentation that prevents regional-scale economies of scope, incomplete labor mobility that limits the matching efficiency of regional labor markets, and R&D investment rates (typically 0.5–1.5 percent of GDP in ASEAN-6) that are one-third to one-quarter the levels sustained by the Northeast Asian developmental states (3–5 percent of GDP in Korea, Japan, and Taiwan). Convergence within a tier is not the same as convergence toward the frontier.

How will these patterns evolve? Three scenarios merit consideration. First, continued US-China decoupling could accelerate "friend-shoring" — the relocation of supply chains from China to politically aligned alternatives, with ASEAN economies (particularly Vietnam, Indonesia, and Thailand) as primary beneficiaries. This would likely steepen the ASEAN convergence trajectory by bringing higher-value production stages into the region, but it would also increase ASEAN's dependence on geopolitically motivated investment flows that could reverse if political conditions change. Second, RCEP-driven integration could reduce intra-ASEAN trade costs sufficiently to enable the regional-scale supply chain networks that currently exist only within China, potentially raising the DVA ceiling by enabling cross-border specialization. Third, climate disruption could reshape the comparative advantage of coastal versus inland production — a channel explored in Section 7.5 — potentially redirecting investment flows in ways that neither the convergence regression nor the trade policy scenarios can predict. The interaction of these forces will determine whether East Asian economic geography in 2040 looks like a more integrated version of the current pattern or something fundamentally different.

The interaction with this chapter's institutional analysis is direct. The Hukou system, fiscal federalism, and sequential liberalization described in Section 7.1 contribute to China's outlier status: China's domestic value-added growth is driven not by market-mediated convergence dynamics but by state-directed industrial policy at a scale that no other economy in the sample can replicate.

## Data in Depth: Cross-Validating Chinese Provincial GDP with Night-Lights

**The question.** How much of the variation in Chinese provincial GDP is genuine economic activity, and how much is statistical manipulation? The well-documented "GDP inflation" problem — provincial GDP totals routinely exceeding the national total by 5–10 percent — raises fundamental questions about the reliability of China's sub-national economic statistics.

**Data sources.**
- **China National Bureau of Statistics (NBS)** provincial GDP panels, available from provincial statistical yearbooks and the CEIC database.
- **VIIRS (Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite)** nighttime lights, available from NOAA/NCEI at approximately 750-meter resolution. Annual composites from 2012 to present.
- **Electricity consumption** by province, reported by the National Energy Administration. Widely considered more reliable than GDP because electricity metering is automated and difficult to falsify.
- **Tax revenue** by province, reported by the Ministry of Finance. Like electricity, tax revenue is based on transactions that leave an audit trail.

**Construction.**
1. Download VIIRS annual composites for 2012–2022. Extract mean radiance within provincial boundaries using zonal statistics (analogous to the procedure in Lab 6, but at the provincial rather than country level).
2. Construct a panel: province × year observations with official GDP, night-light radiance, electricity consumption, and tax revenue.
3. Estimate the relationship between official GDP and each alternative indicator:

$$
\ln(\text{GDP}_{it}) = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \ln(\text{lights}_{it}) + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it}
$$

4. Compute "adjusted GDP" using the night-lights coefficient: $\widehat{\text{GDP}}_{it} = \exp(\hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_1 \ln(\text{lights}_{it}) + \hat{\gamma}_t)$.
5. Compare official and adjusted GDP growth trajectories. Provinces where official growth substantially exceeds adjusted growth are candidates for statistical inflation.

**Expected findings.** Following Chen et al. (2019), adjusted growth rates for northeastern provinces (Liaoning, Jilin, Heilongjiang) are expected to be significantly below official figures, reflecting the well-known statistical inflation in the "Rust Belt" provinces whose heavy industrial output declined but whose reported GDP did not. Coastal provinces — where economic activity is genuinely intense and where night-lights are bright — should show smaller discrepancies between official and adjusted figures.

**Student exercise.** Replicate the analysis for a subset of provinces (e.g., Guangdong, Liaoning, Gansu — representing coastal-successful, northeastern-declining, and interior-lagging archetypes) and interpret the discrepancies in light of the institutional incentives described in Section 7.1. If provincial officials are promoted based on GDP growth (the "tournament" model of Li and Zhou 2005), which provinces have the strongest incentive to inflate?

## Spatial Data Challenge: The Hukou Distortion

China's official population statistics assign individuals to their Hukou registration location, not their actual place of residence and work. This creates a systematic mismeasurement of approximately 300 million internal migrants who live and work in coastal cities but are statistically counted in their rural home provinces. The distortion cascades into per-capita GDP calculations (overstating inland GDP per capita, understating coastal GDP per capita), urbanization rates (China's de facto urbanization rate exceeds its de jure rate by roughly 15 percentage points), and fiscal burden estimates (interior provinces appear to have more residents per unit of public expenditure than they actually serve).

Researchers must use census micro-data (the decennial census and the inter-census 1% population survey), mobile phone location data (Baidu Migration Index, Tencent Mobility), or satellite-derived proxies (night-lights as a cross-check on population and economic activity claims) to approximate actual population distributions. Chapter 6's Spatial Data Challenge on Chinese provincial GDP manipulation compounds the problem: not only is the population denominator wrong, but the GDP numerator is also potentially unreliable. Any panel regression using Chinese sub-national data should treat both sides of the per-capita calculation as measured with substantial and likely non-random error.

## Spatial Data Challenge: ASEAN Regulatory Fragmentation and Data Comparability

Cross-country economic analysis in ASEAN confronts a data landscape that is substantially more heterogeneous than East Asia or Europe. National statistical offices in ASEAN-10 vary enormously in capacity, methodology, and reporting frequency. Singapore publishes quarterly GDP by sector with a two-month lag; Myanmar's GDP statistics are annual and of uncertain reliability. Manufacturing census data, which is essential for calculating domestic value-added at the firm level, is available for Thailand (annually), Vietnam (intermittently), Indonesia (irregularly), and not at all for Laos, Cambodia, or Myanmar.

The WIOD resolves this heterogeneity for the economies it covers (Indonesia, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan are included; Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines are added in some extensions), but at the cost of imposing a common sector classification that may not reflect the actual structure of production in less diversified economies. The TiVA database provides broader coverage but at a coarser sector level. Researchers working with ASEAN panel data should report results with and without the less reliably measured economies and should treat cross-country comparisons with appropriate caution.

## Institutional Spotlight: Inside Bumrungrad — The Operational Architecture of a Services Export Cluster

Section 7.2 established the economic logic of medical tourism as Mode 2 services trade. This spotlight examines the operational details of how Bumrungrad International Hospital actually functions as a services export platform — the institutional machinery that converts Thailand's general comparative advantage in healthcare into a spatially concentrated, internationally competitive cluster.

**The patient pipeline.** Bumrungrad's international patient operation is structured less like a hospital and more like an integrated logistics firm. A patient inquiry from Abu Dhabi triggers a coordinated sequence: the International Medical Coordination department assigns a case manager who speaks the patient's language (Bumrungrad employs translators in over 30 languages); the case manager coordinates with the relevant clinical department to produce a treatment plan and cost estimate within 48 hours; simultaneously, the insurance team verifies coverage with the patient's insurer (from a network of over 200 international insurance partnerships) and the visa facilitation team initiates MT visa documentation with the Thai consulate. By the time the patient boards the flight, their hospital room, surgical schedule, post-operative rehabilitation, and hotel accommodation for accompanying family members are confirmed. The transaction costs that would otherwise deter international patients are absorbed by the hospital's institutional infrastructure.

**The internal labor market.** Bumrungrad's ability to sustain this operation depends on a specialized internal labor market. The hospital employs over 1,200 physicians, many of whom completed residencies or fellowships in the United States, United Kingdom, or Australia and returned to Thailand where their international training commands a premium in the medical tourism market but where their cost to the hospital is a fraction of what a comparably trained physician would earn in the countries where they trained. This wage arbitrage — internationally trained physicians earning $80,000–150,000 in Bangkok versus $300,000–500,000 for equivalent positions in the US — is the micro-level mechanism through which Thailand's cost advantage in medical tourism operates. It is sustained by the amenity value of living in Bangkok, family ties, and the professional prestige of practicing in an internationally recognized institution.

**The revenue geography.** Bumrungrad's international patient revenue — exceeding $600 million annually (direct healthcare revenue; the broader market including ancillary spending on accommodation, travel, and wellness is substantially larger) — comes disproportionately from the Middle East (~35 percent of international patients), South and Southeast Asia (~30 percent), and East Asia (~15 percent), with the remainder from Europe, Africa, and the Americas. This geographic distribution reveals the gravity model in action: the "distance" that matters is institutional (insurance compatibility, visa ease, accreditation recognition) more than geographic, but cultural proximity and diaspora networks still shape the patient flow — Middle Eastern patients, for instance, benefit from Bangkok's established Arabic-language services infrastructure, halal food availability, and decades-long tourism ties.

**The replication problem.** Thailand's Board of Investment has tried to extend the medical tourism model to other cities — Chiang Mai, Phuket, Pattaya — with limited success. The constraint is not physical infrastructure (these cities have modern hospitals) but the density of complementary services: the specialist diagnostic labs, the multilingual case management teams, the insurance billing infrastructure, the post-operative rehabilitation networks, and the critical mass of specialist physicians that make Bumrungrad's operational model possible. Building a hospital is easy; building the institutional ecosystem around it is hard. This is the same replication problem that Chapter 6 identified for semiconductor clusters: the CHIPS Act can fund a fab, but it cannot easily replicate the institutional ecosystem of Hsinchu.

## Conclusion: Scale, Sovereignty, and Spatial Fragmentation

This chapter has examined two distinct but interconnected spatial dynamics: China's institutionally produced internal divergence and ASEAN's sovereignty-produced external fragmentation. The common thread is that institutions — Hukou restrictions, fiscal federalism, regulatory heterogeneity, data localization rules — create spatial barriers that prevent the equalization of factor prices and economic opportunities across space.

China's case demonstrates that rapid aggregate growth can coexist with persistent and widening spatial inequality when institutional barriers prevent the migration of citizenship rights even as they permit the migration of labor. The Hukou system is the institutional mechanism through which China's coastal mega-regions extract labor from interior provinces without paying the full social cost of that labor — a spatial subsidy that accelerates coastal agglomeration at the expense of interior development.

ASEAN's case demonstrates that platform economies can partially overcome regulatory fragmentation through network effects and institutional innovation — Grab and Gojek have created more functional economic integration across borders than the ASEAN Economic Community's formal agreements have achieved — but that the fragmentation imposes real costs visible in the city-by-city, country-by-country expansion strategies that would be unnecessary in a genuinely integrated single market.

The climate dimension adds urgency to both dynamics. The Mekong and Pearl River Deltas face risks that will force large-scale population relocation within decades, and the institutional capacity to manage that relocation — to protect displaced populations while maintaining economic productivity — varies enormously across the region. China's renewable energy industrial policy offers the possibility of spatial rebalancing, directing investment toward interior provinces that the export-oriented model bypassed, but the transmission infrastructure and inter-provincial coordination challenges are formidable.

Chapter 8 takes the analysis to South Asia, where India's IT services revolution presents a spatial dynamic distinct from both the East Asian manufacturing model and the Chinese developmental state: a services-led growth path that has created extreme spatial concentration in a small number of cities (Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune) while bypassing the rest of the subcontinent. The tools developed here — convergence analysis, institutional barrier measurement, platform economy dynamics — travel directly into the Indian context, where the spatial paradox of services trade (Chapter 1's foundational puzzle) is most vividly illustrated.
